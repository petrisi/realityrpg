Disability & Chronic Conditions

Physical disability, chronic illness, and pain management — the invisible and visible realities that shape daily existence.

⚠️ Table Responsibility

This section deals with real disabilities, chronic illness, and pain. Many players may have personal experience with these conditions. Never assign a disability to a character without player consent. Never pressure a player to roleplay a condition they identify with personally. Use content warnings before scenes involving medical emergencies, pain episodes, or discrimination. The GM should ask each player what topics they want out of the game before play begins.

In Reality RPG, disability is not a debuff — it is a life context. Over 25% of U.S. adults have a disability. Chronic conditions affect 1 in 6 Americans. These are not edge cases; they are mainstream human experience. This page provides mechanics for navigating the physical realities of disability and chronic illness with the same depth and respect afforded to every other life domain.

📐 Rule: Disability Is Not Optional

Disability can emerge from birth, accident, illness, aging, or injury during play. A character may start with a disability or acquire one through a Health Event (see §4.3). The GM rolls a hidden 2d10 each time a character suffers a severe injury or major illness — on 18+, the injury results in a lasting disability.

1. The Disability Framework

Accessibility is a core game concept in Reality RPG, not an afterthought. The world is built for able-bodied people by default — and that structural reality has mechanical consequences for every character who navigates it differently.

1.1 The Accessibility Rating

Every location in the game has an Accessibility Rating (AR) from 1 to 10, set by the GM. This represents how well a space accommodates people with diverse physical needs:

ARLevelDescriptionExample
1–2😡 HostileNo accommodations; actively exclusionary; stairs only, no signage, narrow passagesOld basement bar, historic building without elevator
3–4😠 BarelyMinimal accommodations; some ADA compliance but poorly maintainedOlder office building, small town clinic
5–6😐 AdequateStandard ADA compliance; ramps, wide doors, accessible restrooms presentMost modern public buildings, chain restaurants
7–8😊 GoodWell-designed accessibility; sensory accommodations, flexible policiesNew university campus, modern hospital
9–10🌟 UniversalUniversal design; anticipates diverse needs; proactive accommodationsState-of-the-art facility, disability-first organization

1.2 Disability Modifiers

Each disability has a Disability Modifier (DM) that applies to relevant checks when the environment's Accessibility Rating is insufficient for the character's needs. The DM is added to the Target Number (making the check harder):

📐 Rule: Accessibility Check

When a character with a disability attempts an action in an environment with an Accessibility Rating below their Accessibility Need (AN), they suffer a penalty: DM = AN − AR. This DM is added to the TN of any check that requires navigating or functioning in that space. If AR ≥ AN, no penalty applies.

1.3 Energy Budget

Many disabilities require more energy for the same tasks. Characters with a disability have an Energy Budget of 10 + VIG points per day. Each activity costs energy, and exhausting the budget imposes −1 to all attributes for the remainder of the day and adds +2 SP.

ActivityNormal CostWith Disability CostNotes
Basic morning routine1 EP2 EPHygiene, getting dressed, breakfast
Commute1 EP2–4 EPDepends on transportation needs and AR of route
Work/school (per 4 hrs)2 EP3–5 EPHigher if accommodations are lacking
Social interaction1 EP1–3 EPHigher with sensory or communication barriers
Medical management (daily)—1–3 EPMedication, monitoring, device charging
Physical activity2 EP3–6 EPVaries widely by disability type

1.4 Character Creation Options

During character creation, a player may choose to start with a disability. Doing so grants 2 additional Background Points (for use toward education, skills, or socioeconomic advantages) — reflecting the reality that disabled people develop extraordinary resilience and adaptive strategies. A player may also choose to not have a disability; this is a valid and common choice.

💡 GM Tip: The Social Model of Disability

Use the social model: disability is not just what's "wrong" with a body — it's the mismatch between a body and an inaccessible world. A wheelchair user isn't "limited" by their chair; they're limited by stairs. Frame obstacles as environmental failures, not personal deficits. This produces richer, more accurate roleplay.

2. Mobility Impairments

Mobility impairments affect a character's ability to move through physical space. These include paralysis, amputation, severe joint degeneration, muscular dystrophy, and other conditions affecting locomotion.

Characters with limb amputations may use prosthetic devices. Prosthetics have a Function Rating that modifies relevant DXT checks:

📐 Rule: Prosthetic Failure

Once per month, the GM may call for a DXT check (TN 40) for the prosthetic. On failure, the device malfunctions: a socket becomes loose, a battery dies, or a joint locks. The character must make a DXT check (TN 50) to fix it in the field or find appropriate tools/assistance. Prosthetic repair costs $50–$500 per incident.

2.3 Mobility Aids

Characters using canes, crutches, walkers, or braces have an AN of 5 and move at half speed (taking twice as long for any timed movement). They suffer a +3 TN penalty on DXT checks involving running, climbing, or rapid movement.

2.4 Accommodation Rights

Characters with mobility impairments have legal rights under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). If a business or institution refuses reasonable accommodation:

  • The character can file a complaint with the Department of Justice — INT + Law check (TN 50) to successfully navigate the process
  • Successful complaint: the entity must make changes within 30–90 days and may face fines of $55,000–$110,000 (first and second offenses)
  • The character gains +3 SP from the stress of fighting for their rights, even if successful
📝 Example: Maya Navigates an Old Building

Maya uses a power wheelchair. She needs to attend a meeting at a historic downtown office (AR 3). Her AN is 6, so DM = 6 − 3 = 3. The building has only one narrow staircase entrance and no elevator. The GM calls for a DXT check (TN 50 + 3 = 53) to find an accessible route. Maya rolls 47 — she fails. She has to call ahead and request the building manager open a side entrance with a ramp, costing 30 minutes of her time and +2 SP from the frustration.

4. Chronic Physical Conditions

Chronic conditions are long-term health issues that require ongoing management. They generate daily energy costs, periodic complications, and stress. Each condition has a Daily Cost (energy points), a Complication Threshold (when things go wrong), and a Management Modifier (how well treatment controls it).

📐 Rule: Condition Management

At the start of each week, the GM calls for a RES check (TN = 40 − Management Modifier). On success, the condition is well-controlled that week. On failure, the character experiences a complication event (detailed per condition). Characters who are untreated have no Management Modifier and suffer complications every week.

4.1 Diabetes (Type 1)

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition requiring daily insulin management. It is not caused by lifestyle — it is a lifelong medical condition.

Daily Insulin Management Mechanic

Each day, the character must complete their insulin routine. This costs 2 EP from their Energy Budget and involves:

  1. Blood glucose monitoring — DXT check (TN 30) to accurately check blood sugar. Failure means an incorrect reading (±20 mg/dL).
  2. Insulin dosing — Based on the reading, the character calculates and administers insulin. A correct reading + INT check (TN 35) = correct dose. Failure = wrong dose.
  3. Meal planning — Carbohydrate counting requires a INT check (TN 40) to match insulin to food intake.

Insulin dosingINT35Under-dosing (+5 SP, hyperglycemia) or over-dosing (hypoglycemia risk)
Carb countingINT40Blood sugar swings after meal
Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) useNone—Automates steps 1–2; reduces daily EP cost by 1

4.6 Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS)

ME/CFS causes debilitating fatigue that is not relieved by rest and worsens after physical or mental exertion (Post-Exertional Malaise, or PEM).

📐 Rule: Pacing and PEM

Characters with ME/CFS must pace their energy. Each day, the GM tracks their Exertion Level. If they exceed their Energy Budget, they suffer PEM: −2 to all attributes for 1d4 days, +5 SP, and their Energy Budget is reduced by 2 for the next week. Pacing is a skill — characters with high EXP at managing their condition gain a +5 bonus to their Energy Budget.

Daily cost: 2–5 EP (varies by severity). Base Energy Budget: reduced to 5 + VIG (vs. normal 10 + VIG). Management Modifier: +5 with strict pacing; 0 without.

5. Chronic Pain System

Chronic pain is distinct from acute pain. It persists beyond normal healing time (3+ months) and becomes its own condition — reshaping the nervous system, mood, sleep, and identity. Approximately 20% of U.S. adults live with chronic pain.

📝 Example: David's Chronic Pain Day

David has chronic back pain from a workplace injury. He takes prescription NSAIDs (+5 Treatment Modifier) and does physical therapy (+5). His RES is 11 (+1 modifier).

Morning roll: 2d10 = 12 + 1 (RES) + 10 (Treatment) = 23. Pain level: 0–1. Good morning!

Midday, he lifts a heavy box at work (physical exertion). GM calls for RES check (TN 40). David rolls 38 — failure. Flare-up: +3 pain. His pain jumps to 3–4.

Evening, after a stressful argument at home (SP rises to 18). RES check (TN 45). He rolls 42 — failure. Another +2. Pain is now 5–6 (Moderate). He can barely sit through dinner. He gains +2 SP from the pain itself.

He takes a warm bath and does gentle stretching (self-care). Pain drops by 1. He ends the day at 4–5, exhausted but functional.

This creates a Benefit Cliff: working more hours to earn more money may result in losing healthcare and income support worth far more. The GM should track the character's income carefully.

7. Invisible vs. Visible Disabilities

Not all disabilities are apparent. This distinction has profound social and mechanical implications.

7.1 Visible Disabilities

Visible disabilities (wheelchair use, prosthetics, obvious physical differences) are immediately apparent. This creates both advantages and challenges:

✅ Advantages of Visibility

  • Accommodations are more likely to be offered proactively
  • Priority seating, accessible parking, elevator access
  • People understand why the character needs extra time or assistance
  • Clearer path to formal disability recognition/benefits

❌ Challenges of Visibility

  • Pity, staring, unwanted comments or questions
  • Assumptions about intelligence, capability, or independence
  • Physical barriers are more immediately apparent (and frustrating)
  • Higher equipment costs (wheelchair, prosthetics, vehicle mods)

7.2 Invisible Disabilities

Invisible disabilities (chronic pain, Crohn's, MS, ME/CFS, diabetes, mental health conditions) are not apparent to observers. This creates unique social dynamics:

✅ Advantages of Invisibility

  • Can "pass" as able-bodied in good days
  • Lower equipment costs
  • Less immediate public scrutiny
  • Can choose when and how to disclose

❌ Challenges of Invisibility

  • Accommodations must be requested (and often justified)
  • "But you look fine" — constant invalidation
  • Higher risk of being accused of malingering or faking
  • Harder to access benefits (must prove disability)

📝 Example: Invisible Disability at Work

Lena has ME/CFS (invisible). On "good days" she appears completely fine. Her coworker notices she left at 3 PM and says, "Must be nice to have an easy job!" Lena makes a PRC check (TN 40) to explain her situation. She rolls 48 — success. She shares that she has a chronic illness that limits her energy. Her coworker apologizes and becomes more understanding. −1 SP from being heard.

Two weeks later, another coworker says, "I don't see anything wrong with you. You should just try harder." Lena makes a RES check (TN 45) to handle the invalidation. She rolls 39 — failure. +3 SP. She feels defeated and questions whether she should disclose her condition at all.

💡 GM Tip: Disability as Identity, Not Tragedy

Disability is not a tragedy to be overcome. Many disabled people have rich, fulfilling lives and communities. Frame disability as one aspect of a person's identity — like culture, education, or personality. Show the humor, creativity, and resilience that comes from navigating an inaccessible world. The disability community has its own culture, humor, and solidarity.
