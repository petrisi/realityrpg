Law, Government & Civic Life

The invisible architecture of everyday existence. Voting, courts, taxes, emergencies — systems that shape every character's life whether they pay attention or not.

In Reality RPG, the state is not a background detail — it is an active force. Characters pay taxes, interact with police, navigate bureaucracy, vote (or don't), apply for benefits, and respond to emergencies. This page provides the mechanics for every major civic institution and crisis system a player might encounter.

⚠️ Table Note

Law and government topics touch on real trauma — police violence, immigration detention, deportation, systemic racism, and disaster loss. Use lines and veils. Offer players opt-outs for scenarios involving police encounters, immigration enforcement, or government coercion. The goal is engagement, not re-traumatization.

🗳️ 1. Voting & Civic Participation

1.1 Voter Registration

Characters must be registered to vote in their state. Registration is a one-time Life Event that costs 1–3 hours of time (online or in-person at the DMV). A character with INT ≥ 4 can do it online; lower INT characters must visit in person (+1 SP from DMV stress — see Section 4).

📋 Voter Registration Check

At character creation, roll 1d10: on a 1–6, the character is registered. On a 7–10, they are not registered and must take the registration event before their first election. Characters under 18, undocumented immigrants, and certain felons (depending on state) cannot register.

1.2 Election Events

National elections are major campaign events that affect the entire game world. The GM announces an election every 2 or 4 years (matching real cycles). During an election year:

  • Stress increase: All characters gain +1 SP during the final month before the election (reflecting real political anxiety).

  • Character involvement: Characters may campaign, donate, volunteer, or protest. Each action costs time and SP but can provide Morale events if the character's preferred outcome wins.

1.4 Jury Duty

Jury duty is a random life event. Each year, roll 1d100 for each adult character: on a 1–5, they are summoned for jury duty.

📝 Jury Duty Mechanics

Duration: Roll 1d5 + 2 = 3–7 days of lost work time.
Compensation: $50–$75/day (set by state; roll 1d3 × $25 + $25).
Lost wages: Character loses their normal daily income for those days. Net financial impact = (daily wage − jury pay) × days.
Stress: +2 SP for the experience of hearing testimony, deliberating.
INT check: Characters with INT ≥ 5 may be selected as jury foreperson (+1 Morale event, leadership experience).

2.2 DUI / DWI Consequences

A DUI is a major life event with cascading effects:

  • Immediate: Arrest, possible jail time (1d6 hours to 48 hours), license suspension (6 months–2 years)
  • Financial: Fines ($500–$2,500 first offense; $2,000–$10,000+ for repeat), mandatory alcohol education ($100–$500), ignition interlock device ($70–$150/month for 6–12 months), legal fees ($1,500–$5,000)
  • Employment: Roll 1d10: on 1–4, employer finds out; on 1, character is fired
  • Criminal record: Felony DUI (3rd offense or injury involved) makes future employment, housing, and loan applications harder (−2 to all PRC-based social checks for 1 year)
  • Stress: +5 SP at time of arrest; +2 SP/month for 3 months during legal proceedings

💰 3. Taxes

3.1 Annual Tax Filing

Tax season (January–April) is a structured annual event for every working character:

📊 Tax Filing Event

Time: 5–20 hours depending on complexity (roll 1d4 × 5 hours).
Stress: +1 SP (tax season stress).
Filing check: 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 40. On success, the return is filed correctly. On failure, the character makes an error — either a small math mistake (refund reduced by $50–$300) or a missed deduction (owe $100–$800 more than necessary). Characters with Finance skill ≥ +1 get +5 on this check. Characters who hire a tax preparer ($100–$400) auto-succeed.
Deadline: April 15. Missing the deadline adds +2 SP and a $100–$500 late fee.

3.1a W-4 Withholding and Payroll Taxes

Before a character earns their first paycheck, they must complete Form W-4 — the IRS form that tells their employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each pay period. This is one of the most consequential financial decisions a character makes, because it determines whether they get a refund, owe money, or hit the sweet spot of owing exactly zero in April.

The W-4 Form

When a character starts a new job, the employer provides a W-4. The form asks for:

  • Filing status: Single, Married Filing Jointly, or Head of Household
  • Number of dependents: Each dependent reduces withholding
  • Other income or deductions: Characters with side income, mortgage interest, or charitable giving can adjust withholding to match their expected annual tax situation
  • Additional withholding: Characters who know they'll owe extra can specify a flat dollar amount to withhold each paycheck

The old W-4 (pre-2020) used a concept called "allowances" — claiming more allowances meant less tax withheld. The current W-4 uses a more direct system of dollar amounts, but the principle is the same: the more deductions/dependents you claim, the less tax comes out of each paycheck.

📋 W-4 Setup Check

When a character fills out their W-4, the GM calls for a W-4 Setup Check:

Roll: 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 40 (medium difficulty).
Finance skill bonus: Characters with Finance +1 or higher get +5 to this roll. Finance +3 or higher get +10.
Hiring a tax advisor for W-4 help: Costs $50–$150 one-time; auto-succeeds.

On success: The character's withholding is correctly calibrated. Throughout the year, their paycheck withholding closely matches what they'll actually owe. No surprise in April.

On failure: The character's withholding is mis-calibrated. Roll 1d4 to determine the direction:

  • 1–2 (too little withheld): The character claims too many dependents or fills in incorrect deduction amounts. Each paycheck is larger than it should be, but in April they will owe money — see below.
  • 3–4 (too much withheld): The character is overly cautious or leaves fields blank, resulting in extra withholding. Each paycheck is smaller than it should be, but in April they'll get a large refund — see below.

How Withholding Works

Think of withholding as the government taking its share throughout the year rather than demanding one lump sum in April. Each paycheck, the employer calculates how much federal income tax to send to the IRS on the character's behalf, based on the W-4 information and the current tax bracket tables.

💡 The Paycheck Flow

Every paycheck goes through this pipeline: Gross Pay → minus Federal Income Tax (withholding) → minus FICA (Social Security + Medicare) → minus State Income Tax (if applicable) → minus Employer-sponsored benefits (health insurance, 401k, etc.) → Net Pay (what actually hits the bank account).

FICA Taxes — Payroll Taxes

Separate from federal income tax withholding, every paycheck also has FICA taxes deducted. These are not income taxes — they fund specific government programs and are calculated at a flat rate regardless of income bracket:

TaxEmployee RateEmployer MatchWages Fund
Social Security (OASDI)6.2%6.2%Retirement, disability, and survivor benefits
Medicare (HI)1.45%1.45%Healthcare coverage for age 65+ and certain disabled people
Total FICA7.65%7.65%15.3% combined

Key details:

  • The Social Security tax (6.2%) only applies to the first $168,600 of annual wages (2024 limit). If a character earns more than this, the excess is not taxed for Social Security.
  • The Medicare tax (1.45%) has no cap — it applies to every dollar earned. Additionally, characters earning over $200,000/year (single) or $250,000/year (married) pay an Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% on the excess.
  • FICA taxes are mandatory — the W-4 does not affect them. Even if a character claims "exempt" from federal income tax withholding (rare; only for people who genuinely owe zero), FICA still comes out of every check.
  • The employer matches the employee's FICA contribution dollar-for-dollar, but the employee never sees this match — it goes directly to the government.
⚠️ Self-Employed Characters

Self-employed characters pay both the employee and employer share of FICA (15.3% total) plus a self-employment tax. This is called SECA (Self-Employment Contributions Act). They can deduct half of SECA on their tax return, but it still stings. Roughly, a self-employed character earning $50,000 pays about $3,700 in SECA vs. $1,850 for a W-2 employee earning the same — a $1,850 difference just for being your own boss.

Too Much vs. Too Little Withholding

The whole point of withholding is to pre-pay your annual tax obligation. But if the W-4 is mis-calibrated (or if the character's income situation changes during the year), the math doesn't line up:

📊 Underpayment Penalty Mechanics

If a character owes more than $1,000 in April (due to under-withholding), the IRS imposes an underpayment penalty:

  • Penalty amount: Calculate as 5–12% of the underpayment amount (the IRS uses the current federal short-term rate plus 3 percentage points, compounded monthly). For simplicity, roll 1d8 + 4 to get the percentage.
  • Example: If the character owes $3,000 and rolls 7 + 4 = 11%, the penalty is $330 on top of the $3,000.
  • Stress: +3 SP from the surprise bill. If the character cannot pay it immediately, +2 more SP (fear of collection action).
  • Safety rule: A character avoids the penalty entirely if their total withholding + estimated payments cover at least 90% of their current-year tax or 100% of their prior-year tax (110% if AGI > $150,000).

Mid-Year W-4 Adjustments

Characters can update their W-4 at any time. Common triggers:

  • Getting married or divorced
  • Having a child (new dependent)
  • Getting a second job (now earning in two places — withholding may be too low at both)
  • Buying a home (new mortgage interest deduction)
  • Receiving a large raise or bonus

Each W-4 adjustment is a minor INT check: roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35 (easier than initial setup because the character has real data from prior paychecks to work with). On failure, the new withholding is still slightly off — but by a smaller margin than the initial failure (half the previous mis-calibration).

Annual Reconciliation — Tax Day

At the end of each calendar year, the character's total withholding is compared against their actual tax liability (calculated using the brackets in Section 3.2 minus their deductions). The difference determines what happens on April 15:

📅 Tax Day Reconciliation

Step 1: Calculate total federal income tax owed for the year using the brackets in Section 3.2, minus standard or itemized deductions.

Step 2: Subtract total federal income tax withheld from all W-2s during the year. (Note: FICA is separate and is not part of this reconciliation — FICA is a flat-rate payroll tax that is paid in full through withholding and does not generate a refund or balance due.)

Step 3: The result is either:

  • Positive number (withheld > owed): Character receives a refund. The IRS sends a check or direct deposit. Processing time: 2–8 weeks. Characters who use direct deposit (INT ≥ 3 or Finance +1) get it in 2–3 weeks.
  • Negative number (withheld < owed): Character owes money. Due by April 15. Can pay by check, electronic withdrawal, or credit card (+2–3% convenience fee). Characters who cannot pay face escalating penalties: 0.5%/month of unpaid balance as a late-payment penalty, plus the underpayment penalty described above.

3.1b The Filing Process — Step by Step

Filing taxes is not a single roll — it is a multi-step process spanning January through April. Each step has its own cost, risk, and potential for disaster. The GM should walk characters through each phase:

Step 1: Gathering Your Documents

Before you can file, you need your paperwork. Every employer sends a W-2 by January 31. Freelancers and gig workers receive 1099-NEC or 1099-K forms. Investors get 1099-DIV and 1099-B. Renters may need 1099-MIS for rental income. Self-employed characters need every receipt, mileage log, and bank statement from the past year.

Step 3: Filling Out the Return

This is the actual filing check from the Tax Filing Event (Section 3.1). The character fills out Form 1040 — the main individual income tax return — plus any applicable schedules:

  • Schedule A: Itemized deductions (if exceeding the standard deduction)
  • Schedule C: Profit or loss from self-employment (freelancers, gig workers, side businesses)
  • Schedule B: Interest and dividend income
  • Schedule D: Capital gains and losses (investors)
  • Schedule E: Rental income, partnerships, S-corporations
  • Schedule 1: Additional income and adjustments (student loan interest, IRA contributions, etc.)

Step 5: Waiting and Receiving Results

Once filed, the character waits. During this period:

  • E-file accepted: The IRS sends an acceptance confirmation within 24–72 hours. The character can track their refund at IRS2Go or "Where's My Refund?"
  • Refund received: Typically 7–21 days after acceptance for direct deposit. The character's bank account increases by the refund amount. This is a minor Morale event if the refund was larger than expected.
  • Owe money: If the character owes taxes, the bill is due by April 15. Failure to pay triggers:
🔴 Underpayment Penalties

If a character owes taxes and doesn't pay by April 15:

  • Late payment penalty: 0.5% of the unpaid amount per month (max 25%). For a $2,000 balance, this is $10/month.
  • Interest: Federal short-term rate + 3%, compounded daily. Currently ~7–8% APR.
  • Installment agreement: Characters who can't pay in full can request a payment plan. Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35 to set one up. Setup fee: $31–$150 depending on amount owed and payment method.
  • Offer in compromise: Characters in severe financial distress can negotiate to pay less than the full amount. Very difficult — roll 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 65. Approval rate is ~25%.
  • Wage garnishment / levy: If the character ignores the debt for 6+ months, the IRS can seize wages or bank accounts. +5 SP. This is a catastrophic financial event.

Step 6: Estimated Taxes (Self-Employed / Investors)

Characters who are self-employed, have investment income, or receive rental income must pay quarterly estimated taxes. Four payments per year (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15):

📊 Estimated Tax Payments

Amount due: Roughly 25% of the character's expected annual tax liability per quarter. Self-employed characters also owe self-employment tax (15.3% of net earnings) on top of income tax.

Payment check: Each quarter, roll 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 45 to estimate correctly.

  • Overpayment: Safe but wasteful. Money sits with the IRS interest-free. Each overpayment of $500+ costs the character lost savings they could have earned.
  • Underpayment: If total estimated payments are less than 90% of current-year tax or 100% of prior-year tax, the character owes an underpayment penalty of 0.5–8% APR on the shortfall.
  • Failure to pay: Roll 1d10 each missed quarter: on 1–2, the IRS sends a notice (+2 SP). On three consecutive missed payments, the IRS may file a federal tax lien (destroys credit score, blocks new loans).

3.3 Deductions

Characters can reduce their taxable income. Common deductions:

  • Standard deduction: $13,850 (single), $27,700 (married filing jointly)
  • Mortgage interest: Up to $750,000 in mortgage debt
  • Student loan interest: Up to $2,500/year
  • Medical expenses: Amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • Charitable donations: Up to 60% of AGI
  • State and local taxes: Up to $10,000 (SALT cap)
  • Retroactive adjustment: Characters who itemize and find missed deductions during an audit gain a partial refund (see below).

3.3b Tax Credits

Tax credits are more powerful than deductions. A deduction reduces your taxable income — saving you money equal to your marginal tax rate (10–37%). A credit reduces your tax bill directly, dollar for dollar. A $1,000 credit saves you exactly $1,000 regardless of your bracket. Some credits are even refundable — meaning you receive money back even if your tax liability is already zero.

💡 Credits vs. Deductions — The Key Difference

Think of it this way: a deduction is like getting a discount on the price of your groceries. A credit is like getting cash back at the register. Credits are always better, dollar for dollar. A character earning $40,000 (22% bracket) would need a $4,545 deduction to save the same amount as a $1,000 credit.

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The EITC is the largest anti-poverty program in the United States. It provides a refundable credit to low- and moderate-income workers, with the amount increasing (not decreasing) as income rises — up to a point. This is the one credit where earning more money initially increases your refund, up to the phase-in peak.

📝 EITC Example

Maria is a single mother with 2 children earning $18,000/year as a cashier. Her tax liability before credits is $0 (her income is below the standard deduction). Without EITC, she would owe $0 and get $0 refund. With EITC: She receives a refundable credit of approximately $4,500. That $4,500 goes straight into her bank account — it's real money that can cover rent, groceries, or an emergency fund. This is the difference between surviving and thriving.

American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)

For characters pursuing higher education, the AOTC provides up to $2,500/year per student for the first four years of post-secondary education:

  • 100% of the first $2,000 of qualified education expenses
  • 50% of the next $2,000 of qualified expenses
  • Up to $1,000 is refundable (can create a refund even with zero tax liability)
  • Available for a maximum of 4 tax years per student
  • Phases out for MAGI between $80,000–$160,000 (single) or $160,000–$320,000 (married)
  • Student must be enrolled at least half-time
💡 AOTC vs. Lifetime Learning Credit

If the AOTC doesn't apply (student is in year 5+, not enrolled half-time, or expenses are too low), the Lifetime Learning Credit provides up to $2,000/year (20% of first $10,000 in expenses). It's non-refundable and has no year limit, but is worth less. Characters should always maximize AOTC first, then fall back to LLC.

Premium Tax Credit (PTC)

Characters who purchase health insurance through the Affordable Care Act (ACA) marketplace may qualify for the Premium Tax Credit, which subsidizes monthly premiums:

  • Available to characters earning 100–400% of the Federal Poverty Level (~$13,590–$54,360 for a single person in 2024)
  • Can reduce monthly premiums to as little as 2–8.52% of household income, depending on the plan selected
  • For a character earning $30,000 choosing a Silver plan, the PTC could cover $200–$400/month of premiums — that's $2,400–$4,800/year
  • Characters can apply the credit advance (lowering monthly payments) or retroactively (claiming it on their tax return)
🔴 PTC Repayment Risk

If a character applied for advance PTC payments but their income increased during the year (e.g., got a raise, started a side business), they may have received more credit than they qualified for. This creates a "clawback" — the excess must be repaid when filing taxes. Repayment is capped based on filing status ($300–$6,000 max). Characters with Finance +1+ should recalculate their advance PTC amount mid-year if income changed significantly.

Discovering and Claiming Credits — Mechanics

Most characters don't know about all the credits they qualify for. Finding credits is part of the filing process:

🔍 Credit Discovery Check

After completing the main filing check (Step 3 above), the GM determines which credits the character should qualify for based on their life circumstances. The character then makes a Credit Discovery Check:

Roll: 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 40 per credit they might qualify for.

  • On success: The character (or their software/preparer) correctly identifies and claims the credit.
  • On failure: The credit is missed. The character loses the money. They won't know they missed it unless they file an amended return later (expensive and stressful — +2 SP, costs $0 for self-amended or $100–$300 with a preparer).

Common missed credits: EITC (characters think they earn too much, but qualify), Saver's Credit (characters don't know about it), Child Care Credit (characters don't keep receipts), PTC (characters don't realize marketplace insurance creates a credit). A character with Finance +3 or higher automatically discovers all credits they qualify for.

📋 Example: Missed Credits Cost

Devon (INT 3, Finance 0) files using free software. He earns $28,000, has one child, and contributes $1,500 to a Roth IRA. The GM determines he qualifies for:

  • EITC: ~$2,800
  • Child Tax Credit: $2,000
  • Saver's Credit: $750 (50% of $1,500)
  • Total potential credits: $5,550

Devon rolls Credit Discovery for each: 2d10 + 0 + 0 vs TN 40. He fails on EITC and Saver's Credit (rolls 18 and 15). The software doesn't prompt for them because his income seems "too high" to the algorithm. He only claims the CTC. Result: Devon loses $3,550 he was entitled to. He won't know until next year when a friend mentions the Saver's Credit. +2 SP from the frustration of filing an amended return.

3.3c State Income Tax

Federal taxes are only half the story. Depending on where a character lives, they may also owe state income tax — filed separately, with separate forms, separate deadlines, and separate consequences.

States Without Income Tax

Nine states do not levy a general individual income tax:

  • Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming — no state income tax at all
  • New Hampshire — eliminated its income tax on wages and dividends as of 2024 (still taxes interest and dividends during a transition period; fully eliminated by 2025)

Characters living in these states skip state income tax filing entirely. This saves time, money, and +1 SP of stress compared to dual-filing states. It is one of the tangible quality-of-life benefits of living in a no-income-tax state — though these states often make up the revenue through higher sales taxes, property taxes, or other fees.

State Tax Rates

For the 41 states (plus DC) that do levy income tax, rates vary dramatically:

Approximate annual state tax: For a character earning $50,000, state income tax typically ranges from $0 (no-tax state) to $3,000–$5,000 (high-tax state like California or New York).

Filing State Taxes

State tax filing happens after or alongside federal filing. The state return often depends on federal adjusted gross income (AGI), so the federal return must be completed first.

📊 State Tax Filing Check

When: Usually filed by the same April 15 deadline, though some states differ (e.g., Connecticut and Massachusetts use different rules for certain income).

Roll: 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod + method bonus vs TN 35 (most state returns are simpler than federal).

Stress: +1 SP for state filing on top of the +1 SP for federal filing. Total tax season stress: +2 SP for dual-filing states, +1 SP for no-income-tax states.

On failure: Same error structure as federal filing (Section 3.1b, Step 3). State tax errors are generally smaller in dollar amount but equally annoying to correct.

Processing time: State refunds take 3–12 weeks. Some states (Texas, Florida) have no refund to wait for. California and New York are notoriously slow — up to 12 weeks.

Cross-State Workers & Reciprocal Agreements

Characters who live in one state but work in another (commuters, remote workers whose employer is in a different state, seasonal workers) face additional complexity:

  • Dual filing: The character may owe tax to both states — their state of residence and their state of employment.

  • Credit for taxes paid: Most states offer a credit for income tax paid to another state, preventing double taxation. But claiming this credit requires an additional check.

  • Reciprocal agreements: Some state pairs have agreements where residents work in the neighboring state but only pay tax to their home state. Major reciprocal pairs: PA-NJ-MD-DE-WV, IL-IN, MI-IN, OH-IN, CO-WY, NY-CT-NJ-PA.

🔴 Cross-State Filing Penalty

For characters who work across state lines:

  • Additional filing check: Roll 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 45 to correctly handle the cross-state forms (often Form IT-203 in New York, Schedule B in California, etc.).
  • On failure: The character either pays tax twice on the same income (loses $500–$3,000) or misses a filing requirement (penalty of $50–$500 per state).
  • Extra stress: +1 SP on top of the normal state filing stress. Cross-state filers have +3 SP total for tax season (1 federal + 1 home state + 1 work state complexity).
  • Reciprocal agreement benefit: If the character's states have a reciprocal agreement, the extra check and +1 SP are eliminated.
📋 Example: Dual-State Filing

Carlos lives in New Jersey but commutes to Manhattan for his $75,000 job. NJ and NY have a reciprocal agreement — Carlos only pays income tax to New Jersey. He files one state return instead of two. Saves: ~$200 in software/preparer fees, 3–5 hours of work, and +1 SP of stress.

Contrast: Leila lives in Arizona but works remotely for a company based in California. AZ has no income tax, but California used to tax remote workers who worked for CA companies from out of state (this changed in 2020, but the GM may use older rules or a different state pair). Leila must file in both states. She rolls her cross-state check: 2d10 + 1 + 0 vs TN 45. Rolls 4 + 3 = 7 + 1 = 8 < 45. She messes up the credit for taxes paid and ends up owing an extra $1,200 to Arizona. +2 SP from the amendment process.

3.4 IRS Audit

Each year, after a character files taxes, roll 1d100:

  • 1–97: No audit. Normal processing (refund or payment within 2–8 weeks).
  • 98–100: AUDIT. The IRS selects the character's return for review.
🔍 IRS Audit Mechanics

Duration: 1–6 months. +5 SP during the audit period.
Defense roll: 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 55. On success, the return is accepted (possibly with a small adjustment of $100–$1,000). On failure, the IRS finds discrepancies: owe an additional 5–15% of claimed refund or $500–$5,000 in back taxes plus penalties.
Audit risk (chance of being selected): Base 3% (roll 98–100 on 1d100). Modifiers that increase the range: self-employed (+20% → 23%), income over $200k (+10% → 13%), cash business (+15% → 18%), complex itemized deductions (+10% → 13%), prior audit within 3 years (+5% → 8%). These stack.

3.5 Student Loan Repayment

Characters with student loans make monthly payments. The GM tracks the balance:

  • Standard repayment: 10 years, fixed monthly amount based on balance and rate (4–7% typical).
  • Income-driven repayment: Payments capped at 10–20% of discretionary income; remaining balance forgiven after 20–25 years.
  • Default risk: Each missed payment, roll 1d10: on 1–2, the loan goes into default. Consequences: wage garnishment (up to 15%), credit score destroyed, inability to get new loans, +3 SP.

3.6 Property Taxes

Homeowners pay property taxes annually, typically 0.5–2.5% of assessed home value. For a $300,000 home, this is $1,500–$7,500/year. Missed property tax payments can lead to tax liens and eventual foreclosure.

3.7 Amended Returns (Form 1040-X)

Not every mistake is discovered until after the return is already filed. Maybe a late 1099 arrives in June. Maybe a divorce changes filing status retroactively. Maybe the character realizes they forgot to claim a dependent. The IRS allows corrections via Form 1040-X, the Amended U.S. Individual Income Tax Return.

📝 Common Reasons to Amend
  • Discovered additional income: A 1099-NEC, 1099-MISC, or 1099-K arrived after filing. Unreported income means the character underpaid — and the IRS will eventually notice.
  • Missed a deduction or credit: Student loan interest, the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), childcare credits, education credits — forgetting these means leaving money on the table.
  • Changed filing status: A divorce finalized after January 1 but before April 15 means the character should have filed as "Single" or "Head of Household" instead of "Married Filing Jointly."
  • Incorrectly claimed a dependent: Either the character claimed someone they shouldn't have, or someone else claimed the character's dependent first.

Filing a 1040-X — Mechanics

An amended return is not the same as a new filing. It is a correction to a specific prior-year return and has its own constraints:

📊 Amended Return Filing Check

Roll: 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs. TN 50 (Medium–Hard).
Time cost: 3–8 hours of paperwork. Processing takes 16 weeks on average (twice as long as a normal return).
Scope: You can only amend the last 3 completed tax years. If the character discovers an error from 5 years ago, it may still be corrected, but the 3-year window is the practical limit before the statute of limitations closes.
One at a time: You can only amend one year per 1040-X form. If the character made errors in two years, they need two separate forms.

Amending a return flags it for closer scrutiny. When a character files a 1040-X, the base audit probability doubles for that year. Instead of the normal 3% chance (98–100 on 1d100), the amended return is audited on 94–100. If the character was already at elevated audit risk (self-employed, high income, etc.), the amendment can push them well into the danger zone.

Stress Costs

😰 Discovering a Filing Error
  • Small error (missed deduction worth <$500): +1 SP. Annoying but manageable.
  • Medium error (missed income or deduction worth $500–$3,000): +2 SP. The character needs to file an amendment and now has to wait 16 weeks for resolution.
  • Large error (underreported income over $3,000, or wrong filing status): +3 SP. This may involve a phone call from the IRS, potential penalties, and possibly an audit.
  • Fraud discovery (the character intentionally lied and now realizes the IRS knows): +5 SP. See Section 2 for legal consequences of tax fraud.
💡 GM Tip

Use amended returns as a narrative hook. A character who discovers they underreported freelance income in Year 1 might need to file a 1040-X — but the amendment triggers an audit that uncovers another problem from Year 2. Chain events together to create multi-session storylines.

3.8 When You Can't Pay What You Owe

The IRS determination letter arrives: "You owe $12,470." The character checks their bank account: $203.18. This is one of the most stressful situations in adult life, and the Reality RPG treats it that way. The good news — there are options. The bad news — every option has costs, and ignoring the problem is always the worst path.

🔥 Universal Rule: Ignoring the IRS Is Not an Option

The IRS does not go away. It does not forget. It does not get tired. Every month a character ignores a tax debt, the situation worsens. Interest accrues at 0.02% per day (~7.3% annually, tied to the federal short-term rate). A $0.50/day late payment penalty per return applies. After 90 days of non-payment, the IRS escalates to enforcement actions.

Option 1: Short-Term Extension (6-Month Payment Plan)

The simplest option. The character calls the IRS and requests a short-term extension — essentially a 6-month grace period to come up with the full amount. No monthly payments required during the extension.

📞 Short-Term Extension Mechanics

Eligibility: Total debt must be $100,000 or less (including penalties and interest).
Setup: Call the IRS at 1-800-829-1040 or use the Online Payment Agreement tool. 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35 (Easy) to successfully request it over the phone. On failure, the agent gives confusing instructions and the character must call back (+1 SP).
Cost: Free (no setup fee for amounts under $50,000).
Catch: Interest and penalties keep accruing. The extension only buys time — it does not reduce the debt. At the end of 6 months, the full balance (now larger) is due in one lump sum.
Stress: +1 SP at request time. If the character fails to pay the full amount at the end of 6 months, the IRS immediately escalates to an installment agreement with harsher terms (+2 SP).
Best for: Characters who expect a large incoming payment (inheritance, bonus, sale of property) within 6 months.

Option 2: Installment Agreement (Monthly Payment Plan)

The workhorse of IRS payment solutions. The character commits to paying a fixed amount each month until the debt is cleared.

💳 Installment Agreement Mechanics

Setup: 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 45 (Medium). On success, the agreement is approved with standard terms. On failure, the IRS demands a higher minimum payment or requires a full financial disclosure form (+2 SP for the humiliation of proving you're broke to the government).
Minimum monthly payment: Determined by the total amount owed:

📊 Monthly Payment Enforcement

Each month, the character must make their payment. If they miss a payment:

  • First miss: The IRS sends a notice. 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 40 to call and arrange a catch-up. On failure, the agreement is defaulted.
  • Defaulted agreement: The full balance becomes immediately due. The IRS may file a Notice of Federal Tax Lien (see Consequences below). +3 SP.
  • Reinstatement: Possible with a 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 55 (Hard) check and a lump-sum payment of the missed months.

Stress: +1 SP at setup. +1 SP each time a notice arrives. +2 SP if the agreement is defaulted.

Option 3: Offer in Compromise (Settling for Less)

The nuclear option of debt resolution. An Offer in Compromise (OIC) is a formal proposal to the IRS to settle the debt for less than the full amount owed. It is extremely difficult to qualify.

🤝 Offer in Compromise Mechanics

Eligibility: The character must prove one of three grounds:

  • Doubt as to Liability: The character genuinely disputes that the IRS's calculation is correct. 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 60 (Hard). Requires documentation proving the IRS made an error.
  • Doubt as to Collectibility: The character cannot possibly pay the full amount (even over their lifetime). This is the most common basis. 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 65 (Very Hard). The character must document income, expenses, assets, and living costs on Form 433-A (individuals) or Form 433-B (businesses).
  • Effective Tax Administration: Paying the full amount would cause "undue hardship" and the character has a history of compliance. 2d10 + INT mod + RES mod + Finance mod vs TN 70 (Near-Impossible). Requires demonstrating that collection would threaten basic survival.

Cost: $220 application fee (non-refundable if rejected) + $186–$350 in initial payment (depending on the proposed offer amount).
Processing time: 6–18 months. The IRS reviews every detail of the character's finances.
Approval rate: ~38%. The GM rolls 1d100: on 1–38, the offer is accepted. On 39–100, it is rejected (and the character now owes the full original amount plus penalties that accrued during the waiting period).
Stress: +3 SP at application (the financial disclosure is invasive). +2 SP when the decision arrives. On rejection, +2 more SP.

📝 Example: Offer in Compromise

Elena owes $18,000 in back taxes. She has $340 in her checking account, $12,000 in student loans, and a part-time job paying $1,800/month after rent and food leaves her with $200/month disposable income. She files an OIC on the basis of Doubt as to Collectibility, proposing to settle for $5,000.

Her Finance skill is +1 and INT mod is +2. She rolls 2d10 + 2 + 1 vs TN 65. She rolls a 5 and a 7 = 12 + 3 = 15. She fails — the IRS determines her "reasonable collection potential" is higher than her offer and rejects it. She now owes the original $18,000 plus 14 months of accrued interest and penalties (~$3,200 more). Total: $21,200. +7 SP total from the process.

Option 4: Currently Not Collectible (CNC) Status

For characters in genuine crisis — medical emergency, job loss, disability — the IRS can place the account in Currently Not Collectible status. Collections pause entirely. No payments, no calls, no liens. But the clock doesn't stop on interest and penalties.

🛑 Currently Not Collectible Mechanics

Eligibility: The character's monthly necessary living expenses must equal or exceed their monthly income. Essentially, they cannot afford a single dollar of payment without going without food, shelter, or utilities.
Setup: 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod vs TN 50 (Medium–Hard). The character must provide documentation of income, bank statements, and a budget. On failure, the IRS does not accept the CNC determination and continues collection efforts.
Duration: 1–24 months (GM rolls 1d12 × 2 months). The IRS reviews the situation annually. If the character's income improves, CNC status is revoked and collections resume immediately.
Catch: The IRS can still offset tax refunds and garnish Social Security (up to 15%) during CNC status. The debt is not forgiven — it is merely paused.
Stress: +2 SP at request. +1 SP each time the IRS reviews the status (annually). If revoked, +3 SP as collections resume.

Mechanics: Using the Wrong Filer

When a character chooses a filing method that is below the recommended level for their financial complexity, errors become more likely.

⚠️ Filer Mismatch Penalty

Compare the character's financial complexity level (GM-assigned, based on income sources and deductions) to their filing method's capability level:

Complexity LevelDefinitionMin. Recommended Filer
Level 1 — BasicSingle W-2, standard deduction, no dependentsAny free tool
Level 2 — SimpleW-2 + 1–2 additional 1099s, standard deduction, 0–1 dependentsTurboTax Free / TaxAct Basic
Level 3 — ModerateItemized deductions, dependents with credits, some investment incomeTurboTax Deluxe / H&R Block Online / TaxAct Deluxe

Level 5 — ExpertInternational income, trusts/estates, business entities, prior auditCPA / Tax Attorney only
📊 Mismatch Mechanics

If the character's filing method is 1 level below their complexity:

  • Tax Filing Check TN increases by +5 (e.g., TN 40 → TN 45).
  • On failure, the error is a missed credit or deduction worth $100–$800. The character loses money they were entitled to.
  • Additionally, roll 1d10: on a 1, the error is large enough to flag the return for increased audit risk (add +5 to the audit roll range — e.g., 98–100 becomes 93–100).

If the filing method is 2+ levels below their complexity:

  • Tax Filing Check TN increases by +10 (e.g., TN 40 → TN 50).
  • On failure, the character makes a material error: underreported income, wrong filing status, or an incorrectly calculated credit. The IRS will send a CP2000 notice (underpayment assessment) within 3–6 months.
  • Audit probability triples. The 1d100 audit roll threshold drops by 15 (e.g., 98–100 becomes 83–100, a 18% chance).
  • Stress: +2 SP when the CP2000 arrives. +3 SP if it triggers an audit.

If the filing method is above the character's complexity, there is no penalty — the character simply paid more than necessary. The GM may note this as a financial drain event if the character is on a tight budget.

📝 Example: Wrong Filer

DeShawn is self-employed (freelance graphic design, $35k in 1099-NEC income) — Complexity Level 4. He uses Credit Karma Taxes (a Level 1 tool) because it's free. That's 3 levels below his complexity.

His Tax Filing Check TN increases by +15 (TN 40 → TN 55). He rolls 2d10 + INT mod + Finance mod and gets 8 + 3 + 1 + 2 = 14. Total: 14 vs TN 55 = FAIL.

Material error: Credit Karma doesn't support Schedule C, so none of DeShawn's $8,200 in legitimate business expenses (home office, software subscriptions, equipment) were deducted. He overpaid by approximately $1,640 (20% of $8,200). He also has a 48% audit chance (1d100: 53–100) instead of the normal 3%.

Three months later, a CP2000 notice arrives. +2 SP. DeShawn now needs to file a 1040-X — see Section 3.7.

💡 GM Tip: The "Up-Sell" Narrative

TurboTax and other software are famous for guiding users toward paid tiers. In-game, when a character uses free software and the return has even mild complexity, the software may lock critical features behind a paywall. The character can either (a) pay to unlock, (b) switch to a different tool mid-filing (losing work, +1 SP from frustration), or (c) proceed with the limited tool and risk errors. This is a great moment for player choice under pressure.

🏛️ 4. Government Services

4.1 DMV Visits

The Department of Motor Vehicles is universally recognized as a stress-inducing institution.

🚗 DMV Visit — Universal Rule

Every DMV visit costs +2 SP regardless of purpose. Additionally:

  • Wait time: Roll 1d6 × 15 minutes (15–90 minutes). Add +30 min if visiting on a Monday or the first week of the month.
  • Form errors: Roll 1d10: on a 1, the character filled out a form incorrectly and must redo it (add another 1d6 × 15 minutes to wait time).
  • System outage: Roll 1d10: on a 1, the computer system is down. The character must come back another day (+2 more SP).

4.2 Social Security

Characters contribute 6.2% of income to Social Security (matched by employer). At age 62–67 (depending on birth year), they become eligible for monthly benefits:

  • Full retirement age (67): Average benefit ~$1,800/month
  • Early retirement (62): Reduced to ~$1,300/month
  • Disability (SSDI): Characters who become permanently disabled before retirement age may qualify. Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35 to provide adequate documentation. Denial rate is ~60% on first application.

4.3 Medicare / Medicaid

  • Medicare: Available at age 65. Covers hospital (Part A) and medical (Part B) services. Premiums ~$170/month for Part B.
  • Medicaid: Available to low-income individuals (threshold varies by state; typically under 138% of federal poverty line, ~$20,440/year for a single person). Covers most medical costs. Characters below the threshold automatically qualify unless they live in a state that hasn't expanded Medicaid.

4.4 Unemployment Benefits

When a character loses their job involuntarily, they can apply for unemployment:

  • Application: Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 30 to complete the application correctly. On failure, the application is delayed 2–4 weeks.
  • Benefit amount: ~30–50% of prior weekly wage, capped at $300–$800/week depending on state.
  • Duration: 12–26 weeks (varies by state and unemployment rate).
  • Stress: +2 SP at application (uncertainty, potential denial).
  • Eligibility: Characters who were fired for cause or quit voluntarily are not eligible.

4.5 Housing Assistance (Section 8)

Section 8 provides rental subsidies to low-income families:

  • Application: +2 SP. Wait times are 6 months to 5+ years in many areas (the waiting list is often closed).
  • Voucher value: Covers the difference between 30% of the character's income and fair market rent. For a character earning $1,500/month in a city where rent is $1,800, the voucher covers ~$1,350.
  • Annual review: Each year, income is re-verified. A roll of 1d10: on 1, an error is found and benefits are temporarily suspended (+3 SP).

4.6 Food Stamps (SNAP)

Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program provides monthly food benefits:

  • Eligibility: Income below 130% of poverty line (~$20,780/year for a single person).
  • Benefit amount: $150–$835/month depending on household size and income.
  • Application: +1 SP. Takes 30 days to process; expedited benefits available within 7 days for those with <$150 in cash.
  • Stigma: Characters using SNAP may face social judgment. In interactions where food insecurity is visible, roll 1d10: on 1–3, someone makes a negative comment (−1 Morale).

5.2 Green Card Process

Obtaining permanent residency is a multi-stage campaign arc:

  1. Application filed: +3 SP. Wait time: 6 months–3 years depending on category and country of origin.
  2. Background checks: Roll 1d10: on 1–2, additional scrutiny (+6 months delay, +2 SP).
  3. Interview: Roll 2d10 + INT mod + EXP mod vs TN 35. Must demonstrate eligibility, English proficiency (for naturalization), and good moral character.
  4. Approval: Green card issued. Character can work legally, own property, and begin path to citizenship.

5.3 Naturalization (Becoming a Citizen)

Green card holders can apply for citizenship after 3–5 years:

  • Civics test: Answer 6 of 10 questions about U.S. government and history. Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35 per question. Characters with INT ≥ 4 auto-pass 3 questions.
  • English test: Reading, writing, speaking. Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35. Characters who have lived in the U.S. 5+ years get −5 to TN.
  • Oath ceremony: On success, character becomes a U.S. citizen. +1 Morale event.

5.4 Undocumented Life Mechanics

Characters living without legal status face ongoing systemic challenges:

🔴 Undocumented Status — Ongoing Effects
  • Cannot work legally: No SSN. Characters work "under the table" — cash wages, no benefits, no workers' comp. Wage is 20–40% below market rate. Cannot report wage theft.
  • Fear of authorities: Every police encounter has +3 to DC. Any interaction with government offices triggers a 1d10 fear roll: on 1–2, the character avoids the interaction entirely, missing out on services.
  • No federal benefits: Cannot access Medicaid (except emergency), SNAP, Section 8, or federal student aid. Some states provide partial access.
  • Family separation risk: If the character has U.S.-citizen family members, roll 1d100/year: on 1–5, an ICE enforcement action affects the character's social circle, causing +3 SP and possible displacement.
  • Deportation threat: If the character is detained, roll 2d10 + INT mod (legal knowledge) vs TN 45. On failure, a deportation order is issued. Character has 30–90 days to appeal or leave.

5.5 Asylum Process

Characters fleeing persecution may seek asylum:

  • Application: Must be filed within 1 year of arrival. +5 SP (trauma of recounting persecution).
  • Biometrics & interview: 6–12 month wait. +2 SP per interaction.
  • Court hearing: Roll 2d10 + INT mod + RES mod vs TN 40. Must credibly demonstrate fear of returning to home country. Approval rate: ~40–60% depending on country of origin and current policy environment.
  • Pending status: While waiting (average 1–5 years), character has work authorization but lives in limbo. +1 SP/month of ongoing anxiety.
  • Denial: Character must leave or go undocumented (see above). +5 SP from the denial itself.

🎖️ 6. Military Service

6.1 Enlistment

Joining the military is a major life path choice available to characters aged 17–39:

📋 Enlistment Requirements
  • ASVAB test: Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 35. Must meet TN 35 (higher for certain branches).
  • Physical: Roll 2d10 + VIG mod vs TN 35. Must pass medical and fitness screening.
  • Background: Certain criminal records disqualify. Characters with felony records cannot enlist.
  • Contract: 4–6 year commitment. Character receives $20,000–$40,000/year depending on rank and branch, plus free healthcare, housing allowance, and GI Bill education benefits.

6.2 Basic Training

8–12 weeks of intense physical and mental conditioning:

  • Physical demands: Roll 2d10 + VIG mod vs TN 40 each month. Fail 2 months = discharged (medically or for failure to adapt).
  • Mental stress: +3 SP/month during training. Characters must pass a 2d10 + RES mod vs TN 35 mental resilience check each month.
  • On completion: Character gains Military Training skill (counts as +1 to VIG and DXT checks in combat or emergency situations). VIG increases by 1 (minimum 1, maximum 10).

6.3 Deployment

Deployed characters are away from the game world for 6–15 months. During deployment:

  • Combat exposure: Roll 1d10/month: on 1–3, the character experiences a combat event. Roll 2d10 + RES mod vs TN 40. On failure: +3 SP and possible injury (roll on injury table).
  • PTSD risk: After deployment, roll 1d100: on 1–15, the character develops PTSD. This is an ongoing mental health condition (see Mental Health page).
  • Relationship strain: For each significant relationship, roll 1d10: on 1–3, the relationship is strained (−1 to social checks with that person for 3–6 months).
  • On return: +2 SP from reintegration stress. Character gains Veteran status.

6.4 VA Benefits

Veterans have access to:

  • GI Bill: Up to 36 months of education benefits (covers tuition, fees, housing)
  • Healthcare: Free or low-cost healthcare through VA hospitals
  • Disability compensation: If injured in service, monthly payments based on disability rating (0–100%)
  • Home loans: VA-guaranteed mortgages with no down payment
  • Application stress: Filing VA claims is notoriously difficult. Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 40. On failure, the claim is initially denied (can appeal; +2 SP).

6.5 The Draft

In a national emergency campaign event, the GM may announce a draft:

  • All male characters aged 18–45 are registered. Roll 1d100 for each: on 1–10, they are called up.
  • Deferments: Characters in school (−5), with dependents (−5), with essential jobs (−5), or with medical conditions (VIG ≤ 3, auto-deferred) reduce their chance.
  • Conscientious objector: Roll 2d10 + RES mod vs TN 40. On success, assigned to non-combat service. On failure, must serve or face court-martial.
  • Stress: Being drafted is a +5 SP event for the character and their family.

🩹 8. First Aid & Emergency Response

8.1 First Aid as a Skill

First Aid is a learnable skill that any character can acquire. It provides mechanical benefits during emergencies:

📚 First Aid Skill

Training cost: $50–$200 for a Red Cross or AHA course (4–8 hours). +0 SP (empowering, not stressful).

Training check: Roll 2d10 + INT mod vs TN 30 to pass the certification exam. On failure, must retake the course (+$50, +1 SP from frustration).

Benefits while certified:

  • In emergencies: Character can attempt to stabilize an injured person before EMS arrives. Roll 2d10 + DXT mod + EXP mod vs TN 35. On success, reduce the injured person's injury severity by one level (e.g., Moderate → Minor).
  • Self-treatment: For minor injuries (cuts, burns, sprains), character can self-treat at home: −50% to treatment cost, −2 days to recovery time.
  • Morale: Characters with First Aid feel more prepared. −1 SP when an emergency occurs (they know what to do).
  • Recertification: Every 2 years, must renew CPR/First Aid certification ($30–$80, 2–4 hours).

8.2 CPR Certification

CPR is a subset of First Aid but can be taken separately:

  • Cost: $30–$80
  • Duration: 2–4 hours
  • Benefit: In a cardiac arrest scenario, a CPR-certified character has a 50% chance of keeping the victim alive until EMS arrives (vs. 10% without certification). Roll 1d100: on 1–50, victim survives to hospital.
  • Good Samaritan protection: In most states, CPR performed in good faith is protected from civil liability.

📋 Scenario: The Storm

Setup: It's September in South Florida. A Category 3 hurricane is making landfall. Three characters are in the area:

He fails to evacuate properly — but his undocumented status means he's afraid to go to a government shelter. He stays, hiding in his mobile home. Yuki evacuates to a hotel inland. Mr. Henderson tries to evacuate but his car breaks down (roll 1d10: result 8 — mechanical failure). He rides out the storm at home.

During the storm:

Diego's mobile home takes direct hit. Roll 1d10 for damage: 9 = severe damage. The roof is torn off. Diego rolls 2d10 + VIG mod vs TN 40 for injury: 2 + 5 + 6 = 13 < 40 ✗. He suffers minor cuts and bruises (2 weeks recovery, −1 to DXT). All his belongings are destroyed (estimated value: $3,000). +5 SP. He has no insurance to file a claim with.

Mr. Henderson's house has a tree fall on the roof. Moderate structural damage (~$25,000). He suffers a mild concussion (DXT check failed: 2d10 + DXT mod = 6 < 35 ✗). +4 SP. His insurance covers most of it ($500 deductible).

Yuki is safe at her hotel but receives a call from her elderly neighbor who couldn't evacuate. She uses her CPR certification and First Aid training to help when she returns 3 days later. Her disaster kit allowed her family to be self-sufficient during 5 days without power.

Aftermath (1–6 months):

Diego must rebuild from nothing. He works cash jobs to save for a new place (+2 SP/month for 3 months). He considers applying for FEMA aid but fears his undocumented status will be discovered (he skips it).

Mr. Henderson files an insurance claim. Roll 1d10: 7 = claim accepted. He uses his insurance to repair and receives a FEMA grant of $8,000. He buys a basic emergency kit afterward.

Yuki's condo has minor water damage ($2,000). Insurance covers it. She volunteers at a relief center (5 hours/week for a month, +1 SP but +2 Morale). Her experience convinces her to advocate for mandatory evacuation enforcement.

💡 Final GM Guidance

Law, government, and emergencies are the structural forces that make Reality RPG feel real. They should not overwhelm the game — they should frame it. Use these systems to create moments of tension, growth, and community. A well-timed jury duty event, a shared disaster, or a character's first time voting can be just as memorable as any combat encounter in a fantasy RPG. The real world is full of stakes. Your job is to make them feel meaningful, not paralyzing.
