Law, Government & Civic Life

The invisible architecture of everyday existence. Voting, courts, taxes, emergencies — systems that shape every character's life whether they pay attention or not.

In Reality RPG, the state is not a background detail — it is an active force. Characters pay taxes, interact with police, navigate bureaucracy, vote (or don't), apply for benefits, and respond to emergencies. This page provides the mechanics for every major civic institution and crisis system a player might encounter.

⚠️ Table Note

Law and government topics touch on real trauma — police violence, immigration detention, deportation, systemic racism, and disaster loss. Use lines and veils. Offer players opt-outs for scenarios involving police encounters, immigration enforcement, or government coercion. The goal is engagement, not re-traumatization.

🗳️ 1. Voting & Civic Participation

1.1 Voter Registration

Characters must be registered to vote in their state. Registration is a one-time Life Event that costs 1–3 hours of time (online or in-person at the DMV). A character with INT ≥ 4 can do it online; lower INT characters must visit in person (+1 SP from DMV stress — see Section 4).

📋 Voter Registration Check

At character creation, roll 1d10: on a 1–6, the character is registered. On a 7–10, they are not registered and must take the registration event before their first election. Characters under 18, undocumented immigrants, and certain felons (depending on state) cannot register.

1.2 Election Events

National elections are major campaign events that affect the entire game world. The GM announces an election every 2 or 4 years (matching real cycles). During an election year:

  • Stress increase: All characters gain +1 SP during the final month before the election (reflecting real political anxiety).
  • Policy shifts: After the election, the GM determines which policies change. This may affect taxes, healthcare access, immigration enforcement intensity, environmental regulations, and criminal justice.
  • Character involvement: Characters may campaign, donate, volunteer, or protest. Each action costs time and SP but can provide Morale events if the character's preferred outcome wins.

1.4 Jury Duty

Jury duty is a random life event. Each year, roll 1d100 for each adult character: on a 1–5, they are summoned for jury duty.

📝 Jury Duty Mechanics

Duration: Roll 1d5 + 2 = 3–7 days of lost work time.
Compensation: $50–$75/day (set by state; roll 1d3 × $25 + $25).
Lost wages: Character loses their normal daily income for those days. Net financial impact = (daily wage − jury pay) × days.
Stress: +2 SP for the experience of hearing testimony, deliberating.
INT check: Characters with INT ≥ 5 may be selected as jury foreperson (+1 Morale event, leadership experience).

The most common legal encounter. Characters receive traffic tickets based on driving behavior and 2d10 + DXT checks when driving in risky situations.

Points on license: Accumulating 6+ points in 12 months triggers a license suspension (1–6 months). During suspension, the character cannot legally drive. Roll 1d10 each time they drive anyway: on a 1–3, they are pulled over and face additional penalties.

2.2 DUI / DWI Consequences

A DUI is a major life event with cascading effects:

  • Immediate: Arrest, possible jail time (1d6 hours to 48 hours), license suspension (6 months–2 years)
  • Financial: Fines ($500–$2,500 first offense; $2,000–$10,000+ for repeat), mandatory alcohol education ($100–$500), ignition interlock device ($70–$150/month for 6–12 months), legal fees ($1,500–$5,000)
  • Employment: Roll 1d10: on 1–4, employer finds out; on 1, character is fired
  • Criminal record: Felony DUI (3rd offense or injury involved) makes future employment, housing, and loan applications harder (−2 to all PRC-based social checks for 1 year)
  • Stress: +5 SP at time of arrest; +2 SP/month for 3 months during legal proceedings

📋 Example: Traffic Stop

Marcus (Black, 24, RES 3, EXP 2) is pulled over for a broken taillight. DC = 8 + 3 (ethnicity) + 1 (young male) = DC 12. Marcus rolls 2d10 and gets 6 + 4 = 10. Total: 10 + 3 + 2 = 15 ≥ 12 ✓. He remains calm, hands are visible, answers politely. Officer gives him a $75 warning (no fine) and tells him to fix the light. +0 SP. Marcus walks away unharmed.

Same roll, different character: If Marcus had rolled a 2 + 1 = 3, total = 8 < 12 ✗. The officer extends the stop, asks to search the vehicle. Marcus refuses. Officer finds a small amount of marijuana in the glove box (GM fiat). Marcus is arrested for possession — see Minor Criminal Charges table.

💰 3. Taxes

3.1 Annual Tax Filing

Tax season (January–April) is a structured annual event for every working character:

📊 Tax Filing Event

Time: 5–20 hours depending on complexity (roll 1d4 × 5 hours).
Stress: +3 SP (filing anxiety, deadline pressure, complexity).
INT check: Characters with INT ≥ 4 can file themselves (free). INT < 4 should hire a preparer ($100–$400) or use free IRS filing programs.
Deadline: April 15. Missing the deadline adds +2 SP and a $100–$500 late fee.

3.3 Deductions

Characters can reduce their taxable income. Common deductions:

  • Standard deduction: $13,850 (single), $27,700 (married filing jointly)
  • Mortgage interest: Up to $750,000 in mortgage debt
  • Student loan interest: Up to $2,500/year
  • Medical expenses: Amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • Charitable donations: Up to 60% of AGI
  • State and local taxes: Up to $10,000 (SALT cap)
  • Retroactive adjustment: Characters who itemize and find missed deductions during an audit gain a partial refund (see below).

3.4 IRS Audit

Each year, after a character files taxes, roll 1d100:

  • 1–97: No audit. Normal processing (refund or payment within 2–8 weeks).
  • 98–100: AUDIT. The IRS selects the character's return for review.
🔍 IRS Audit Mechanics

Duration: 1–6 months. +5 SP during the audit period.
Roll: 2d10 + INT vs DC 12. On success, the return is accepted (possibly with a small adjustment of $100–$1,000). On failure, the IRS finds discrepancies: owe an additional 5–15% of claimed refund or $500–$5,000 in back taxes plus penalties.
Audit risk modifiers: Self-employed (+20 to roll), high income (+10), cash business (+15), complex deductions (+10), prior audit (+5).

3.5 Student Loan Repayment

Characters with student loans make monthly payments. The GM tracks the balance:

  • Standard repayment: 10 years, fixed monthly amount based on balance and rate (4–7% typical).
  • Income-driven repayment: Payments capped at 10–20% of discretionary income; remaining balance forgiven after 20–25 years.
  • Default risk: Each missed payment, roll 1d10: on 1–2, the loan goes into default. Consequences: wage garnishment (up to 15%), credit score destroyed, inability to get new loans, +3 SP.

3.6 Property Taxes

Homeowners pay property taxes annually, typically 0.5–2.5% of assessed home value. For a $300,000 home, this is $1,500–$7,500/year. Missed property tax payments can lead to tax liens and eventual foreclosure.

🏛️ 4. Government Services

4.1 DMV Visits

The Department of Motor Vehicles is universally recognized as a stress-inducing institution.

🚗 DMV Visit — Universal Rule

Every DMV visit costs +2 SP regardless of purpose. Additionally:

  • Wait time: Roll 1d6 × 15 minutes (15–90 minutes). Add +30 min if visiting on a Monday or the first week of the month.
  • Form errors: Roll 1d10: on a 1, the character filled out a form incorrectly and must redo it (add another 1d6 × 15 minutes to wait time).
  • System outage: Roll 1d10: on a 1, the computer system is down. The character must come back another day (+2 more SP).

4.2 Social Security

Characters contribute 6.2% of income to Social Security (matched by employer). At age 62–67 (depending on birth year), they become eligible for monthly benefits:

  • Full retirement age (67): Average benefit ~$1,800/month
  • Early retirement (62): Reduced to ~$1,300/month
  • Disability (SSDI): Characters who become permanently disabled before retirement age may qualify. Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 10 to provide adequate documentation. Denial rate is ~60% on first application.

4.3 Medicare / Medicaid

  • Medicare: Available at age 65. Covers hospital (Part A) and medical (Part B) services. Premiums ~$170/month for Part B.
  • Medicaid: Available to low-income individuals (threshold varies by state; typically under 138% of federal poverty line, ~$20,440/year for a single person). Covers most medical costs. Characters below the threshold automatically qualify unless they live in a state that hasn't expanded Medicaid.

4.4 Unemployment Benefits

When a character loses their job involuntarily, they can apply for unemployment:

  • Application: Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 8 to complete the application correctly. On failure, the application is delayed 2–4 weeks.
  • Benefit amount: ~30–50% of prior weekly wage, capped at $300–$800/week depending on state.
  • Duration: 12–26 weeks (varies by state and unemployment rate).
  • Stress: +2 SP at application (uncertainty, potential denial).
  • Eligibility: Characters who were fired for cause or quit voluntarily are not eligible.

4.5 Housing Assistance (Section 8)

Section 8 provides rental subsidies to low-income families:

  • Application: +2 SP. Wait times are 6 months to 5+ years in many areas (the waiting list is often closed).
  • Voucher value: Covers the difference between 30% of the character's income and fair market rent. For a character earning $1,500/month in a city where rent is $1,800, the voucher covers ~$1,350.
  • Annual review: Each year, income is re-verified. A roll of 1d10: on 1, an error is found and benefits are temporarily suspended (+3 SP).

4.6 Food Stamps (SNAP)

Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program provides monthly food benefits:

  • Eligibility: Income below 130% of poverty line (~$20,780/year for a single person).
  • Benefit amount: $150–$835/month depending on household size and income.
  • Application: +1 SP. Takes 30 days to process; expedited benefits available within 7 days for those with <$150 in cash.
  • Stigma: Characters using SNAP may face social judgment. In interactions where food insecurity is visible, roll 1d10: on 1–3, someone makes a negative comment (−1 Morale).

5.2 Green Card Process

Obtaining permanent residency is a multi-stage campaign arc:

  1. Application filed: +3 SP. Wait time: 6 months–3 years depending on category and country of origin.
  2. Background checks: Roll 1d10: on 1–2, additional scrutiny (+6 months delay, +2 SP).
  3. Interview: Roll 2d10 + INT + EXP vs DC 10. Must demonstrate eligibility, English proficiency (for naturalization), and good moral character.
  4. Approval: Green card issued. Character can work legally, own property, and begin path to citizenship.

5.3 Naturalization (Becoming a Citizen)

Green card holders can apply for citizenship after 3–5 years:

  • Civics test: Answer 6 of 10 questions about U.S. government and history. Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 10 per question. Characters with INT ≥ 4 auto-pass 3 questions.
  • English test: Reading, writing, speaking. Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 10. Characters who have lived in the U.S. 5+ years get −1 to DC.
  • Oath ceremony: On success, character becomes a U.S. citizen. +1 Morale event.

5.4 Undocumented Life Mechanics

Characters living without legal status face ongoing systemic challenges:

🔴 Undocumented Status — Ongoing Effects
  • Cannot work legally: No SSN. Characters work "under the table" — cash wages, no benefits, no workers' comp. Wage is 20–40% below market rate. Cannot report wage theft.
  • Fear of authorities: Every police encounter has +3 to DC. Any interaction with government offices triggers a 1d10 fear roll: on 1–2, the character avoids the interaction entirely, missing out on services.
  • No federal benefits: Cannot access Medicaid (except emergency), SNAP, Section 8, or federal student aid. Some states provide partial access.
  • Family separation risk: If the character has U.S.-citizen family members, roll 1d100/year: on 1–5, an ICE enforcement action affects the character's social circle, causing +3 SP and possible displacement.
  • Deportation threat: If the character is detained, roll 2d10 + INT (legal knowledge) vs DC 14. On failure, a deportation order is issued. Character has 30–90 days to appeal or leave.

5.5 Asylum Process

Characters fleeing persecution may seek asylum:

  • Application: Must be filed within 1 year of arrival. +5 SP (trauma of recounting persecution).
  • Biometrics & interview: 6–12 month wait. +2 SP per interaction.
  • Court hearing: Roll 2d10 + INT + RES vs DC 12. Must credibly demonstrate fear of returning to home country. Approval rate: ~40–60% depending on country of origin and current policy environment.
  • Pending status: While waiting (average 1–5 years), character has work authorization but lives in limbo. +1 SP/month of ongoing anxiety.
  • Denial: Character must leave or go undocumented (see above). +5 SP from the denial itself.

🎖️ 6. Military Service

6.1 Enlistment

Joining the military is a major life path choice available to characters aged 17–39:

📋 Enlistment Requirements
  • ASVAB test: Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 10. Must score at least 10 (higher for certain branches).
  • Physical: Roll 2d10 + VIG vs DC 10. Must pass medical and fitness screening.
  • Background: Certain criminal records disqualify. Characters with felony records cannot enlist.
  • Contract: 4–6 year commitment. Character receives $20,000–$40,000/year depending on rank and branch, plus free healthcare, housing allowance, and GI Bill education benefits.

6.2 Basic Training

8–12 weeks of intense physical and mental conditioning:

  • Physical demands: Roll 2d10 + VIG vs DC 12 each month. Fail 2 months = discharged (medically or for failure to adapt).
  • Mental stress: +3 SP/month during training. Characters must pass a 2d10 + RES vs DC 10 mental resilience check each month.
  • On completion: Character gains Military Training skill (counts as +1 to VIG and DXT checks in combat or emergency situations). VIG increases by 1 (minimum 1, maximum 10).

6.3 Deployment

Deployed characters are away from the game world for 6–15 months. During deployment:

  • Combat exposure: Roll 1d10/month: on 1–3, the character experiences a combat event. Roll 2d10 + RES vs DC 14. On failure: +3 SP and possible injury (roll on injury table).
  • PTSD risk: After deployment, roll 1d100: on 1–15, the character develops PTSD. This is an ongoing mental health condition (see Mental Health page).
  • Relationship strain: For each significant relationship, roll 1d10: on 1–3, the relationship is strained (−1 to social checks with that person for 3–6 months).
  • On return: +2 SP from reintegration stress. Character gains Veteran status.

6.4 VA Benefits

Veterans have access to:

  • GI Bill: Up to 36 months of education benefits (covers tuition, fees, housing)
  • Healthcare: Free or low-cost healthcare through VA hospitals
  • Disability compensation: If injured in service, monthly payments based on disability rating (0–100%)
  • Home loans: VA-guaranteed mortgages with no down payment
  • Application stress: Filing VA claims is notoriously difficult. Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 12. On failure, the claim is initially denied (can appeal; +2 SP).

6.5 The Draft

In a national emergency campaign event, the GM may announce a draft:

  • All male characters aged 18–45 are registered. Roll 1d100 for each: on 1–10, they are called up.
  • Deferments: Characters in school (−5), with dependents (−5), with essential jobs (−5), or with medical conditions (VIG ≤ 3, auto-deferred) reduce their chance.
  • Conscientious objector: Roll 2d10 + RES vs DC 14. On success, assigned to non-combat service. On failure, must serve or face court-martial.
  • Stress: Being drafted is a +5 SP event for the character and their family.

📋 Example: Car Crash Scenario

Amara (28, DXT 5, VIG 4, RES 6) is driving home from work during rain. She rolls 2d10 + DXT for the driving check: 3 + 7 + 5 = 15. DC for rainy conditions is 14. She barely makes it — but a car ahead brakes suddenly.

Impact: Roll 1d10 for severity: result is 6 = moderate collision. Airbags deploy. Amara rolls 2d10 + VIG vs DC 12 for injury: 4 + 2 + 4 = 10 < 12 → she suffers a whiplash injury (2 weeks recovery, −1 to DXT during recovery).

Costs: Her deductible ($500) + rental car ($40/day × 14 days = $560) + medical copay ($150) = $1,210 out of pocket. Insurance premium increases 10% for 3 years (+$240/year).

Stress: +4 SP from the crash, +1 SP/week during recovery. The other driver is also injured — Amara is not at fault (DXT roll was successful), so no liability.

🩹 8. First Aid & Emergency Response

8.1 First Aid as a Skill

First Aid is a learnable skill that any character can acquire. It provides mechanical benefits during emergencies:

📚 First Aid Skill

Training cost: $50–$200 for a Red Cross or AHA course (4–8 hours). +0 SP (empowering, not stressful).

Training check: Roll 2d10 + INT vs DC 8 to pass the certification exam. On failure, must retake the course (+$50, +1 SP from frustration).

Benefits while certified:

  • In emergencies: Character can attempt to stabilize an injured person before EMS arrives. Roll 2d10 + DXT + EXP vs DC 10. On success, reduce the injured person's injury severity by one level (e.g., Moderate → Minor).
  • Self-treatment: For minor injuries (cuts, burns, sprains), character can self-treat at home: −50% to treatment cost, −2 days to recovery time.
  • Morale: Characters with First Aid feel more prepared. −1 SP when an emergency occurs (they know what to do).
  • Recertification: Every 2 years, must renew CPR/First Aid certification ($30–$80, 2–4 hours).

8.2 CPR Certification

CPR is a subset of First Aid but can be taken separately:

  • Cost: $30–$80
  • Duration: 2–4 hours
  • Benefit: In a cardiac arrest scenario, a CPR-certified character has a 50% chance of keeping the victim alive until EMS arrives (vs. 10% without certification). Roll 1d100: on 1–50, victim survives to hospital.
  • Good Samaritan protection: In most states, CPR performed in good faith is protected from civil liability.

💡 GM: Emergency Preparedness as Character Depth

A character who invests in emergency preparedness tells a story. Maybe they lived through a hurricane. Maybe a family member died in a fire and they're determined to be ready. Maybe they're a prepper, or maybe they just learned from a close call. Let players explain why their character is (or isn't) prepared — it's a window into their backstory and values.

📖 Comprehensive Example: A Night of Emergencies

📋 Scenario: The Storm

Setup: It's September in South Florida. A Category 3 hurricane is making landfall. Three characters are in the area:

Diego — 34, undocumented construction worker, lives in a mobile home. VIG 6, INT 3, EXP 4, DXT 5, RES 5, PRC 4. No insurance. No emergency kit.

Yuki — 29, nurse, owns a condo. VIG 4, INT 6, EXP 3, DXT 4, RES 7, PRC 6. Has homeowner's insurance and a disaster preparedness kit. CPR certified.

Mr. Henderson — 72, retired teacher, lives in a house. VIG 3, INT 5, EXP 7, DXT 2, RES 4, PRC 5. Has insurance but no kit. Lives alone.

Evacuation: Mandatory evacuation order issued. Diego rolls 2d10 + EXP to evacuate with belongings: 7 + 3 + 4 = 14 ≥ 10 ✓. But his undocumented status means he's afraid to go to a government shelter. He stays, hiding in his mobile home. Yuki evacuates to a hotel inland. Mr. Henderson tries to evacuate but his car breaks down (roll 1d10: result 8 — mechanical failure). He rides out the storm at home.

During the storm:

Diego's mobile home takes direct hit. Roll 1d10 for damage: 9 = severe damage. The roof is torn off. Diego rolls 2d10 + VIG vs DC 12 for injury: 2 + 5 + 6 = 13 ≥ 12 ✓. He's shaken but unharmed. However, all his belongings are destroyed (estimated value: $3,000). +5 SP. He has no insurance to file a claim with.

Mr. Henderson's house has a tree fall on the roof. Moderate structural damage (~$25,000). He suffers a mild concussion (DXT check failed: 2d10 + 2 = 6 < 12). +4 SP. His insurance covers most of it ($500 deductible).

Yuki is safe at her hotel but receives a call from her elderly neighbor who couldn't evacuate. She uses her CPR certification and First Aid training to help when she returns 3 days later. Her disaster kit allowed her family to be self-sufficient during 5 days without power.

Aftermath (1–6 months):

Diego must rebuild from nothing. He works cash jobs to save for a new place (+2 SP/month for 3 months). He considers applying for FEMA aid but fears his undocumented status will be discovered (he skips it).

Mr. Henderson files an insurance claim. Roll 1d10: 7 = claim accepted. He uses his insurance to repair and receives a FEMA grant of $8,000. He buys a basic emergency kit afterward.

Yuki's condo has minor water damage ($2,000). Insurance covers it. She volunteers at a relief center (5 hours/week for a month, +1 SP but +2 Morale). Her experience convinces her to advocate for mandatory evacuation enforcement.

💡 Final GM Guidance

Law, government, and emergencies are the structural forces that make Reality RPG feel real. They should not overwhelm the game — they should frame it. Use these systems to create moments of tension, growth, and community. A well-timed jury duty event, a shared disaster, or a character's first time voting can be just as memorable as any combat encounter in a fantasy RPG. The real world is full of stakes. Your job is to make them feel meaningful, not paralyzing.
