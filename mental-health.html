🧠 Mental Health

Mental health is not a side quest in Reality RPG — it is the game. Every character carries psychological weight, and how they manage it shapes every other mechanic in play. This page covers the full mental health system: the Stress framework, detailed conditions, treatment, recovery, stigma, and safety tools for the table.

⚠️ Table Responsibility

This section deals with real mental health conditions. The GM must establish safety tools (see §7 below) before introducing any mental health mechanics. Players may opt out of having their character carry a diagnosed condition. Never pressure a player to roleplay something that mirrors their own trauma.

1. The Mental Health Framework

Mental health in Reality RPG operates on three interconnected tracks: Stress Points, Triggers, and Episodes. Together they form a dynamic system that reflects how mental health conditions actually behave — fluctuating, context-dependent, and responsive to treatment.

1.1 Stress Points (SP)

Every character has a Stress Pool measured in Stress Points (SP). The maximum Stress capacity equals 20 + RES modifier × 5. Characters start each scene at 0 SP unless an ongoing condition or recent event has loaded them.

Stress LevelSP RangeEffects
😌 Stable0No penalty. Character functions normally.
😐 Elevated1–5Minor distraction. −2 modifier on all RES-based checks.
😟 Stressed6–15Noticeable impairment. −5 modifier on RES and INT checks. Must make a RES check (TN 40) each night to get restful sleep.
😰 Overwhelmed16–25Significant dysfunction. −8 on all checks. Character gains one Trigger marker. Risk of Episode if SP exceeds max.
💥 Episode Threshold> Max SPCharacter enters an Episode (see below). Condition-specific effects apply.

1.2 Stress Sources

Stress accumulates from events, environment, and conditions. The GM assigns SP based on severity:

SourceSP GainedExample
Minor inconvenience+1 SPMissed bus, bad meal, awkward interaction
Stressful event+3 SPHeated argument, failed exam, work deadline
Significant trauma+5 SPJob loss, breakup, accident, public humiliation
Catastrophic event+8 SPDeath of loved one, assault, eviction, arrest
Chronic condition (per day)+1–3 SP/dayOngoing untreated condition slowly drains resources

1.3 Stress Recovery

Stress naturally decreases over time. Characters recover 1 SP per full night's sleep. Additional recovery comes from:

  • Relaxation activities (exercise, meditation, hobbies): −1 SP per activity, once per day
  • Therapy session: −3 to −5 SP (see §3)
  • Strong social support (trusted friend, family): −2 SP per meaningful interaction
  • Medication: reduces baseline SP gain by 50% (see §3)

1.4 Triggers

A Trigger is a specific situation, stimulus, or topic that causes an immediate SP spike. Each diagnosed condition comes with 2–4 default triggers. Triggers add +3 to +5 SP when encountered.

📐 Rule: Trigger Exposure

When a character encounters a Trigger, the GM may call for a RES check (TN 50). On success, the character manages their reaction and gains only +2 SP instead of the full amount. On failure, the full SP is applied and the GM may narrate a brief stress reaction (trembling, avoidance, emotional outburst).

1.5 Episodes

When SP exceeds the character's maximum capacity, they enter an Episode. An Episode lasts 1d4 hours for mild conditions or 1d4 days for severe ones. During an Episode:

  • All checks suffer a +10 TN penalty (i.e., the target number is effectively 10 harder)
  • The character gains condition-specific penalties (detailed per condition below)
  • The character cannot gain additional SP — they are at maximum stress
  • After the Episode ends, SP resets to 50% of maximum
💡 GM Tip: Episodes as Narrative Beats

An Episode is not just a mechanical penalty — it's a story moment. Use it to explore vulnerability, show character growth, or demonstrate the importance of support systems. A well-handled Episode can be one of the most memorable and meaningful moments in a session.

2. Detailed Conditions

Each condition has Symptoms (narrative description), Mechanical Effects (dice modifiers and penalties), Triggers, and Severity Levels (Mild, Moderate, Severe). Characters may have one primary condition and one or two secondary conditions. Conditions are assigned during character creation or emerge from in-game events.

📐 Rule: Condition Severity

Severity scales all mechanical effects: Mild = 50% of stated penalties, Moderate = full penalties, Severe = 150% of stated penalties (round up). Severity can change based on treatment adherence, life circumstances, and passage of time.

2.1 Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) 😞

The "heavy blanket" condition. MDD is characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest, and physical fatigue that compounds over time.

Symptoms

  • Persistent sadness or emptiness (narrative: character seems "flat" or withdrawn)
  • Anhedonia — inability to feel pleasure from previously enjoyable activities
  • Sleep disruption: either insomnia (can't fall/stay asleep) or hypersomnia (sleeping 12+ hours)
  • Appetite changes (significant weight gain or loss)
  • Difficulty concentrating and making decisions
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt

Mechanical Effects

EffectModifierNotes
During Episodes+10 TN on all checksEverything becomes harder
Base VIG penalty−5 VIG (effective)Applies to all VIG-based checks
Sleep disruptionRES check (TN 50) each nightFailure = no recovery SP that night
Concentration−3 on INT-based checksAffected constantly, not just during episodes
Social withdrawal−3 on EXP-based social checksCharacter avoids interaction
Chronic SP drain+2 SP per dayUntreated condition slowly fills stress pool

Triggers

Reminders of loss or failure, social isolation, seasonal changes (fall/winter), rejection, reminders of past failures, overwhelming responsibility.

📝 Example: Depression in Play

Marcus (INT 12, RES 8, VIG 10) has Moderate MDD. He's been in a mild Episode for 2 days. His effective VIG is now 5 (mod +0 instead of +2). He needs to prepare a presentation at work — a normal INT + Communication (TN 55) check. But with depression: the TN becomes 65 (episode penalty), and his INT check suffers −3 from concentration issues. He rolls 2d10 = 47, adds INT mod (+2) and Communication skill (+2) = 51 total. TN is effectively 65. Failure. He fumbles the presentation, gains +5 SP, and the Episode likely continues. The GM narrates that Marcus's mind went blank, his voice shook, and his boss noticed the decline.

2.2 Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) 😰

Chronic, excessive worry about everyday things. The mind that won't shut off, always scanning for danger that isn't there.

Symptoms

  • Constant worry about multiple areas of life (work, health, relationships, finances)
  • Physical tension (muscle aches, restlessness, fatigue)
  • Racing thoughts, difficulty "turning off" the brain
  • Irritability and short temper
  • Panic attacks (sudden, intense fear with physical symptoms)

Mechanical Effects

EffectModifierNotes
Baseline anxiety−2 on all checksConstant mental noise reduces focus
Panic attacksOn any failed RES check, GM may trigger a panic attackCharacter is incapacitated for 1d6 minutes; gains +5 SP
Avoidance behaviorCharacter may refuse to attempt checks in anxiety-provoking situationsGM determines what situations provoke this
Sleep disruptionRES check (TN 55) each nightFailure = no SP recovery; −2 on all checks next day
Chronic SP drain+2 SP per dayAnxiety constantly generates stress
During Episodes+10 TN on all checks; all RES checks become TN 65+Severe anxiety spiral

Triggers

Uncertainty/ambiguity, deadlines, crowded places, being the center of attention, unexpected changes, confined spaces, conflict situations.

📝 Example: Panic Attack at Work

Yuki (RES 10) has Moderate GAD. Her boss unexpectedly calls an all-hands meeting to discuss layoffs — a classic uncertainty trigger. The GM calls for a RES check (TN 50). Yuki rolls 2d10 = 63. With her RES mod of +2 and −2 baseline anxiety penalty, her total is 63. Failure. The GM triggers a panic attack: Yuki's heart races, she can't breathe properly, and she has to leave the room. She gains +5 SP and is incapacitated for 1d6 = 4 minutes. Her colleague notices and helps her — a potential social bonding moment.

2.3 Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) 💔

When a past traumatic event continues to hijack the present. The brain's alarm system is permanently sensitized.

Symptoms

  • Flashbacks — reliving the trauma as if it's happening now
  • Nightmares related to the traumatic event
  • Hypervigilance — constantly scanning for threats
  • Emotional numbness alongside emotional volatility
  • Startle response magnified to extreme levels
  • Avoidance of anything connected to the trauma

Mechanical Effects

EffectModifierNotes
FlashbacksWhen triggered: character is forced into a stress event+5 SP immediately; RES check (TN 60) or enter Episode
Hypervigilance (bonus)+3 to PERCEPTION checks for dangers/threatsCharacter notices threats others miss
Hypervigilance (penalty)−3 to all social and relaxation checksCan't relax; reads hostility where none exists
Emotional numbness−3 on EXP-based empathy checksDifficulty connecting with others emotionally
NightmaresRES check (TN 55) each nightFailure = no SP recovery +1 SP gained
Chronic SP drain+3 SP per dayPTSD is one of the most draining conditions
Startle responseSudden loud noises or surprises force RES check (TN 45)Failure = −3 on next check as character is disoriented

Triggers

Specific to the traumatic event: locations, sounds, smells, dates, situations that resemble the original trauma. The GM and player should collaboratively define 3–5 specific triggers during character creation.

⚠️ GM Responsibility: PTSD

PTSD is often tied to specific trauma. The GM should never trigger a flashback or episode by directly re-enacting the character's trauma. Instead, use indirect triggers — a similar sound, a location that reminds the character, a date that approaches. The trauma itself should be a Line (completely off-limits) for most tables.

2.4 Bipolar Disorder 🎢

Characterized by cycling between depressive and manic phases. The unpredictability is the real mechanic — characters (and players) never know which phase they're in until it hits.

Symptoms

Depressive phase: Same as MDD — low mood, fatigue, anhedonia, sleep disruption.
Manic phase: Elevated or irritable mood, decreased need for sleep, racing thoughts, grandiosity, impulsivity, increased talkativeness, risk-taking behavior.
Hypomanic phase: Milder version of mania — increased energy and productivity, but without full impairment.

Mechanical Effects

PhaseDurationMechanical Effects
Depressive 2d6 days All MDD effects apply. Additionally: character may refuse to leave home (−5 on all checks if forced to go out)
Manic 1d4 days −8 TN on all checks (things seem easy), but: +3 TN on any check requiring caution, impulse control, or long-term thinking; character makes impulsive decisions (GM narrates 1d3 risky actions per day); −5 SP recovery per night (no sleep needed but no rest either)
Hypomanic 1d6 days +2 on all checks (genuine productivity boost), but: −2 on impulse-control checks; character undertakes one unnecessary project or conversation per day
Mixed 1d4 days Most dangerous phase: depressive symptoms + manic energy. +10 TN on all checks AND character makes risky decisions. High Episode risk.

Phase Tracking

At the start of each week, the GM rolls 1d6 to determine the character's current phase (or the player may choose, if they prefer agency):

d6PhaseNotes
1DepressiveWorst case; full MDD effects
2–3StableNo phase effects; normal function
4–5HypomanicProductive but slightly impaired judgment
6Manic or MixedRoll 1d2: 1 = Manic, 2 = Mixed
📐 Rule: Medication Stability

If the character is on a consistent medication regimen (see §3), replace the weekly phase roll with a RES check (TN 40). On success, the character remains in their current phase for another week. On failure, roll the phase table normally. This represents medication stabilizing mood but not eliminating cycling entirely.

2.5 Psychopathy / Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD) 😈

A complex condition characterized by reduced empathy, superficial charm, impulsivity, and lack of remorse. In RPG terms, this is not "evil" — it's a different operating system for human interaction.

Symptoms

  • Reduced empathy — difficulty genuinely understanding others' emotions
  • Superficial charm — can be highly persuasive and charismatic
  • Impulsivity — acts on urges without considering consequences
  • Lack of remorse — doesn't feel guilt for harmful actions
  • Manipulativeness — uses others to achieve goals
  • Shallow emotions — experiences emotions less deeply than others
  • Risk-taking — seeks thrill and stimulation constantly

Mechanical Model

EffectModifierNotes
Superficial charm+3 to PRC-based social checks for first impressions and persuasionVery effective at initial encounters; wears off over time
Reduced empathy−5 on EXP-based empathy, compassion, and emotional support checksCannot genuinely comfort or connect emotionally
Impulsivity−3 on any check requiring patience, long-term planning, or delayed gratificationActs now, deals later
Lack of remorseImmune to SP from guilt or moral conflictDoes not gain SP from actions that would normally cause moral distress
Emotional resilience+5 to RES checks for emotional suppressionCan maintain composure in situations that would break others
Risk-seeking+2 on checks involving dangerous or thrilling activitiesThrives in high-stakes situations
Long-term social penaltyAfter 1d4 weeks of sustained interaction, others gain −3 to trust checks with this characterThe mask slips; people notice something is "off"
Stress immunity (partial)Gains only 50% SP from emotional stressorsPhysical and practical stressors still apply normally

Nuance Track

Not all characters with ASPD are the same. The GM and player should establish a Nuance Track — 3–5 specific areas where the character does feel connection or care. This prevents the character from being a flat "villain" and creates compelling roleplay opportunities.

💡 GM Tip: Playing ASPD

ASPD characters are powerful social tools but can derail a campaign if they exploit other players' characters. Establish clear boundaries: ASPD characters can be charming and manipulative in the world, but not at other players. The charm bonus applies to NPCs, not to coercing fellow players into uncomfortable situations.

2.6 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) 🔁

Not just "liking things tidy." OCD involves intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors (compulsions) that consume time and mental energy.

Symptoms

  • Intrusive, unwanted thoughts that cause anxiety
  • Compulsive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety (checking, counting, washing, arranging)
  • Mental rituals (silent counting, repeating phrases, mental reviewing)
  • Time consumed by obsessions/compulsions (1+ hours per day at moderate severity)

Mechanical Effects

EffectModifierNotes
Obsessive thoughtsOnce per scene, GM may call for RES check (TN 50)Failure = character is distracted; −3 on next check
CompulsionsEach compulsion takes 1d4 minutes of in-game timeReal time cost; can delay important actions
Mental exhaustionAfter 3+ compulsions in a day: −2 on all checks for remainder of dayMental energy is depleted
Resistance penaltyIf character refuses to perform a compulsion: +3 SP immediatelyFighting the urge is stressful
Chronic SP drain+2 SP per dayMental toll of constant internal struggle

Obsession Types (choose 2–3)

ObsessionCompulsionTime Cost
Contamination / germsExcessive washing, cleaning, sanitizing1d4 min per episode
Harm / causing damageRepeated checking (locks, stove, doors)1d4 min per check
Symmetry / orderArranging, aligning, organizing objects1d6 min per episode
Forbidden thoughtsMental rituals, counting, repeating phrases1d4 min; invisible to others

2.7 Substance Use Disorder (SUD) 🍷

Addiction modeled as a progressive mechanical system. The substance provides real short-term benefits that mask long-term harm — exactly as in reality.

Addiction Stages

StageSP ThresholdMechanical Effects
Experimentation None (choice) Character chooses to use. No mechanical penalty yet. +2 to one relevant check per use (social lubrication, pain relief, etc.).
Regular Use After 10+ uses Same +2 benefit. Must make RES check (TN 45) to stop using. Failure = continues. Tolerance begins: benefits reduce to +1.
Dependence After 30+ uses Benefits cancel out with side effects (net 0). Must use daily or face withdrawal. RES check (TN 55) each day to resist craving. Failure = character uses.
Addiction After 60+ uses Net negative effect: −2 on all checks when not using. Withdrawal is severe. RES check (TN 65) to resist. Character will go to extreme lengths to obtain substance.
Severe Addiction After 100+ uses or multiple relapses −5 on all checks when not using. Withdrawal includes physical symptoms. RES check (TN 75) to resist. Health begins declining (−1 VIG per month). Social relationships deteriorate (−3 on all social checks with non-users).

Withdrawal Effects

When a dependent or addicted character goes without their substance:

Hours WithoutEffectSP Gained
0–6Mild discomfort, irritability+1 SP
6–24Physical symptoms (tremors, sweating, nausea)+3 SP
24–48Severe symptoms; VIG check (TN 50) to avoid Health damage+5 SP
48–72Peak withdrawal; all checks at +5 TN; character is barely functional+5 SP
72+ hoursWithdrawal begins subsiding; SP gain reduces to +3, then +1 per dayDecreasing

Relapse Mechanics

After a period of abstinence (7+ days without using), any stress event that would normally add +5 SP or more triggers a Relapse Check: RES check (TN = 60 + 5 × number of previous relapses). Failure means the character uses again and returns to their previous addiction stage.

📝 Example: Addiction Progression

Derek (RES 9, mod +1) starts drinking socially at a party — Experimentation. He gets +2 to social checks and has a great time. Over the next months, he drinks regularly at bars — Regular Use. The +2 benefit shrinks to +1 as tolerance builds. When his ex moves in across the hall, the stress pushes him into Dependence. He now needs a drink each evening or he gets +3 SP from withdrawal. He tries to quit — RES check (TN 55) daily. He rolls 2d10 = 44. With his +1 mod, total is 44. Success! He makes it through the day. But on day 3, a work crisis adds +5 SP. Relapse check: RES (TN 60). He rolls 2d10 = 58. Total 59. Failure. He goes to the store and buys a bottle. The cycle continues.

3. Treatment Mechanics

Treatment is the core loop for mental health in Reality RPG. Just as fantasy RPGs have healing potions and rest, reality has therapy, medication, and lifestyle changes. Treatment is slow, imperfect, and requires investment — mirroring real-world recovery.

3.1 Therapy Sessions 🛋️

Regular therapy is the most reliable treatment mechanic. Each session takes 1 hour of game time and costs $50–$200 (depending on insurance/status). The character gains a Therapy Level that improves over time, similar to a skill.

Therapy LevelSessions RequiredBenefits
Level 0 (New)0–4 sessions−20 Stress per session (one-time relief only)
Level 1 (Awareness)5–12 sessions−20 SP per session; character learns to identify triggers (advantage on one RES check per session)
Level 2 (Coping Skills)13–24 sessions−25 SP per session; −1 to all condition penalties; character can self-regulate once per day (auto-success on one RES check)
Level 3 (Management)25–40 sessions−30 SP per session; condition severity drops one level (Severe→Moderate→Mild); chronic SP drain reduced by 50%
Level 4 (Resilience)41+ sessions−30 SP per session; condition severity drops again or goes into remission; character gains +5 max SP capacity (learned emotional tolerance)
📐 Rule: Therapy Progress

Therapy advances one level after the required number of completed sessions. Missing sessions resets progress toward the next level (not the entire count). If a character misses 3+ consecutive sessions, they lose one Therapy Level. This models the real-world importance of consistency in treatment.

3.2 Medication 💊

Medication provides mechanical benefits but also carries side effects. The GM and player should discuss medication choices — there is no "best" option, only trade-offs.

Medication TypeBenefitsSide EffectsCost/Day
Antidepressants (SSRI) Reduces baseline SP gain by 50%; +2 to RES checks for emotional regulation −2 on intimacy/social bonding checks (emotional blunting); possible weight change; takes 4–6 weeks to become effective $0.10–$5
Anxiolytics (Benzodiazepines) −3 SP per dose; prevents panic attacks for 4–6 hours Risk of dependence (treat as mild SUD after 30+ days); −2 on all INT checks while under influence; drowsiness $0.05–$3
Mood Stabilizers Reduces phase transitions for bipolar characters (see §2.4); −2 to chronic SP drain Tremor; weight gain; −1 on all DXT checks; requires regular blood tests $0.10–$8
Antipsychotics Reduces severity of psychotic symptoms; +3 to RES checks Significant weight gain (−2 VIG); sedation (−2 on all checks first hour after dosing); metabolic changes $0.05–$10
Stimulants (ADHD) +3 to INT and DXT checks while active (4–6 hours) Increased anxiety (+1 SP/hour while active); crash after wearing off (−2 on all checks for 1 hour); appetite suppression $0.10–$6
📐 Rule: Medication Adherence

Characters must make a RES check (TN 40) each week to maintain consistent medication. Failure means the character missed doses that week: all medication benefits are reduced by 50% until adherence is restored for one full week. This models the real-world challenge of remembering and sticking to medication regimens.

3.3 Lifestyle Changes 🌱

Non-clinical interventions that provide modest but meaningful mechanical benefits:

PracticeTime RequiredBenefit
🏃 Regular exercise (3x/week)1 hour per session−1 SP per session; +1 to VIG checks; improves sleep quality
🧘 Meditation / mindfulness20 minutes per day−1 SP per session; after 30 days: +1 to RES checks for emotional regulation

4. Recovery Track 📈

Recovery is not linear. The Recovery Track models the ups and downs of long-term mental health improvement. Characters progress through stages, but setbacks are normal and expected.

Recovery Stages

StageRequirementsCharacter State
🔴 Crisis Starting state for diagnosed characters Condition is actively impairing daily function. Frequent Episodes. All mechanical effects at full (or severe) strength.
🟠 Seeking Help Character begins treatment (therapy, medication, or both) Condition effects reduced by 25%. Episodes become less frequent (roll 1d6; Episode only on roll of 6). Character shows awareness of their condition.
🟡 Managing 3+ months of consistent treatment; Therapy Level 2+ Condition effects reduced by 50%. Chronic SP drain reduced by 50%. Episodes are rare (roll 1d10; Episode only on 9–10). Character can function normally most days.
🟢 Stable 6+ months of consistent treatment; Therapy Level 3+ Condition effects reduced by 75%. Chronic SP drain eliminated. Episodes only occur during major life crises. Character has developed strong coping skills.
🟢 Remission 12+ months of stability; Therapy Level 4+ Condition effects are minimal (only 25% of original penalties). Character can reduce treatment frequency. Not cured — relapse is still possible under extreme stress.
📐 Rule: Setbacks

Life happens. Any event that adds +8 SP or more (catastrophic event) forces a RES check (TN 60). Failure means the character drops one Recovery Stage. This is not failure — it's realism. Recovery is a spiral, not a straight line. The character retains their Therapy Level and can climb back up.

5. Stigma Mechanics 🏷️

Mental health conditions carry social weight in the real world. NPCs react to disclosed conditions based on their own beliefs, culture, and knowledge. This creates real mechanical consequences for choosing to be open or secretive.

5.1 Disclosure Effects

NPC TypeReaction to DisclosureMechanical Effect
Supportive (educated, empathetic) Offers understanding and support +3 to all social checks with this NPC; NPC may provide resources (free therapy referral, accommodation)
Neutral (average person) Mildly uncomfortable but not hostile No immediate effect; NPC may be less likely to share personal information (−1 to information-gathering checks)
Stigmatizing (prejudiced) Views character as unstable, unreliable, or dangerous −5 to all social checks with this NPC; NPC may spread negative information (GM narrates gossip consequences)
Professional (employer, landlord, etc.) Depends on NPC's training and legal awareness Roll 1d6: 1–2 = discrimination (−5 to relevant checks); 3–4 = neutral; 5–6 = accommodation (+2 to relevant checks)

5.2 The Disclosure Decision

When a character considers disclosing their condition, they face a real trade-off:

  • Disclose: Risk stigma but potentially gain support, accommodations, and authenticity in relationships
  • Conceal: Avoid stigma but suffer −2 on all EXP-based checks when hiding their true self (the energy of masking)
💡 GM Tip: Stigma as a Choice

Stigma should never be automatic. The GM should determine each NPC's reaction individually — some strangers may be surprisingly supportive, some family members may be cruel. This mirrors real life and gives the player meaningful social navigation to perform.

6. Detailed Gameplay Scenarios 🎭

Scenario 1: The Depression Spiral

📝 Marcus's Week

Setup: Marcus (MDD, Moderate; Therapy Level 1; on SSRIs) has had a rough start to the week. He's at 12/25 SP.

Monday: Marcus wakes up. SSRIs reduce his chronic SP gain to +1 instead of +2. He attends therapy (−20 SP, but his pool is only 12, so he goes to 0). He feels better. Goes to work and crushes a presentation (INT + Communication = 42 vs TN 55 — success!).

Wednesday: His medication adherence check: RES (TN 40). He rolls 2d10 = 48. With RES mod +1, total = 47. Failure. He's been skipping doses. Medication benefits drop to 50% for the rest of the week.

Thursday: His girlfriend breaks up with him text-message. +5 SP (significant trauma). Chronic SP gain (+1). He's at 6 SP — entering the "Stressed" zone. −5 on RES and INT checks. That night: RES sleep check (TN 50). Rolls 2d10 = 55. With −5 stress penalty and +1 RES mod: effective roll = 51. Failure. No SP recovery. He's still at 6.

Friday: Chronic SP (+1) = 7. A trigger hits — he walks past the café where he proposed. +3 SP = 10. Now in Overwhelmed territory. −8 on all checks. He has to present at work. TN effectively becomes 65 (episode threshold approaching). He rolls 2d10 = 33. With INT mod +2, Comms +2, and −8 stress penalty: 29. Success — barely. He stumbles through but doesn't break.

Saturday: SP hits 14 (still accumulating). He misses therapy. Loses progress toward Therapy Level 2. The GM narrates Marcus sitting in his apartment, curtains drawn, phone buzzing with messages he can't bring himself to answer.

Resolution: Sunday, his friend checks in (social support: −2 SP → 12). He attends therapy Monday (−20 → 0). The cycle continues — but the player and character have learned that consistency matters.

Scenario 2: The PTSD Trigger Chain

📝 Amara's Night Out

Setup: Amara (PTSD from a car accident; RES 14, mod +2; Therapy Level 2) is going to a friend's birthday party at a bar near a busy intersection. She's at 4/27 SP.

Arrival: The party is loud. Background noise alone is mildly stressful (+1 SP → 5). She's in the "Elevated" zone now (−2 on RES checks).

The Trigger: A car backfires outside. Trigger hit. +5 SP → 10. She's now "Stressed." The GM calls for a RES check (TN 60) to avoid a flashback. Her Therapy Level 2 gives her advantage (roll twice, take better). She rolls 2d10 = 28 and 2d10 = 52. Takes 28. With +2 RES mod and −2 stress penalty: 28. Success! She feels the wave of panic but her grounding techniques (learned in therapy) keep her from fully breaking. She excuses herself to the bathroom for 5 minutes.

Hypervigilance in play: While in the bathroom, she hears shouting from the next room. Her hypervigilance bonus (+3 to threat perception) kicks in — she correctly identifies that it's just friends playing a drinking game, not a real fight. But the penalty (−3 to relaxation checks) means she can't actually calm down. She stays in the bathroom for 20 more minutes.

Resolution: She leaves the party early. The social cost (she missed the celebration) is real, but she managed it. The GM rewards her self-awareness: she texts her friend "I had a bad time but I'm okay" — a vulnerable but healthy communication that strengthens their relationship (+1 to future social checks with this friend).

Scenario 3: The Addiction Relapse

📝 Derek's Toughest Month

Setup: Derek (Severe Alcohol Addiction; RES 9, mod +1) has been sober for 21 days. He's in a support group (Therapy Level 1 for SUD). He's at 3/20 SP. His last relapse was 3 months ago (so relapse TN = 60 + 5 = 65).

Week 1 of sobriety: Daily RES (TN 65) to resist craving. Monday: rolls 2d10 = 37. Total 38. Success. Tuesday: rolls 2d10 = 60. Total 61. Success. He's feeling good. Support group helps (−2 SP → 1).

Week 2: Things get harder. Withdrawal subsided, but the psychological craving remains. He gets a promotion at work — celebratory drinks are offered. This is a massive trigger. The GM calls for his relapse check: RES (TN 65). He rolls 2d10 = 55. Total 56. Success — barely. He declines. His boss notices and respects it (+2 to future professional checks with boss).

Week 3: His anniversary of the accident that killed his brother arrives. +8 SP (catastrophic stressor). He's at 9 SP — entering "Stressed." The relapse check becomes harder because his SP is high: the GM adds +1 to the TN per 5 SP above 5. New TN = 65 + 1 = 66. He rolls 2d10 = 62. Total 63. Failure.

The relapse: He drives to the liquor store. Buys a bottle. Drinks alone. The GM narrates the shame — the cycle of using → feeling worse → using more. He gains +8 SP (hits max → Episode). He passes out. Wakes up the next day with a hangover (−3 on all checks for the day) and 10/20 SP.

Aftermath: This is his second relapse. Future relapse TN = 60 + 10 = 70. He returns to support group the next day — a small victory. The GM narrates that his sponsor says "What matters isn't that you fell. It matters how quickly you got back up." He gains −2 SP from the support interaction.

Lesson: Relapse is part of the journey, not the end of it. The mechanics reflect this — Derek can try again. The TN is higher, yes, but his Therapy Level will grow, and so will his resilience.

7. Safety Tools for the Table 🛡️

Mental health content is powerful and necessary in a realistic RPG — but it demands responsibility. These tools are mandatory, not optional, when mental health mechanics are in play.

7.1 Lines and Veils

Before the first session, every player (including the GM) establishes:

  • 🚫 Lines: Topics that are completely off-limits. The GM will never introduce these. If a player has a Line on, say, domestic violence, no character will ever experience or witness it. Period.
  • 🌫️ Veils: Topics that can happen but should be fade-to-black. The event occurs off-screen; consequences are narrated but not described. Example: a character's suicide attempt happens between scenes; we pick up after with the aftermath and recovery.
📐 Rule: Lines Are Sacred

If a Line is crossed, the session stops immediately. No debate, no "but the story needs it." The GM apologizes, the topic is removed, and play resumes on different ground. A Line is a hard boundary, not a negotiation.

7.2 Check-Ins

Regular emotional temperature checks during play:

  • Pre-session check-in: "How are you feeling today? Any topics you'd like to add to the Lines or Veils list for this session?"
  • Mid-session check-in: After any intense scene, the GM asks: "Everyone good? Green (fine), yellow (getting intense but I'm okay), or red (I need a break)?"
  • Post-session check-out: "What hit hard for you? What felt good? Anything to carry forward or leave behind?"

7.3 Content Warnings

The GM provides warnings before introducing potentially triggering content:

Warning LevelWhen to UseWhat It Means
🟢 MildEvery session start"This session will touch on workplace stress and relationship conflict."
🟡 ModerateBefore specific scenes"In the next scene, a character will experience a panic attack. We'll handle it respectfully. Anyone want to adjust their Lines?"
🔴 StrongBefore heavy content"The upcoming scene involves a character confronting their addiction. This will be intense. Everyone still green? If anyone is yellow or red, we'll skip or veil this scene."

7.4 The X-Card

Any player, at any time, can say "X" (or use a physical X-card at the table). This means stop immediately, no explanation needed. The scene ends, the topic is dropped, and play moves to something else. No one asks why. No one challenges it. This is the ultimate safety net.

💡 GM Tip: Aftercare

After a heavy session involving mental health content, the GM should explicitly separate game from reality: "Alright everyone, we're done for tonight. Remember: these are stories we're telling together. If anything from tonight is sticking with you, I'm happy to talk or point you to resources. Take care of yourselves."
