👤 Character Creation

In Reality RPG, creating a character means creating a person — with all the complexity, privilege, disadvantage, culture, and history that entails. There are no "races" or "classes." There is only the messy, beautiful reality of human diversity.

Step 1: Age and Starting Point

Choose your character's age. Most campaigns start characters at 18–25 (beginning adult life) or 30–45 (established but facing midlife challenges). The GM may set a specific starting age for the campaign.

Age determines three things: your total point budget (older characters get more points for accumulated experience), your starting resources, and your life stage narrative context.

📐 Unified Point Budget

Your total point budget buys everything: attributes, skills, advantages, and disadvantages. There is no separate "skill pool" or "advantage pool" — it's all one budget. This means a 22-year-old with 120 points must choose between a high Intellect score and a bunch of skill levels, while a 50-year-old with 160 points can afford both. This reflects reality: older characters have had more time to develop.

💡 How to Build Efficiently

The most cost-effective way to reach a +3 modifier on an attribute is to buy the attribute at 13 (cost 15) and take a relevant Advantage that adds +1 or +2 (cost 3), rather than buying the raw attribute to 15 (cost 25). This saves 7 points per optimized attribute. A character who takes Book Smart and buys INT 13 ends up with INT 14 (mod +2) for just 18 total points — the same cost as buying INT 12 without the Advantage, but one point higher.

The expanded trait list (20 Advantages, 20 Disadvantages) lets you build highly specific characters. A night-shift nurse might take Night Owl + First-Responder Instincts + Disciplined with Poor Sleeper and Compulsive Overthinker — traits that reflect real occupational patterns rather than generic bonuses.

Step 2: Attribute Allocation

Assign attributes using the Point Buy System. Use your age-based point budget from Step 1. The goal is to build a real person — someone with strengths, weaknesses, and the kind of uneven capability distribution you see in actual humans.

2.1 The Cost Curve

Attributes use a progressive cost curve. Average scores are cheap; exceptional scores cost more. This lets you have one or two standout abilities without breaking your budget. The score 8 is the human average (modifier +1) and costs 5 points. Going below 8 refunds points; going above 8 costs increasingly more.

📐 Hard Limits

Minimum: 3 in any attribute. Maximum: 15 in any attribute at character creation (before bonuses from Advantages, ethnicity, or background). No attribute can be reduced below 3 or raised above 15 during point buy. The GM may allow a 16–18 starting score for characters playing historical figures, Olympic athletes, or other genuinely exceptional people — this requires narrative justification and costs 30 points.

2.2 How to Think About Your Budget

A perfectly average character (all 8s) costs exactly 30 points from your budget — the rest goes to skills, advantages, and disadvantages. A young character with 100 points has room for moderate specialization, while an older character with 160 points can afford genuine expertise. Here's how to think about spending:

💡 Design Philosophy

Reality RPG characters are not superheroes. A 15 in any attribute is genuinely exceptional — think Olympic athlete for Vigor, Nobel-level researcher for Intellect, master negotiator for Expression. Most of your character's attributes should cluster between 7–12. If every attribute is 10+, you're building someone unrealistically well-rounded. Weaknesses are as important as strengths — a character with a 5 in RES is someone who genuinely struggles with emotional regulation, and that creates compelling gameplay.

2.4 Advantage Attribute Bonuses

Certain Advantages add +1 or +2 to a specific attribute during creation. These bonuses are applied after point buy and do not count against the 15 maximum. For example:

  • Charismatic → PRC +2 (a character who bought PRC 13 gets PRC 15, modifier +3)
  • Book Smart → INT +1 (a character who bought INT 14 gets INT 15, modifier +3)
  • Athletic → VIG +1 (a character who bought VIG 14 gets VIG 15, modifier +3)
  • Resilient → RES +1 (a character who bought RES 14 gets RES 15, modifier +3)

This means the effective maximum at creation is 17 (15 bought + 2 from Charismatic) if you want to maximize an attribute. Plan accordingly — buying a 13 in the attribute your Advantage boosts is often the most cost-effective way to reach a +3 modifier.

📐 Age Adjustment Rules

Age adjustments are applied after point buy. An attribute can never drop below 3 or exceed 20 from age adjustments. A 70-year-old character who bought VIG 8 gets VIG 6 (8 − 2). A 22-year-old who bought VIG 15 stays at 15 (capped at 15 at creation; the +1 age bonus is absorbed by the cap). Age adjustments reflect population averages — a fit 65-year-old may argue with the GM for a −1 instead of −2 to VIG, backed by narrative justification.

Step 3: Ethnicity and Heritage

Reality RPG does not use fantasy "races." Instead, ethnicity is a mechanical system that reflects the real-world advantages, disadvantages, cultural knowledge, and social dynamics that come with a person's ethnic background in their society of play.

Ethnicity provides:

  • Cultural Knowledge — languages, traditions, social norms of your heritage community
  • Social Positioning — how society treats you (privilege or discrimination), which affects TNs for social interactions
  • Physical Traits — minor genetic predispositions (not stereotypes — these are optional and subtle)
  • Community Ties — access to ethnic community networks, support systems

⚖️ Important Design Note

These modifiers are not meant to quantify the "value" of any ethnic identity. They represent the structural realities of living in a society with systemic inequalities. The GM should work with players to customize these based on the specific setting (city, country, era) and each character's personal experience. A Black character in a predominantly Black neighborhood will face very different social dynamics than one in a rural white town.

Step 5: Personality Traits

Select 3 Advantages and 2 Disadvantages from the lists below. Each Advantage costs 3 points from your budget. Each Disadvantage refunds 3 points.

📐 Traits Come From Your Life

Advantages and Disadvantages are not free-floating bonuses. Each one must be grounded in your character's upbringing, experiences, or environment. The "Origin" column below shows the kinds of real-world backgrounds that produce each trait. When you pick a trait, tell the GM why your character has it — this becomes part of their backstory. A character who grew up in chaos is Street Smart, not because the rules say so, but because that's what surviving that environment does to you.

💡 Net Cost

3 Advantages (+9 points) and 2 Disadvantages (−6 refund) = 3 net points from your budget for the standard trait selection. You may choose more or fewer traits — each additional Advantage costs 3 points, and each additional Disadvantage refunds 3 points.

⚠️ Mutually Exclusive Traits

Certain traits are mechanically or narratively incompatible — a character cannot have both. If a player selects a trait that conflicts with one they already chose, the GM should ask them to pick a replacement. The following pairs are mutually exclusive:

📝 Example: Traits Grounded in Backstory

Mei Lin Chen picks Book Smart because her parents were immigrants who made her read encyclopedias as a child — "education is the only thing they can't take from you." She picks Disciplined because her mother ran the household like a military operation — wake at 6, study until 10, no excuses. She picks Healthy Habits because she joined a running club her freshman year to manage anxiety, and the routine stuck. Her Procrastinator disadvantage comes from the same pressure — when the stakes feel too high, she freezes and puts things off until the last minute. Her Shy/Socially Anxious disadvantage traces back to being the new kid three times in four years as her parents' jobs moved them around California.

These aren't just trait checkboxes — they're the skeleton of her backstory.

📝 Example: Tallulah Red Elk — Community Worker, 38

Tallulah Red Elk picks People Person because she grew up as the middle child in a large Lakota family — always mediating between her older sister and younger brother, always the one organizing family gatherings. She picks Artistic because her grandmother taught her beadwork and painting, and she still creates traditional pieces that she sells at tribal markets. She picks Community Anchor because she's lived on the reservation her entire life — everyone knows her, and she knows everyone's business. Her Trust Issues disadvantage comes from the car accident at 19 that killed her best friend — she learned that the people you rely on can be gone in an instant, and she doesn't let anyone get close enough to risk that again. Her Impulsive disadvantage shows up when she's triggered — she'll say or do things in the moment she later regrets, especially when confronted with reminders of that night.

Notice how Tallulah's traits create dramatically interesting gameplay: her Community Anchor means she can gather information and call in favors in her neighborhood, but her Trust Issues makes it hard to form new relationships outside it. Her Artistic trait gives her a creative outlet (and income), while her Impulsive streak means the GM has built-in moments to call for tense RES checks. These traits don't just describe her — they drive her story every session.

🎯 Choosing Traits for Gameplay — Not Just Backstory

When picking your Advantages and Disadvantages, think about what kind of scenes you want to play. Traits are your character's recurring dramatic hooks — the GM will keep coming back to them. Here's how to think about it from a gameplay perspective:

Pick Advantages that let you shine in situations you want to be in. If you want your character to be the group's problem-solver during crises, take First-Responder Instincts. If you want to be the social connector who opens doors, take Natural Leader or Community Anchor. If you imagine scenes where your character cooks a meal that brings people together, Good Cook gives you the mechanical tool to make that matter.

Pick Disadvantages that create drama you can still control. A good Disadvantage puts your character in tough spots without removing your agency. Perfectionist is great — it slows you down and creates time pressure, but you still choose how to respond. People-Pleaser generates conflict (you can't say no), but you still get to play the internal struggle each time. Compulsive Overthinker drains stress nightly, giving the GM a built-in reason to push your character toward crisis — but you still control how your character copes.

Avoid Disadvantages that just remove fun. Chronic Pain and Food Insecurity are mechanically heavy — they degrade your character every single day with no player input required. Take these only if the grinding hardship is the story you want to play. Similarly, Lone Wolf blocks teamwork bonuses entirely — in a group game, this can isolate you from the fun. If you want a solitary character, Shy / Socially Anxious is a lighter alternative that creates social friction without cutting you off from the party.

The best trait combinations create internal tension. A character who is Disciplined but has Gambling Tendency is not allowed (they're mutually exclusive) — and that's by design. The game wants your strengths and weaknesses to exist in different domains so they don't cancel out. Instead, pair Disciplined with Trust Issues: your character can control their impulses but can't trust others, creating rich roleplay every time someone tries to help them. Pair Community Anchor with Perfectionist: everyone depends on you, and you can't stand doing things halfway — that's a recipe for burnout arcs the GM will love to run.

Example trade-off: A player wants to play a scrappy underdog. They could take Street Smart + Resilient + Night Owl (advantages that reflect someone who survived rough nights) paired with Poor Driver (learned in a panic, never had a patient teacher) and Gamer / Screen Addict (escapism after a hard day). This creates a character who's tough and adaptable but has two clear fault lines the GM can pull on — their driving and their screen time — without making their life unplayable.

Step 6: Mental Health Baseline

Every character starts with a mental health profile. This is not optional — mental health is as fundamental to human existence as physical health. See the Mental Health chapter for full details.

At character creation, roll 1d6 on the Mental Health Baseline Table, or choose with GM approval:

d6Mental Health Starting Point
1Clinical condition present — Choose one from the Mental Health chapter (depression, anxiety disorder, etc.). Start with appropriate symptoms active.
2Elevated stress — Start with 30 Stress Points. A specific stressor is identified (family, work, finances).
3–4Generally stable — Start with 0–10 Stress. No clinical conditions. May develop during play.
5Emotionally resilient — Start with 0 Stress; gain the "Resilient" advantage for free.
6History of trauma — Start with a trauma response (GM and player collaborate on what this means mechanically). 20 Stress.

Step 7: Starting Inventory

Based on your socioeconomic background, you start with appropriate possessions. See the Inventory & Economy chapter for full item lists and costs.

📝 Starting Inventory by Class

Wealthy: Latest smartphone, laptop, car, apartment deposit, designer wardrobe, gym membership, credit cards, travel passport.

Middle Class: Reliable smartphone, used laptop, decent wardrobe, maybe a car (or transit pass), basic kitchen, one-room apartment.

Working Class: Older smartphone (cracked screen), no laptop, hand-me-down clothes, bus pass, shared room or small apartment, minimal furniture.

Poor/Homeless: Phone (if lucky), one change of clothes, whatever fits in a backpack. No fixed address.

Step 8: Name, Face, and Story

Give your character a name. Describe their appearance — not as mechanical stats (those are covered by Presence), but as a person. What do they look like? How do they carry themselves? What's their story?

Write a Backstory Paragraph (100–200 words) covering:

  • Where were you born and raised?
  • What was your family like?
  • What shaped your worldview?
  • What are you doing right now, at the start of the game?
  • What do you want?
💡 GM Tip: Collaborative Creation

Encourage players to connect their characters. Maybe Mei and Carlos went to the same high school. Maybe Marcus's brother is Raj's boss. These connections create instant drama and shared investment. The GM should offer connection bonuses: +5 to any Empathy check involving a character you have a backstory link with.
