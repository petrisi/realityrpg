👤 Character Creation

In Reality RPG, creating a character means creating a person — with all the complexity, privilege, disadvantage, culture, and history that entails. There are no "races" or "classes." There is only the messy, beautiful reality of human diversity.

Step 1: Age and Starting Point

Choose your character's age. Most campaigns start characters at 18–25 (beginning adult life) or 30–45 (established but facing midlife challenges). The GM may set a specific starting age for the campaign.

Step 2: Attribute Allocation

Assign attributes using the Point Buy System. You have 75 points to distribute among the six attributes. Minimum 3, maximum 15 (before bonuses).

Cost to buy an attribute: Value × 3 points. So a 10 costs 30 points, a 15 costs 45 points.

📝 Example Point Buy

A physically strong, mentally average character: VIG 15 (45pts), DXT 8 (24pts), INT 7 (21pts), EXP 6 (18pts), RES 8 (24pts), PRC 5 (15pts) = 147pts. Too high! Trim VIG to 12 (36pts) and DXT to 6 (18pts) = 108pts. Still high. Trim INT to 5 (15pts) and RES to 5 (15pts) = 90pts. Trim PRC to 4 (12pts) = 87pts. Still over. Trim EXP to 4 (12pts) = 84pts. One more trim: VIG to 11 (33pts) = 81pts. Close enough — the GM may allow a 10-point buffer for background bonuses.

Step 3: Ethnicity and Heritage

Reality RPG does not use fantasy "races." Instead, ethnicity is a mechanical system that reflects the real-world advantages, disadvantages, cultural knowledge, and social dynamics that come with a person's ethnic background in their society of play.

Ethnicity provides:

  • Cultural Knowledge — languages, traditions, social norms of your heritage community
  • Social Positioning — how society treats you (privilege or discrimination), which affects TNs for social interactions
  • Physical Traits — minor genetic predispositions (not stereotypes — these are optional and subtle)
  • Community Ties — access to ethnic community networks, support systems

⚖️ Important Design Note

These modifiers are not meant to quantify the "value" of any ethnic identity. They represent the structural realities of living in a society with systemic inequalities. The GM should work with players to customize these based on the specific setting (city, country, era) and each character's personal experience. A Black character in a predominantly Black neighborhood will face very different social dynamics than one in a rural white town.

Religious / Spiritual Background

Choose one. This provides community ties and potential internal conflicts.

  • Christian (denomination of choice) — Church community: automatic social network; Weekly service: −5 Stress; Conflict: +5 TN on RES when personal beliefs clash with doctrine
  • Muslim — Mosque community; Daily prayer routine (−5 Stress if maintained); Fasting during Ramadan (−5 VIG checks, +5 RES checks); Potential discrimination (see Ethnicity)
  • Jewish — Synagogue community; Holiday observance provides family bonding; Cultural emphasis on education (Academics +1)
  • Hindu — Temple community; Strong family structure; Yoga/meditation training (RES +1); Caste background may add complexity
  • Buddhist — Meditation practice (−10 Stress per week of regular practice); Mindfulness training (Empathy +1); Community varies by tradition
  • Atheist / Agnostic — No religious community bonus; Freedom from doctrine (no RES conflicts); May face social friction in religious communities (+5 TN in deeply religious areas)
  • Other / Spiritual but not religious — GM and player collaborate on specific benefits and conflicts

Step 6: Mental Health Baseline

Every character starts with a mental health profile. This is not optional — mental health is as fundamental to human existence as physical health. See the Mental Health chapter for full details.

At character creation, roll 1d6 on the Mental Health Baseline Table, or choose with GM approval:

d6Mental Health Starting Point
1Clinical condition present — Choose one from the Mental Health chapter (depression, anxiety disorder, etc.). Start with appropriate symptoms active.
2Elevated stress — Start with 30 Stress Points. A specific stressor is identified (family, work, finances).
3–4Generally stable — Start with 0–10 Stress. No clinical conditions. May develop during play.
5Emotionally resilient — Start with 0 Stress; gain the "Resilient" advantage for free.
6History of trauma — Start with a trauma response (GM and player collaborate on what this means mechanically). 20 Stress.

Step 7: Starting Inventory

Based on your socioeconomic background, you start with appropriate possessions. See the Inventory & Economy chapter for full item lists and costs.

📝 Starting Inventory by Class

Wealthy: Latest smartphone, laptop, car, apartment deposit, designer wardrobe, gym membership, credit cards, travel passport.

Middle Class: Reliable smartphone, used laptop, decent wardrobe, maybe a car (or transit pass), basic kitchen, one-room apartment.

Working Class: Older smartphone (cracked screen), no laptop, hand-me-down clothes, bus pass, shared room or small apartment, minimal furniture.

Poor/Homeless: Phone (if lucky), one change of clothes, whatever fits in a backpack. No fixed address.

Step 8: Name, Face, and Story

Give your character a name. Describe their appearance — not as mechanical stats (those are covered by Presence), but as a person. What do they look like? How do they carry themselves? What's their story?

Write a Backstory Paragraph (100–200 words) covering:

  • Where were you born and raised?
  • What was your family like?
  • What shaped your worldview?
  • What are you doing right now, at the start of the game?
  • What do you want?
💡 GM Tip: Collaborative Creation

Encourage players to connect their characters. Maybe Mei and Carlos went to the same high school. Maybe Marcus's brother is Raj's boss. These connections create instant drama and shared investment. The GM should offer connection bonuses: +5 to any Empathy check involving a character you have a backstory link with.
