Vehicles & Transportation

From bicycles to airplanes — driving mechanics, vehicle ownership, insurance, modifications, air travel, and the real cost of getting from A to B.

🚘 1. Vehicles Framework

Why Vehicles Matter

In modern life, transportation is not optional — it is the backbone of daily existence. A character's access to reliable transport determines their employment options, social opportunities, emergency preparedness, and even mental health. Living in a car-dependent area without a functioning vehicle is a severe disadvantage that can cascade into financial, social, and health problems.

📋 Core Concept: Mobility = Opportunity

A character without reliable transportation has a −5 penalty to any INT + Career or EXP + Social checks that depend on physical presence (commuting to work, attending events, visiting family). In rural or suburban settings, this penalty increases to −10. Characters in dense urban areas with good public transit suffer no penalty.

Vehicles confer:

  • Commuting: Access to jobs, appointments, and social events across wider geographic ranges.
  • Travel: Road trips, vacations, visiting family in other states/countries.
  • Social Status: The car you drive signals wealth, taste, and personality. A well-maintained vehicle gives +1 PRC in first impressions; a beat-up or illegal vehicle gives −1 PRC.
  • Emergency Transport: Ability to respond to crises, help others, evacuate quickly.
  • Work Requirements: Many jobs (delivery, sales, construction, rideshare) require a reliable vehicle as a condition of employment.

The Driving Skill

Driving is a skill rooted in DXT (Dexterity). The core driving check is:

🎲 Driving Check

2d10 + DXT modifier + Driving skill level vs. Target Number (TN)

Driving skill levels range from 0 (never driven) to 5 (professional/racer). Each level adds +5 to the check. Characters who have never driven cannot attempt driving checks at all — they must first complete driver's ed or equivalent training (1–6 months, $200–$800).

💡 Maintenance Checks

At the start of each season (every 3 months of game time), the GM may call for a INT + General Knowledge TN 30 check to notice if a vehicle is deteriorating. Failure means the player is unaware of pending issues — the vehicle may break down unexpectedly during a critical moment, adding +2 SP from the surprise and stress.

Total Cost of Ownership

The purchase price of a vehicle is only the beginning. Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) includes:

  • Purchase price (or monthly payment)
  • Insurance (annual premium)
  • Fuel/charging costs
  • Maintenance and repairs
  • Registration and taxes
  • Parking/storage
  • Depreciation (lost value over time)
  • Towing, tolls, tickets, and unexpected costs

A $20,000 economy sedan may cost $6,000–9,000 per year to own and operate — that's roughly $500–750/month just for the vehicle, not counting the initial purchase. This is a major budget line item that the GM should factor into a character's finances.

⚠️ GM Note: Vehicle Debt Spiral

A character who takes out a large loan for a vehicle beyond their means enters a debt spiral. Each month, the payment competes with rent, food, and other necessities. Characters with vehicle payments exceeding 10% of their monthly income should gain +1 SP per month from financial stress until the debt is resolved.

💡 EV Mechanics

EV owners save on fuel (~$0.04/mile vs. ~$0.12/mile for gas) and maintenance (40% less — no oil changes, fewer moving parts). ⚠️ Motorcycle Risk Modifier

All motorcycle accident severity checks are +2 to the severity roll compared to cars. Additionally, protective gear reduces injury severity by 1 stage per gear item worn (helmet, jacket with armor, gloves, boots = up to −4 stages). Riding without a helmet increases injury severity by +2 stages and may result in additional fines.

📋 Motorcycle License

In the US, operating a motorcycle requires a Class M endorsement (or separate Class M license) on top of a standard driver's license. This requires passing a written test and a skills test on a motorcycle. Some states allow a motorcycle safety course to waive the skills test. Cost: $50–200 for the course. Characters without proper licensing who ride a motorcycle risk immediate impound and fines of $200–1,000.

📖 Example: Motorcycle Accident

Carlos, DXT 14 (+2), Driving skill 2 (+10), rides his 600cc sport bike to work through downtown traffic. A car runs a red light at an intersection.

Evasive maneuver check: 2d10 + 2 + 10 = 2d10 + 12 vs TN 55. Carlos rolls 12 + 8 = 20 — total 32, well below TN 55. FAILURE.

Because his total (32) is more than 20 below the TN, this is a bad failure — an accident occurs. The GM rolls on the severity table: d10 = 6 → severe crash. Motorcycle penalty: +2 → severity becomes 8 → critical crash. Carlos is wearing a helmet and armored jacket (−2 stages), bringing it back to severity 6 → severe crash.

Carlos suffers moderate injuries (broken collarbone, road rash, concussion). Vehicle is Broken Down. Medical costs: $8,000–15,000. Insurance pays some. Carlos gains +5 SP from trauma and pain. Recovery: 4–8 weeks off work.

📋 US Driver's License Classes
  • Class D: Standard passenger vehicle license. Covers cars, SUVs, small trucks.

📋 DUI Encounter Sequence

When a character is pulled over while intoxicated, the following sequence applies:

  1. Field Sobriety Test: DXT + VIG TN 55 to pass. Failure → arrested.
  2. Breathalyzer: If arrested, BAC is measured objectively. No roll — the number is the number.
  3. Arrest & Booking: License revoked on the spot. Possible jail time (hours to days).
  4. Court Date: 30–90 days later. Legal defense costs: $500–5,000+ depending on attorney quality.
  5. Sentencing: Fine, license suspension, possible jail, ignition interlock device ($100/month for 6–24 months).
  6. Long-term effects: Insurance increases 50–100%+ for 3–5 years. Employment consequences. Family strain. Social stigma.

Repeat offenses escalate dramatically. A third DUI within 10 years is a felony in all 50 states, with prison sentences of 1–10 years.

📖 Example: DUI Night

Tom, age 32, has had 5 drinks at a bar and decides to drive home. He's driving erratically and gets pulled over.

Field Sobriety Test: Tom's DXT is 10 (0) and VIG is 12 (+1). 2d10 + 0 + 1 = 2d10 + 1 vs TN 55. He rolls 8 + 5 = 13, total 14. CATASTROPHIC FAILURE — 41 below TN. He stumbles, can't walk a straight line, fails the one-leg stand.

Breathalyzer: BAC reads 0.17 — Aggravated DUI. Tom is arrested, booked, and spends the night in jail.

Consequences (3 months later): $3,500 fine, 18-month license suspension, 60 days in jail (served on weekends), ignition interlock for 12 months ($1,200). Legal fees: $2,500. Insurance increases 80% for 4 years. Tom's employer (delivery driver) fires him. His wife files for separation. Tom gains +10 SP from the cumulative trauma of losing his job, family, and freedom.

An accident occurs when a driving check results in a critical failure (roll ≥ 96 on 2d10 before modifiers) or when the total result is more than 20 points below the TN. The GM determines the exact nature and severity of the accident.

📋 Accident Resolution Sequence
  1. Roll severity: d10 on the table above. Modify by vehicle type (motorcycle +2, large truck/SUV −1).
  2. Apply safety features: Seatbelts (−1 severity), airbags (−1 severity), ABS (−1 on icy roads). Each safety feature reduces severity by 1 stage, minimum severity 1.
  3. Vehicle damage: Move vehicle down the Condition Track per the table. Estimate repair costs.
  4. Occupant injury: Each occupant makes a VIG TN 40 check to resist injury. Failure = injury per severity level. Critical failure = double injury severity.
  5. Stress: All occupants gain SP equal to severity roll value (minimum +1 SP even for near misses).
  6. Aftermath: Insurance claims, police reports, possible legal charges if at fault.

🗺️ 14. Special Driving Scenarios

Road Trips

Road trips are a major life event in many campaigns. Planning and execution require multiple checks:

📋 Road Trip Mechanics
  • Planning: INT + General Knowledge TN 30 to plan route, rest stops, and budget. Failure = poor planning, wasted time/money.
  • Per day of driving (4+ hours): One DXT + Driving TN 30 check for the driving portion. Fatigue from sitting adds +1 to TN per consecutive day.
  • Rest stop: Stopping for 30+ minutes resets fatigue penalty. Skipping rest stops is a common (foolish) player choice.
  • Breakdown risk: Each day of driving, roll d20. On a 1, the vehicle has a breakdown event (use the Breakdown Events table). Vehicles in "Needs Repair" condition roll on d20 ≤ 5.
  • Daily costs: Gas ($20–50/day), food ($15–40/day), lodging ($60–200/night), tolls ($5–25/day).

Driving with Passengers

📋 Passenger Penalties
  • Argumentative passenger: +5 to all driving TNs from distraction.
  • Child in back seat (crying, fighting): +3 to all driving TNs.
  • GPS navigation: −3 to driving TNs (GPS helps), but fumbling with phone adds +5.
  • Child seat requirement: Children under 8 (varies by state) must be in age-appropriate car seats. Driving without one: fine of $100–500 and +2 SP from guilt/worry if an accident occurs.

Night Driving

Driving at night (defined as between 10 PM and 5 AM) adds +5 to all driving TNs due to reduced visibility, potential fatigue, and increased DUI enforcement presence. Characters who are sleep-deprived (less than 6 hours of sleep) add an additional +5 TN.

Foreign Country Driving

Driving in a foreign country adds several complications:

  • International Driving Permit (IDP): Required in many countries. Cost: $20 in the US. Without one: fine or arrest.
  • Different driving side: UK, Japan, Australia drive on the left. First day driving on the wrong side: +10 to all driving TNs.
  • Different road signs/rules: INT + General Knowledge TN 35 to navigate unfamiliar road rules. Failure = wrong turns, tickets, or dangerous situations.
  • Rental car: $30–150/day plus insurance ($15–40/day). International insurance is complex and often has exclusions.

Towing / Trailer Driving

Towing a trailer, boat, or RV adds +5 to all driving TNs regardless of conditions. Additional checks may be required:

  • Backing up a trailer: DXT + Driving TN 50. This is notoriously difficult even for experienced drivers.
  • Trailer sway (wind, passing trucks): DXT + Driving TN 55 to regain control. Failure = trailer jackknife.

Defensive Driving Course

💡 Defensive Driving Benefits

Completing a defensive driving course (8 hours, $15–50) grants:

  • +1 Driving proficiency level (e.g., Competent → Experienced)
  • Insurance discount: 5–15% off premium for 1–3 years
  • Ticket dismissal: In many states, completing a course allows you to dismiss one traffic ticket without points
  • Time cost: 8 hours (usually can be done online in states that allow it)

This is one of the best ROI investments in the entire game — $25 spent can save $500+ in insurance and ticket costs over 3 years.

📖 Example: Cross-Country Road Trip

The Chen family — parents and two kids (ages 6 and 10) — drive from Los Angeles to New York (2,800 miles) in their midsize SUV. 5-day trip, 4 driving days.

Day 1 (600 miles): Dad drives. DXT + Driving TN 30 (Dad: DXT 13, Driving 3 → 2d10 + 1 + 15). Rolls 22 → total 38. SUCCESS. Breakdown roll: d20 = 14. No issues. Cost: gas $45, food $80, hotel $120 = $245.

Day 2 (700 miles): Mom drives. Fatigue penalty: +1 TN → TN 31. Mom (DXT 14, Driving 2 → 2d10 + 2 + 10). Rolls 19 → total 31. BARE SUCCESS (exactly met TN). Close call — she nearly drifts into the shoulder but corrects. +1 SP from near-miss adrenaline. Breakdown roll: d20 = 3. BREAKDOWN!

Breakdown event: d6 = 4 → brake failure. The family pulls over safely (lucky). Tow to nearest shop: $150 tow + $400 brake repair = $550. Delay: 4 hours. The kids are cranky. Mom gains +2 SP from the stress of the breakdown and delay.

Day 3 (500 miles): Shorter day to recover. Dad drives again. Fatigue reset from rest stop. 2d10 + 16 vs TN 30. Rolls 15 → total 31. SUCCESS. Cost: $180.

Day 4 (600 miles): Mom drives. Fatigue +1 → TN 31. Rolls 25 → total 37. SUCCESS. Breakdown roll: d20 = 17. Clear. Cost: $200.

Day 5 (400 miles + arrival): Dad drives. Easy day. SUCCESS. Cost: $150.

Total trip cost: $245 + $245 + $550 (breakdown) + $180 + $200 + $150 = $1,570 for the family. That's $392.50 per person — more than a budget flight for most routes, but the family values the shared experience.

Total SP accumulated: Mom +3 SP, Dad +0 SP, Kids +1 SP each from travel fatigue. The family arrives home tired but bonded.

🎭 15. Vehicles as Story Elements

Vehicles are not just mechanical objects — they are deeply tied to character identity, relationships, and life transitions.

Social Signaling

The vehicle a character drives sends powerful social signals:

  • Luxury vehicle: Signals wealth, success. +1 to +2 PRC in status-conscious social situations. However, can attract unwanted attention, theft risk, and resentment.
  • Economy/reliable vehicle: Signals practicality, modesty. No bonus or penalty in most situations. Respected in working-class contexts.
  • Electric vehicle: Signals environmental awareness, tech-savviness. +1 PRC in progressive/tech circles; −1 PRC in traditional/rural contexts.
  • Motorcycle: Signals freedom, risk-taking. +1 PRC in youth/alternative cultures; −1 PRC in conservative/professional settings.
  • Dirty/neglected vehicle: Signals irresponsibility or poverty. −1 to −2 PRC in first impressions.
  • No vehicle (in car-dependent area): Signals financial constraint or lifestyle choice. May limit social and career opportunities.

Life Transitions

Major life events often involve vehicle changes:

  • First car: Coming-of-age milestone. Learning to drive is a rite of passage. The anxiety of the first solo drive: RES TN 35 to stay calm.
  • Family car upgrade: Growing family requires more space. Minivan or SUV purchase. Old car sold/traded in.
  • Financial crisis: Selling the good car for a cheaper one. Trading down is a −1 PRC social hit but a financial relief.
  • Success upgrade: Career advancement allows luxury vehicle purchase. New social opportunities but higher monthly costs.
  • Going car-free: Moving to a city with good transit. Selling the car saves money but requires lifestyle adjustment.
📖 Example: The First Car

Keisha, age 16, just passed her driving test. Her parents bought her a used 2012 Honda Civic for $5,500 (saved up over 2 years). On her first solo drive to pick up her friend from school, she's nervous.

First solo drive check: DXT + Driving TN 30. Keisha is a Novice driver (+5 TN penalty), so TN becomes 35. Her DXT is 14 (+2), Driving skill 1 (+5). 2d10 + 2 + 5 = 2d10 + 7. She rolls 12 + 10 = 22, total 29. FAILURE — 6 below TN.

Minor incident: Keisha misjudges a turn and clips a mailbox at the intersection. No one is hurt, but the mailbox is damaged. She's shaken. +2 SP from the adrenaline and guilt. She calls her mom, who comes to pick her up and deal with the mailbox.

Repair: Minor dent on her bumper ($200). Mailbox replacement ($150). Her parents pay but are disappointed. The next week, Keisha practices more and her driving improves. After a month of regular driving, the GM may award her Competent proficiency, removing the Novice penalty.

💰 Insurance Claims Process

📋 Filing a Claim
  1. Report the incident: Call insurance within 24–48 hours. INT TN 30 to provide accurate information.
  2. Adjuster assessment: Takes 3–10 business days. INT TN 40 to effectively document your losses (photos, receipts, witness statements).
  3. Settlement offer: INT + Negotiation TN 45 to negotiate a fair settlement. Failure = lowball offer (60–80% of actual value).
  4. Repair or replacement: If repairable, repairs authorized. If totaled, you receive actual cash value minus deductible.
  5. Premium increase: After any claim, expect a 10–30% premium increase for 3–5 years, regardless of fault in many states.
⚠️ Claim Denial Scenarios

Insurance companies deny claims for specific reasons:

  • Lapsed coverage: Even one day without active insurance = denied claim
  • Misrepresentation: Lying about driving history, vehicle use, or accident details
  • Excluded drivers: An excluded household member drove the vehicle
  • Illegal activity: Damages occurring during criminal activity are not covered
  • Wear and tear: Normal depreciation and maintenance issues are excluded
  • Flood without flood coverage: Standard comprehensive may exclude flood damage in some states

When a claim is denied, the character can appeal with an INT + Negotiation TN 50 check. Characters can hire an insurance attorney for $200–500/hour to fight denials.

📖 Example: Insurance Claim After Accident

Step 5: James's premium increases 20% for 3 years — an extra $600 over 3 years — more than the original claim cost. The GM notes this for James's budget tracking.

🚨 24. Emergency Scenarios

Vehicle emergencies are stressful, dangerous, and often happen at the worst possible time.

🛑 Breakdown on the Highway

📋 Highway Breakdown Sequence
  1. Recognize problem: INT TN 30 to identify what's wrong. Failure = doesn't notice until worse.
  2. Pull over safely: DXT + Driving TN 35. Failure = breaks down in dangerous position. Nearby traffic: DXT + Driving TN 40 to avoid collision.
  3. Assess situation: INT + Mechanical TN 35 to determine if drivable to nearest exit.
  4. Call for help: Wait time: 30 min–3 hrs. Cost: $50–200 (free with AAA). No cell service? Must flag down help or walk for phone.
  5. While waiting: RES TN 35 to stay calm. Failure = +2 SP. Dangerous location: RES TN 40 or +3 SP from fear.

🔥 Vehicle Fire

📋 Vehicle Fire Sequence

Vehicle fires are rare (d20 = 1 on breakdown AND secondary d20 = 1–3) but catastrophic:

  1. Detect smoke: INT TN 25. Success = time to pull over. Failure = fire starts unnoticed.
  2. Evacuate: All occupants: DXT TN 30. Failure = trapped; VIG TN 40 to resist smoke.
  3. Call 911: Automatic if cell service. Response: 5–20 min.
  4. Outcome: Vehicle Totaled. Personal items may be lost. Comprehensive insurance pays (minus deductible).

🌊 Flood / Water Hazard

📋 Driving Through Flooded Roads
  • < 6 inches: DXT + Driving TN 35. Most cars handle this.
  • 6–12 inches: DXT + Driving TN 45. Dangerous for sedans. Failure = stalling, engine damage ($2k–5k).
  • 12–18 inches: DXT + Driving TN 55. Most cars stall. SUVs: TN 45.
  • 18+ inches: DO NOT ATTEMPT. Vehicles swept away. If tried: DXT + Driving TN 70. Failure = swept away; VIG TN 50 swimming checks to survive.

🏔️ Being Stranded in Remote Areas

📋 Stranded Survival

If a vehicle breaks down in a remote area without cell service:

  1. Assess supplies: Water, food, first aid, warmth. Typical car: 1 day minimum if prepared.
  2. Per hour: RES TN 35 to stay calm. Failure = +2 SP. EXP + Survival: −10 bonus.
  3. Temperature extremes: Heat: VIG TN 40 per hr after 2. Cold: VIG TN 40 per hr without shelter. Failure = heat exhaustion / hypothermia.
  4. Rescue: If route known: 12–24 hrs. If not: must walk for help (VIG + Survival TN 45 per mile).
📖 Example: Desert Breakdown

Maya and her boyfriend Tom drive through the Mojave at 105°F. Maya's older Honda overheats on a road with no cell service (2 PM).

Recognize: INT TN 30: 33 — success. Steam spotted, pulls over. DXT + Driving TN 35: 38 — success. Safe stop under shade.

Assess: Maya has Mechanical Level 1. INT + Mechanical TN 35: 40 — success. Ruptured coolant hose. Car cannot be driven.

Supplies: 2 water bottles, 2 granola bars, first aid kit, phone (no service). Nearest town: 40 miles.

Hours 1–2: Both RES TN 35. Maya: 38 — success. Tom: 30 — failure. Tom +2 SP from panic. Maya calms him.

Hour 3: VIG TN 40. Maya: 42 — success. Tom: 35 — failure. Heat exhaustion signs. They share water, wet bandanas. Tom's VIG −1 temporarily.

Hour 4: A semi-trucker sees hazard lights, stops, offers ride to town. Calls tow for the Honda.

Aftermath: 4 hours stranded. Maya +3 SP. Tom +5 SP. Tow: $250. Hose replacement: $150 + $75 labor (good Samaritan discount). Total: $475. Both now always carry extra water and an emergency kit.
