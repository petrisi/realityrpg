Sexual Health & Intimacy

Identity, consent, relationships, reproduction, menstruation, and trauma — the full spectrum of embodied life.

⚠️ Table Safety First

This section covers sexuality, reproduction, and trauma. All sexual content uses fade-to-black — nothing explicit is described at the table. The GM must establish a Lines & Veils list and a safety word (e.g. "red light" = stop immediately, "yellow light" = adjust tone, "green light" = continue) before beginning this chapter. 1. Sexual Orientation & Gender Identity 🏳️‍🌈

Sexual orientation (who you're attracted to) and gender identity (who you are) are core identity factors, not optional accessories. They are chosen during character creation with full player agency and carry no inherent mechanical penalties. However, the world your character lives in may impose real social consequences — and that is where the mechanics engage.

1.1 Identity as Character Factor

During character creation, the player selects or describes their character's sexual orientation and gender identity. This is never randomly rolled. The GM cannot assign a player's identity without consent. Options include but are not limited to:

CategoryExamplesMechanical Treatment
Sexual Orientationstraight, gay, lesbian, bisexual, pansexual, asexual, demisexual, queer, questioningIdentity tag on character sheet. No inherent stat modifiers.
Gender Identitywoman, man, non-binary, genderfluid, agender, trans woman, trans man, two-spirit, questioningIdentity tag on character sheet. No inherent stat modifiers.
Expressionmasculine, feminine, androgynous, gender non-conformingSocial interaction modifier in environments with rigid gender norms (see below).
💡 Design Principle

Being LGBTQ+ is not a disadvantage. The mechanics reflect the reality that prejudice is the disadvantage — not the identity itself. In supportive environments, LGBTQ+ characters may actually receive community support bonuses that straight/cis characters cannot access.

1.2 Minority Stress Mechanic

Characters whose orientation or gender identity is stigmatized in their environment face minority stress — the chronic, low-grade stress of existing in a world that may not accept them. This is a real psychological phenomenon and is modeled as follows:

Environment TypeSP Gain (per day/week)Additional Effects
🟢 Supportive (allies, LGBTQ+ spaces, affirming family)0 SPCommunity Support Bonus: −2 to TN on RES checks involving social belonging. Gain +1 temporary SP resistance per week spent here.
🟡 Neutral/Unknown (new city, mixed signals)+1 SP per weekOne RES check per month (TN 8) to maintain emotional equilibrium. Failure = +2 SP.
🟠 Unsupportive (family rejection, hostile workplace)+2 SP per weekRES check per week (TN 10). Failure = +3 SP. May trigger mental health episodes.
🔴 Hostile/Dangerous (hate crime zones, criminalized identity)+3 SP per weekRES check per day (TN 12). Failure = +4 SP. Physical danger may apply. May trigger traumatic event (see §6).
🎲 Example: Maya's Weekly Stress

Maya is a trans woman living in a small conservative town (unsupportive environment). Her roommate is an ally (supportive). Each week she rolls 2d10 + RES mod against TN 10. Maya has RES 6 (mod +1). She rolls a 7 and a 3 = 10, plus +1 = 11. Success! She manages her stress this week. Next week she rolls a 2 and a 4 = 6, plus +1 = 7. Failure — she gains +3 SP. Her roommate helps her process it, granting the community support bonus of −2 to the next RES check.

1.3 Coming Out as a Structured Event

Coming out — disclosing one's orientation or gender identity to others — is a structured event in Reality RPG. It is always a player choice, never forced by the GM. The scene is resolved using the social interaction framework with the following steps:

  1. Choose the audience. Family? Boss? Friends? The wider public? Each has a different base TN.
  2. Set the stakes. What does the character hope to gain? What do they fear losing? The player states both.
  3. Roll the check. The character rolls 2d10 + RES mod + EXP mod against the audience's acceptance TN. The GM sets TN based on the audience's known views (TN 6 for allies, TN 10 for uncertain, TN 14+ for hostile).
  4. Resolve the outcome. See the table below.
Roll ResultNarrative OutcomeMechanical Effect
🟢 Critical Success (roll ≥ TN + 5)Warm acceptance. The person may become a stronger ally.Relationship tier increases by one. Character gains Community Support Bonus for 1 month. −3 SP (stress relief).
🟡 Success (roll ≥ TN)Acceptance, perhaps with questions or adjustment period.Relationship stable or improves slightly. −1 SP.
🟠 Narrow Failure (roll TN − 1 to TN − 3)Awkwardness, hesitation, or conditional acceptance.No relationship change. +2 SP from the tension. May need follow-up conversations.
🔴 Failure (roll TN − 4 to TN − 6)Rejection, conflict, or relationship damage.Relationship tier decreases by one. +4 SP. May trigger Episode check.
💀 Critical Failure (roll ≤ TN − 7)Severe rejection. Possible violence, estrangement, or homelessness.Relationship may be severed entirely. +6 SP. May trigger traumatic event. GM must check in with player.
📜 Safety Rule

If a coming out scene results in a critical failure, the GM must pause and check with the player. Offer to veil the worst consequences, reduce the severity, or skip ahead past the painful period. The player always has the right to say "I don't want to play through that part."

🎲 Example: Sam Comes Out to His Boss

Sam (RES 7, EXP 5) wants to come out as non-binary to his manager, who has been friendly but whose views are unknown. TN = 10 (uncertain audience). Sam rolls 2d10 + 2 + 0 = 6 + 9 + 2 = 17. Critical success! His manager responds: "Thank you for trusting me. How can I support you?" Sam gains −3 SP from the relief, and his workplace relationship moves to "Trusted." The manager begins using Sam's correct pronouns, reducing his weekly minority stress to 0 SP.

2. Intimacy & Physical Relationships 💕

Intimacy in Reality RPG is modeled across three levels — emotional, physical, and sexual — each with its own mechanics. The foundation of every intimate interaction is consent, both in-game (between characters) and out-of-game (between players at the table).

2.1 Table-Level Consent Framework

📜 Table Consent Rules (Mandatory)

Before any intimate scene is played:

  1. Player consent: Every player must agree to the scene proceeding. A single "no" or "pass" means the scene does not happen.
  2. Lines & Veils: At session zero, each player declares Lines (topics absolutely off-limits) and Veils (topics that can happen but should fade-to-black).
  3. Safety word: Establish "red/yellow/green" or a custom safety word usable by any player at any time.
  4. X-Card: Any player may silently raise an X-card (or equivalent signal) to immediately skip content without explanation.

2.2 In-Game Consent Mechanics

Consent between characters is modeled explicitly. Every intimate interaction requires affirmative, informed, ongoing consent. The mechanical framework:

StepActionMechanic
1. Signal interestOne character expresses interest in getting closerNo roll needed. Player states intent out-of-character: "My character is interested."
2. Mutual check-inThe other player decides if their character is interestedIf yes, proceed. If no, the scene ends gracefully. No roll — the player's choice is the mechanic.
3. Boundary settingCharacters discuss what they're comfortable withPlayer describes their character's boundaries. These are absolute — the other character cannot cross them.
4. Ongoing consentConsent can be withdrawn at any pointIf circumstances change (stress, fear, discomfort), either player may halt the scene in-character.
5. AftercarePost-intimacy check-inBoth characters may roll 2d10 + RES mod (TN 6) to process emotions. Failure = +1 SP from vulnerability.
💡 Design Note

Notice that no dice are rolled to determine if someone wants intimacy. Consent is a player decision, not a random outcome. This is intentional — modeling consent as a dice roll would normalize the idea that "no" can be overcome with enough persistence. It cannot.

2.3 Intimacy Levels

Relationships develop through three intimacy levels. Characters progress through them gradually, and each level has distinct mechanical effects:

LevelDescriptionHow to ReachMechanical Effects
💛 EmotionalDeep trust, vulnerability, shared secrets, emotional supportMultiple successful social interactions; EXP check (TN 8) to open up; time investment (weeks of shared scenes)−2 SP when the partner is present during stressful events. +1 to relationship satisfaction tracking.
💗 PhysicalCuddling, holding hands, hugging, non-sexual touchEmotional intimacy + mutual player consent + DXT check (TN 6) to navigate physical comfort−3 SP when together. Physical touch provides comfort bonus: +1 to next RES check within 24 hours of affectionate contact.
❤️ SexualSexual activity (always fade-to-black at the table)Physical intimacy + explicit mutual consent (both players AND both characters) + no outstanding relationship conflicts+2 to relationship satisfaction. However: vulnerability increases. If the relationship ends poorly, SP damage is doubled.

2.4 Relationship Satisfaction Tracking

Each meaningful relationship has a Satisfaction Score from 0 to 10. This score changes based on how well the characters' needs are being met:

SatisfactionScoreStateEffects
💔0–2Broken / ToxicActive source of +2 SP per week. Relationship may end.
😕3–4StrugglingMonthly EXP check (TN 10) to prevent further decline. +1 SP per week.
😐5–6StableNo SP effects. Relationship maintains current tier.
😊7–8Fulfilling−1 SP per week (emotional buffer). Relationship may grow closer.
🥰9–10Deeply fulfilling−2 SP per week. Access to Community Support Bonus even in unsupportive environments.

Satisfaction changes based on key events:

  • Quality time together: +1 (max once per week)
  • Act of kindness or support: +1
  • Betrayal or broken promise: −2
  • Unresolved conflict: −1 per week until addressed
  • Major life event navigated together: +2
  • Neglect (no contact for 2+ weeks): −1 per week

2.5 Fade-to-Black Rules

📜 Fade-to-Black (Mandatory)

All sexual content in Reality RPG uses fade-to-black. The narrative cuts away before explicit content and resumes after. Here is how it works:

  1. Players confirm mutual consent (both out-of-game and in-game).
  2. The GM narrates the scene leading up to the intimate moment: "You both undress, and the door closes behind you..."
  3. The scene fades to black. Nothing explicit is described, rolled, or discussed at the table.
  4. Time passes (usually a scene break or the next morning).
  5. The GM resumes: "You wake up the next morning..." and describes any mechanical consequences (SP changes, relationship effects, health outcomes).

Players may discuss their characters' feelings about the experience afterward, but only at the level of comfort everyone at the table has established.

🎲 Example: Fade-to-Black in Practice

GM: "Jin and Aiko have been dating for months. Tonight, after dinner, Jin reaches for Aiko's hand. Aiko's player nods — they're both comfortable proceeding. The GM describes them walking to Aiko's apartment, the conversation slowing, the tension building. 'You both know where this is going. The door clicks shut...'" [fade to black] Next scene: "Morning light filters through the blinds. You wake up tangled in sheets together. How do you feel?" Jin's player: "Relieved and happy." GM: "Okay. −2 SP for Jin, relationship satisfaction +1 to 8." Aiko's player: "Aiko feels a bit anxious about things changing." GM: "Understood. Aiko gains +1 SP from uncertainty, but −1 from overall happiness. Net 0 SP."

3. Sexual Health 🏥

Sexual health encompasses STI prevention and treatment, contraception, and sexual function. Characters who are sexually active should regularly engage with these mechanics — just as real people should.

3.1 STI Testing

Characters should get tested regularly, especially after new sexual partners. Testing is a structured action:

StepActionMechanic
Access careFind a clinic, doctor, or testing centerINT check (TN 7) to find appropriate care. Cost: $0–$200 depending on insurance.
Get testedBlood draw, urine sample, or swabNo roll. Takes 1–2 visits (some results take 1–2 weeks). VIG check (TN 6) if character has needle/phobia anxiety — failure = +2 SP.
Receive resultsPositive or negativeGM determines outcome based on exposure history. If positive: character learns what they have and treatment options.
Post-test emotional impactProcessing resultsRES check (TN 8) after receiving results. Failure = +2 SP regardless of outcome (anxiety of waiting is real).

💡 Prevention Best Practices

Characters who practice regular testing, use condoms consistently, and limit partners reduce their STI risk by ~90%. The GM should reward responsible characters — not punish them with random infections. A character who always uses protection and gets tested quarterly should almost never contract an STI.

Contraception prevents unwanted pregnancy. Each method has a typical-use effectiveness rate. When a character engages in sexual activity without wanting pregnancy, the contraception's effectiveness is tested:

Contraception Failure Resolution

When contraception fails (rolled or narratively determined), there is a chance of pregnancy. Roll 2d10 and compare to the failure chance:

  • Roll ≤ failure percentage ÷ 10: Pregnancy occurs. (e.g., pill failure = 9% → roll ≤ 0.9, so a roll of 1 on 2d10 [minimum 2] means pill failure almost never results in pregnancy in practice — reflecting that 9% is per-year typical-use, not per-act. For per-act resolution, use 1% per act for pill, 5% for condoms, 15% for natural methods.)
  • Otherwise: No pregnancy this time.
💡 Simplified Per-Act Pregnancy Risk

For gameplay purposes, use these per-act pregnancy chances when contraception fails: Pill = 1%, IUD = 0.1%, Condom = 5%, Natural = 15%, No contraception = 20–25% (varies by cycle timing). Roll 1d100 or 2d10 × 5 against the percentage.

4. Pregnancy & Reproduction 🤰

Pregnancy is one of the most significant life events in Reality RPG. It affects nearly every mechanical system — VIG, time, finances, stress, relationships, and life planning. Pregnancy is always a player choice in terms of whether to continue it.

4.1 Conception Mechanics

When a character engages in unprotected sexual activity (or contraception fails), pregnancy may occur. The chance depends on the character's age, health, and where they are in their menstrual cycle:

FactorBase Chance per ActModifier
Fertile window (days 10–16 of cycle)25–30%Peak fertility
Non-fertile days5%Low but non-zero (sperm can live 5 days)
Age 20–34Base chancePeak fertility years
Age 35–39−5%Declining fertility
Age 40+−10%Significantly reduced
High SP (>10)−5%Stress can suppress ovulation
Low VIG (<4)−5%Poor health affects fertility

To resolve: Roll 2d10 × 5 (giving 10–100). If the result is ≤ the adjusted percentage, conception occurs.

🎲 Example: Conception Roll

Emma (age 28, VIG 7, SP 3) has unprotected sex during her fertile window. Base chance = 28%. No modifiers apply. She rolls 2d10 × 5: gets 4 and 7 = 11 × 5 = 55. 55 > 28, so no conception this time. If she rolled a 1 and 2 = 3 × 5 = 15, that's ≤ 28, so conception occurs.

5. Menstruation 🩸

For characters who menstruate, the monthly cycle is an ongoing game mechanic. It affects VIG, SP, time, inventory, and daily planning.

5.1 The Menstrual Cycle Mechanic

Each menstrual cycle lasts 21–35 days (GM or player picks a number; 28 is average). The cycle has four phases with different mechanical effects:

5.2 Period Tracking

Characters should track their cycle. This can be done with a calendar, app, or period tracker:

  • Tracking method: Free apps (Flo, Clue, etc.) or a simple calendar. INT check (TN 6) to establish accurate tracking in the first 3 cycles.
  • Accurate tracking benefits: Predict period timing (plan around it), identify fertile window (for conception or avoidance), detect irregularities (may indicate health issues).
  • Irregular cycles: Characters with high SP (>10), eating disorders, PCOS, or extreme stress may have irregular cycles. GM may shift period start by ±5 days unpredictably.

5.4 Endometriosis

Endometriosis is a severe chronic condition affecting ~10% of menstruating people. It occurs when tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, causing severe pain, infertility, and organ damage.

🎲 Example: Priya's Endometriosis

Priya has undiagnosed endometriosis. Each cycle: her period lasts 9 days. Days 1–4 she's at −2 VIG and +3 SP, often staying home (losing work/school time). Days 5–9 she's at −1 VIG and +1 SP. During the luteal phase (7 days before her period), she's at −1 VIG and +1 SP. That means roughly 16 days per month she's operating at a penalty. Her annual menstrual product cost is $60 (she uses pads and heating pads). She's been telling doctors about her pain for 3 years. Each year she rolls 2d10 + INT mod against TN 12 to be taken seriously. With INT 5 (mod 0), she's rolled failures all three years. The fourth year she rolls a 9 and a 5 = 14. Breakthrough! A new doctor orders an MRI and refers her to a specialist. Surgery is scheduled: $15,000, but her insurance covers 80%. Out-of-pocket: $3,000.

6. Sexual Trauma 🛡️

⚠️ Critical Safety Notice

This section deals with sexual assault and trauma. If any player has indicated this topic as a Line (off-limits), this entire section is skipped. Even if it's a Veil (fade-to-black), the GM should handle all sexual trauma content off-screen. The following mechanics exist for characters who have experienced sexual trauma before the campaign began, or for situations where the player has explicitly consented to explore this topic.

Never use sexual trauma as a "plot twist" or punishment. Never describe the assault itself. Always focus on recovery, agency, and healing.

6.1 Safety Tools for Trauma Content

Before any trauma-related content, the table must establish:

  • Lines: Topics that will not appear at all. If "sexual assault" is a Line, it never happens to any character, PC or NPC.
  • Veils: Topics that may happen but are never described. If "sexual assault" is a Veil, it may be referenced as backstory but is never depicted at the table.
  • Safety word: An immediate stop signal that requires no explanation.
  • Aftercare plan: How the table checks in with players after heavy content. This may include a break, a check-in question, or a scene shift.
  • Player override: Any player can change a Line or Veil at any time, for any reason, with no justification required.

6.2 Trauma as a Pre-Existing Condition

Characters may have experienced sexual trauma before the campaign began. This is established during character creation only if the player chooses. The player describes the broad nature of the trauma (e.g., "my character was assaulted years ago") without detail. The GM then applies the following mechanical framework:

MechanicEffectNotes
TriggerCharacter has a Trigger related to the trauma (see Mental Health page). Common triggers: being touched unexpectedly, certain locations, authority figures, specific smells/sounds.When triggered: +3 SP immediately. RES check (TN 12) or gain an Episode.
Baseline SP increaseCharacter starts at +2 SP each week from hypervigilance and emotional carry.This is not a penalty — it reflects the real cost of carrying trauma. It can be reduced through treatment.

Hypervigilance bonusCharacter has heightened awareness of danger. +2 to EXP checks to detect threats or read dangerous situations.Trauma survivors often develop real protective skills. This is a strength, not just a weakness.

6.3 Recovery Mechanics

Recovery from sexual trauma is a long-term arc, measured in months or years, not scenes. It is nonlinear — progress, setbacks, and plateaus are all normal.

Therapy cost: $50–$200 per session without insurance; $0–$50 with insurance. Finding a trauma-specialized therapist: INT check (TN 10). Waitlists are real — 1–6 months before first session is common.

Support Systems

Recovery is faster and more stable with strong support:

  • Trusted friend/partner: −1 to TN on all therapy checks. Relationship satisfaction with this person must be ≥ 7.
  • Support group: −2 to TN on therapy checks. Finding a group: INT check (TN 8).
  • Medication (for comorbid depression/anxiety): −1 to TN on RES checks. $0–$100/month. Side effects possible: VIG check (TN 6) monthly.
  • Crisis hotline: Available at any time. Immediate −2 SP. Free. Can be used once per week for mechanical effect.

Setbacks

Recovery is not linear. Setbacks are normal and expected:

  • Anniversary reactions: On the anniversary of the trauma, +3 SP automatically. RES check (TN 10) to manage.
  • Unexpected triggers: Random encounters may trigger responses. Roll 1d100 per month. On 1–10: unexpected trigger. +2 SP. RES check (TN 10) to self-regulate.
  • Relationship stress: If a close relationship ends poorly during recovery, all therapy progress resets one phase back.
🎲 Example: Recovery Arc — Jordan

Jordan (RES 8, INT 6) experienced sexual assault 2 years before the campaign. The player chose to include this backstory. Jordan has a Trigger for "being physically restrained" and starts each week at +2 SP baseline.

Month 1–3 (Stabilization): Jordan finds a therapist (INT check TN 10: rolls 5+4=9, failure — takes 3 months to get an appointment). Each weekly session: RES check TN 10. Jordan succeeds 4 out of 12 sessions. By month 3, baseline SP drops from +2 to +1.

Month 4–8 (Processing): RES checks at TN 12. Jordan succeeds 6 out of 20 sessions. Setback in month 6 (anniversary reaction: +3 SP, but Jordan uses a crisis hotline for −2 SP, net +1). By month 8, triggers reduced to +2 SP.

Month 9–14 (Integration): Sessions now biweekly. RES checks at TN 14. Jordan's supportive partner provides −1 to TN. Succeeds 8 out of 26 sessions. Baseline SP eliminated. Triggers now only +1 SP. Jordan can engage in intimacy without fear of triggering.

Month 15+ (Post-Traumatic Growth): Jordan has integrated the experience. Gains −1 to all future RES checks. Starts volunteering at a crisis center (mentor role). The trauma is part of Jordan's story but no longer defines it.

💡 GM Guidance

The recovery arc above is a template. Each character's journey is different. Some heal faster; some take longer. Some choose not to pursue formal therapy and heal through community, spirituality, or other means. The player always leads — the GM supports. If the player wants to fast-forward through recovery, that's valid. If they want to play through every session, that's valid too.
