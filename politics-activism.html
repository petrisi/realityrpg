Politics, Activism & Systemic Change

Voting, protesting, organizing, lobbying, running for office, fighting misinformation, and the long arcs of changing society — because real heroism looks like showing up to a city council meeting at 7 PM on a Tuesday.

⚠️ Table Safety — Critical

Politics and activism touch real trauma — police violence at protests, government persecution, doxxing, employer retaliation, political violence, and the chronic stress of living under systems that harm you. Full Lines & Veils conversation required. A player may declare their character was not present for any politically-charged scene. The GM must never force a player to resolve a scenario that parallels their own real-world political trauma. If a player goes red light, the scene ends immediately.

Introduction

Politics in Reality RPG is not a side quest. It is the architecture of every other system — the laws characters navigate, the taxes they pay, the schools their children attend, the police that respond to 911 calls, the hospitals they can or cannot afford. Political engagement is modeled as a commitment system with time costs, stress effects, social consequences, and real mechanical impact on a character's world.

Whether your character is a casual voter, a weekend protestor, a full-time organizer, or someone running for city council, this page provides the tools to make political life concrete, meaningful, and mechanically impactful.

⚔️ Fantasy RPGs

Politics = a king you overthrow with a sword, or a corrupt noble you assassinate. Change happens through combat. The "system" is a person in a tower.

🏠 Reality RPG

Politics = showing up to school board meetings, canvassing door-to-door, filing FOIA requests, building coalitions over years, and accepting that change is incremental. The "system" is a thousand interlocking institutions, and changing it requires patience, coalition-building, and relentless persistence.

🔥 1. Political Polarization

In modern life, political identity is not a checkbox — it is a core dimension of self that shapes who you date, who you befriend, who you argue with at Thanksgiving, and which neighborhoods feel safe. Political polarization affects every social interaction a character has.

1.3 Family Dinner Arguments

One of the most common politically-charged scenes: family gatherings where politics come up. This is not optional — characters with Intensity 2+ will encounter these regularly.

📏 Family Argument Mechanics

When a political argument erupts at a family event:

  • Each participant gains +1 SP per round of argument (3+ rounds typical).
  • Characters may attempt to de-escalate: 2d10 + RES mod vs TN 10. Success: argument diffuses after 1 round. Failure: it escalates.
  • Characters may attempt to "win" the argument: 2d10 + INT mod + EXP mod vs TN 12. Success: they make their point, but the other person gains +2 SP (feeling dismissed). Failure: they say something regrettable.
  • Characters with Intensity 4–5 gain +2 to their argument roll but also gain an additional +1 SP regardless of outcome (the cost of caring this much).
📝 Example: Thanksgiving Goes Wrong

Setup: Marcus (INT 5, RES 3, EXP 4, Intensity 4) is at Thanksgiving with his uncle, who recently started posting conspiracy theories on Facebook. His uncle brings up a false claim about the election.

Round 1: Marcus gains +1 SP (current: 4/25). He tries to de-escalate: 2d10 + RES = 7 + 1 = 8 vs TN 10 → fail. His uncle doubles down.

Round 2: Marcus gains another +1 SP (5/25). He tries to argue: 2d10 + INT + EXP + Intensity bonus = 11 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 17 vs TN 12 → critical success. He calmly cites sources and his uncle goes quiet.

Aftermath: Marcus gains his activist penalty of +1 SP (6/25). His uncle gains +2 SP (feeling humiliated). The rest of the meal is tense. Marcus's mom gives him the look. Net result: Marcus "won" but everyone is stressed. The uncle will bring it up again next time.

1.5 Social Media Echo Chambers

Characters spend time on social media. The algorithm actively shapes their worldview:

  • Echo chamber effect: Characters with Intensity 3+ spend 1–2 hours/day consuming politically-aligned content. They gain +1 to EXP-based checks within their ideological community but −1 to social checks with people outside it.
  • Outrage cycle: Each week, roll 1d10. On a 8–10, the algorithm surfaces deeply upsetting content. The character gains +1 SP from outrage/despair.
  • Cancel culture encounters: Characters with public social media presence (Intensity 3+) have a 5% chance per month of being "cancelled" — called out for a past post, a controversial opinion, or being misquoted. This triggers a Reputation event (see Social Dynamics page) and adds +3 SP.
💡 GM Tip: Political Stress Budgets

Track a character's political SP budget separately from their general stress. Political stress accumulates from arguments, news consumption, protest participation, and campaign work. When their political SP exceeds their total SP capacity, they experience political burnout — they disengage, mute political accounts, or avoid political spaces for 1–3 months. This is a protective mechanism, not a failure.

🗳️ 2. Voting & Civic Engagement

Beyond just showing up on Election Day, civic engagement is a spectrum of commitment — from passive registration to active participation in the machinery of democracy itself.

2.1 Voter Registration & Status

At character creation, determine registration status. Roll 1d10: on 1–7, the character is registered. On 8–10, they are not. Characters under 18, undocumented immigrants, and certain felons (state-dependent) cannot register. Registering costs 1–3 hours and may require a 2d10 + INT vs TN 8 check to navigate the bureaucracy successfully.

2.2 Voting Methods

In-person, Election Day1–4 hours (wait time varies)±0HighLongest lines; most visible civic act

Mail-in / Absentee1–2 hours (filling + mailing)±0Medium-HighRisk of rejection if form errors; +1 SP from anxiety about ballot counting

2.3 Local Elections Matter More

This is a key Reality RPG principle: local elections (school board, city council, county commissioner, sheriff, judge) have more direct impact on characters' daily lives than national elections — yet they get 1/10th the attention.

📏 Local Election Impact System

After each local election cycle (annually or biennially), the GM determines outcomes for 5 domains that directly affect characters:

  1. School quality — affects characters with children; roll 2d10 and compare to school board composition
  2. Property taxes — affects housing costs for homeowners
  3. Policing policy — affects encounters with law enforcement
  4. Land use / zoning — affects housing availability, gentrification pressure
  5. Local services — parks, libraries, public transit, sanitation

Characters who voted in local elections gain a +1 bonus to any check related to those domains for the next year (they know what to expect and can plan accordingly).

2.4 Poll Worker Duties

Characters can volunteer as poll workers — a real civic role with actual responsibilities:

  • Time commitment: 7–10 hours on Election Day (plus 2–4 hours of training beforehand)
  • Compensation: $50–$150 per shift (varies by state)
  • Checks: 2d10 + PRC vs TN 10 to handle voter disputes calmly; 2d10 + INT vs TN 8 to troubleshoot voting machine issues
  • SP: +1 SP from dealing with frustrated voters and bureaucratic stress
  • Reward: Characters who serve as poll workers gain the Civic Servant trait: +1 to all civic engagement checks for the year
📝 Example: Elena's Poll Worker Experience

Elena (INT 4, PRC 5, EXP 3) volunteers as a poll worker at her neighborhood elementary school. She arrives at 6 AM for setup.

Morning: A voter shows up claiming they're registered, but the system says otherwise. Elena handles it with a 2d10 + PRC = 13 + 2 = 15 vs TN 10 → success. She issues a provisional ballot and de-escalates the situation. The voter is grateful.

Afternoon: The voting machine jams. Elena troubleshoots: 2d10 + INT = 9 + 1 = 10 vs TN 8 → success. She reboots the system and minimizes the line backup. The supervisor commends her.

End of day: Elena earns $100, gains +1 SP from the long day, and earns the Civic Servant trait. She feels a genuine sense of purpose — a rare Morale event in her otherwise stressful month.

2.5 Voter Registration Drives

Characters can organize or participate in voter registration drives — a form of grassroots civic engagement:

  • Organizing a drive: 2d10 + EXP vs TN 12. Success: you register 1d10 × 5 new voters. Failure: you register fewer (1d6 × 2) and gain +2 SP from the frustration.
  • Participating in a drive: No check needed; you contribute to the group total. Gain +1 SP from the effort but also +1 Morale from contributing to democracy.
  • Time cost: 4–8 hours per event day; planning takes 2–4 additional hours

📣 3. Protesting & Demonstrations

Protests are one of the most visible forms of political engagement — and one of the most mechanically complex. They involve planning, risk, social consequences, and uncertain outcomes. A protest in Reality RPG is a multi-stage event that can span multiple sessions.

3.1 Organizing a Protest

Organizing a protest is a multi-check project that takes 1–4 weeks of preparation:

PhaseCheckTNTimeFailure Effect
📋 Planning & permits2d10 + EXP103–7 daysPermit denied; protest must be unpermitted (+risk)
📢 Outreach & recruitment2d10 + PRC + EXP125–10 daysFewer attendees than expected (−50% turnout)

🤝 Coalition building2d10 + PRC141–2 weeksKey groups don't show up; weaker turnout

📏 Protest Size Modifier

After all planning checks, sum successes. Each success adds +1 to a final 1d10 roll that determines protest size:

  • 1–2: Small gathering (10–50 people)
  • 3–4: Moderate (50–200 people)
  • 5–6: Large (200–1,000 people)
  • 7–8: Major (1,000–5,000 people)
  • 9–10: Massive (5,000+ people; city-wide attention)

Protest size directly affects police response level, media coverage, and personal risk.

3.2 Participating in a Protest

Once a protest is underway, participants face ongoing risk:

3.3 Police Response & Counter-Protests

The GM determines police response based on protest size and type:

  • Small, permitted: Minimal police presence; observational only
  • Medium, permitted: Visible police line; possible use of barricades
  • Large or unpermitted: Active policing; possible tear gas, rubber bullets, mass arrests
  • Counter-protests: Roll 1d10 at the start of any protest with 200+ attendees. On 6–10, counter-protesters show up. This adds +1 SP to all participants and increases confrontation risk by +2 to all TNs.

3.4 Protest Effectiveness

Does the protest actually change anything? In Reality RPG, single protests rarely do — but they are building blocks:

📏 Protest Impact System

After a protest, the GM assigns Momentum Points toward the cause:

  • Small, peaceful: 1 Momentum Point
  • Medium, well-organized: 2 Momentum Points
  • Large, media-covered: 3–5 Momentum Points
  • Riot / clash: 1 Momentum Point (negative publicity often hurts the cause)

Accumulating 10+ Momentum Points on a single issue triggers a Policy Review event — officials must respond. Accumulating 20+ triggers actual policy change. This is the core mechanic behind Systemic Change Arcs (Section 8).

📝 Example: The Riverside Housing Protest

Setup: Aisha (INT 4, EXP 6, PRC 5, RES 4) is organizing a protest against a new luxury development displacing low-income families in Riverside. She has 3 weeks to plan.

Planning phase:

  • Permits: 2d10 + EXP = 14 + 3 = 17 vs TN 10 → success. City grants permit for downtown plaza.
  • Outreach: 2d10 + PRC + EXP = 12 + 2 + 3 = 17 vs TN 12 → success. Strong community buy-in.
  • Logistics: 2d10 + INT + DXT = 8 + 1 + 1 = 10 vs TN 10 → success (barely). Sound system works but signage is limited.
  • Coalitions: 2d10 + PRC = 11 + 2 = 13 vs TN 14 → fail. The tenant union doesn't show, weakening turnout.
  • Safety: 2d10 + INT = 15 + 1 = 16 vs TN 10 → success. De-escalation team is prepared.

Result: 4 successes → roll 1d10 + 4 = 7 + 4 = 11 → clamped to 10 → Massive protest (5,000+). Media covers it. Police are present but don't escalate. Counter-protesters show up (roll 8 on 1d10) but the de-escalation team prevents clashes.

Aftermath: Aisha gains +2 SP from the stress (now at 8/25). The protest earns 5 Momentum Points toward the housing justice cause. The developer announces they'll "reconsider" — a small win, but not a victory. Aisha knows she needs 15 more Momentum Points to force a real policy review.

3.5 Employer Retaliation

After a high-visibility protest, characters face real-world consequences:

  • Roll 1d10 if the character was photographed or named: on 1–3, their employer finds out.
  • If employer finds out: roll 1d10 on 1–2, they are fired; on 3–5, they receive a formal warning (−1 to all job security checks for 3 months); on 6–10, nothing happens.
  • Characters in unionized jobs gain +2 to resist employer retaliation.
  • Characters in at-will employment (most Americans) have no protection and face the full risk.

🏘️ 4. Community Organizing

Community organizing is the slow, unglamorous work of building power from the ground up. It is not a single event but a long-term commitment measured in months and years. This is where real systemic change begins.

4.1 Door-to-Door Canvassing

The bread and butter of organizing. Canvassing involves knocking on doors, talking to strangers, and hearing "no" 90% of the time:

ActivityCheckTNTimeSuccess EffectFailure Effect
🚪 Canvassing (per door)2d10 + PRC102–4 hrs/shiftGain 1 Support Point toward the causeNo gain; +0.5 SP per 10 rejections

✉️ Mailer campaign2d10 + INT111–3 days to prepareGain 2 Support Points per successful mailingPoor response rate; wasted resources
📏 Rejection Handling

Canvassing is emotionally exhausting. After each canvassing shift:

  • Roll 1d10. On 1–3, a resident is hostile or abusive. The character gains +2 SP and must make a 2d10 + RES vs TN 12 check or they need to take a break (ending the shift early with no Support Points).
  • Characters with the Thick Skin trait (EXP ≥ 5 in relevant field) reduce SP gain from rejection to +1.
  • Characters with Organizer Experience (3+ months of active organizing) gain +1 to all canvassing checks.

4.2 Community Meetings

Organizing requires regular community meetings — the lifeblood of grassroots movements:

  • Hosting a meeting: 2d10 + EXP + PRC vs TN 12. Success: good turnout (1d6 × 5 attendees), productive discussion, 2–3 new volunteers recruited.
  • Attending a meeting: No check required. Gain +1 Morale from community connection. May gain +1 EXP in relevant organizing skill per month of attendance.
  • SP cost: +1 SP per meeting for organizers (the emotional labor of holding space); +0 for attendees.
  • Time cost: 1.5–3 hours per meeting, plus travel time.

4.3 Building Coalitions

The hardest part of organizing: getting different groups to work together despite competing interests:

📏 Coalition Building Mechanics

Each coalition partner requires a separate 2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 14 check. The GM assigns a difficulty modifier based on how aligned the groups are:

  • Natural allies (same ideological family): TN reduced to 12
  • Uneasy allies (different priorities but shared goal): TN 14
  • Unlikely allies (different ideologies, forced coalition): TN 16
  • Adversaries turned partners (former opponents): TN 18 — but success grants double Support Points

A coalition with 3+ active partners gains coalition bonus: all organizing checks within the coalition gain +1. With 5+ partners, the bonus increases to +2.

4.4 The Slow Work

Organizing is measured in months and years, not sessions. Track progress on a Campaign Timeline:

MilestoneTime RequiredSupport Points NeededSP Cost to ReachCampaign Effect
🌱 Initial awareness1–2 months10 SP+5 SP cumulativeIssue is on community radar
📢 Public campaign3–6 months25 SP+10 SP cumulativeMedia attention; officials must respond
⚡ Policy pressure6–12 months50 SP+15 SP cumulativePolicy review triggered; hearings scheduled
🏛️ Policy change1–3 years100 SP+20 SP cumulativeActual legislation or policy adopted
🌍 Systemic shift3–10 years200 SP+30 SP cumulativeCultural/normative change; lasting impact
⚠️ Burnout Risk

Characters who accumulate more than 15 SP from organizing activities in a single month are at risk of activist burnout. The GM should offer a 2d10 + RES vs TN 14 check at the end of such a month. Failure means the character needs 1–3 months of reduced activity to recover. This is not a failure of character — it is a realistic modeling of how organizing actually works. The best organizers know when to rest.

📝 Example: Aisha's Tenant Union Campaign (Month 1)

Week 1: Aisha canvasses Riverside. 3 shifts of 3 hours each. 2d10 + PRC checks: 2 successes, 1 failure. She gains 2 Support Points. Week 1 rejection roll: a 7 — no hostility. SP: +2 from canvassing stress (now 10/25).

Week 2: Phone banking night. 2d10 + PRC = 14 + 2 = 16 vs TN 9 → success. Gains 3 Support Points. SP: +1 (now 11/25). Community meeting: +1 Morale event offsets the SP. Net SP: 10/25.

Week 3: Coalition building. Aisha tries to bring the local church and the tenant union together. 2d10 + PRC + EXP = 16 + 2 + 3 = 21 vs TN 14 → critical success. Both groups agree to co-sponsor. Coalition bonus unlocked (+1 to all checks).

Week 4: Big community meeting. 30 attendees. 5 new volunteers sign up. Aisha gains 4 Support Points from the event. SP: +1 (now 11/25). End of month total: 9 Support Points toward the 10 needed for Initial Awareness. One more successful activity and the campaign reaches its first milestone.

🏛️ 5. Running for Office

Running for political office is one of the most ambitious character goals in Reality RPG. It is a multi-session campaign arc that touches every other system in the game — finances, social dynamics, mental health, time management, and public reputation.

💡 GM Tip: Start Small

Encourage players to start with local offices. School board and city council are where real change happens, and they're mechanically achievable. A character running for Congress on their first campaign is like a level 1 character trying to solo the final boss — technically possible, but not fun for anyone.

5.2 Campaign Phases

A campaign unfolds in 4 overlapping phases, each with its own checks and risks:

Phase 1: Fundraising

  • Fundraising events: 2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 12 per event. Success: raise 1d6 × $500 (local office) to 1d6 × $5,000 (state/national). Failure: raise 1d4 × $100.
  • Donor cultivation: Each major donor requires a separate relationship-building arc (see Social Dynamics page). 2d10 + PRC vs TN 14 per donor.
  • Self-funding: Characters may use personal savings. This is a major financial risk — if they lose the election, that money is gone.
  • SP cost: +2 SP per fundraising event (the awkwardness of asking for money, the pressure of quotas)

Phase 2: Platform Development

  • Research: 2d10 + INT vs TN 12. Success: well-researched, defensible platform. Failure: platform has holes that opponents will exploit.
  • Community input: Hold town halls. 2d10 + PRC vs TN 10. Success: platform reflects community needs. Failure: platform misses key issues.
  • Platform coherence: 2d10 + INT + EXP vs TN 14. Success: platform is internally consistent and compelling. Failure: platform is contradictory (e.g., pro-business AND anti-tax without explaining revenue).

Phase 3: Campaign Execution

ActivityCheckTNEffect
🎤 Debate performance2d10 + PRC + EXP14Success: +2 Campaign Points; Fail: −1 Campaign Point
📰 Media interview2d10 + PRC + INT12Success: positive coverage; Fail: misquoted or taken out of context
🚪 Canvassing own campaign2d10 + PRC10Success: +1 Campaign Point per shift; Fail: no gain
📱 Social media management2d10 + INT11Success: growing online presence; Fail: engagement drops

Phase 4: Opposition Research & Scandal Management

  • Opposition research: 2d10 + INT vs TN 14. Success: discover opponent's vulnerabilities (gain +1 to debate and media checks against them).
  • Scandal hits: Roll 1d10 each month of campaign. On 1–2, a scandal emerges (old social media post, past controversy, gaff). The character must respond: 2d10 + PRC + RES vs TN 14. Success: manage the damage (−1 Campaign Point). Failure: major damage (−3 Campaign Points, possible campaign-ending).
  • SP cost of scandal: +3 SP minimum; +5 if the scandal is personal (family, health, past trauma exposed)

5.3 Winning & Losing

At election time, the GM compares Campaign Points accumulated by each candidate. The candidate with the most points wins. Ties go to the incumbent.

📏 Election Resolution

Final roll: 2d10 + Campaign Points + EXP vs a base TN set by the office level:

  • School Board: TN 10
  • City Council: TN 12
  • County: TN 14
  • State: TN 16
  • Congress: TN 18

Incumbents start with +3 Campaign Points (name recognition, established network). Open seats have no incumbent bonus.

📝 Example: Marcus Runs for City Council

Marcus (INT 5, EXP 4, PRC 4, RES 3) decides to run for City Council Ward 7. He has 6 months and $30,000 in savings.

Month 1–2 (Fundraising): 4 events. 3 successes, 1 failure. Raises $4,500 + $3,000 + $5,500 + $400 = $13,400. SP: +8 from fundraising stress (now 6/20). Uses $5,000 for campaign materials.

Month 3 (Platform): Research check: 2d10 + INT = 13 + 2 = 15 vs TN 12 → success. Town hall: 2d10 + PRC = 10 + 1 = 11 vs TN 10 → success. Platform focuses on affordable housing and small business support — resonates with Ward 7.

Month 4–5 (Campaign): Debate: 2d10 + PRC + EXP = 16 + 1 + 2 = 19 vs TN 14 → success (+2 Campaign Points). Media interview: 2d10 + PRC + INT = 8 + 1 + 2 = 11 vs TN 12 → fail. He's misquoted on a housing policy detail. Canvassing: 5 shifts, 4 successes (+4 Campaign Points). Social media: 2d10 + INT = 14 + 2 = 16 vs TN 11 → success. Endorsement from local NAACP: 2d10 + PRC + EXP = 15 + 1 + 2 = 18 vs TN 14 → success. Total Campaign Points: 7.

Month 6 (Scandal): Roll 1d10 = 2 — a scandal! An old tweet about a controversial topic resurfaces. Response check: 2d10 + PRC + RES = 12 + 1 + 0 = 13 vs TN 14 → fail. −3 Campaign Points. SP: +3 (now 14/20 — dangerously close to burnout). Campaign Points: 4.

Election Day: Final roll: 2d10 + 4 + 2 = 11 + 6 = 17 vs TN 12 → WIN. Marcus is elected City Councilor. The campaign cost him $15,000 of savings, 14 SP of stress, and 6 months of his life. He won — but the real work starts now.

5.4 Consequences of Office

  • Winning: Character gains the Elected Official trait. They can influence policy in their domain (see Section 6). Salary varies by office (school board: often unpaid; city council: $30K–$100K; state: $50K–$100K; Congress: $174K).
  • Losing: Character gains +3 SP from the loss. Campaign debt may persist. However, they gain the Campaign Veteran trait: +1 to all future political checks (they know how to campaign now).
  • Social consequences: Running for office makes a character public. Their social media is scrutinized, their past is researched, their family is affected. All social checks involving the character gain +1 difficulty during the campaign (people are wary of politicians).

🗣️ 6. Lobbying & Influence

Most policy change happens behind closed doors — not in protests or elections, but in the quiet work of building relationships with decision-makers and their staff. This section covers how characters can influence policy from the outside.

6.1 Levels of Influence

LevelMethodCheckTNReal ImpactTime

2 — ContactCalling a representative's office, attending a town hall2d10 + PRC10Low (staff takes notes; rarely acts)1–2 hours

💡 The Staffer Insight

In real politics, staffers do the actual work. Elected officials set direction, but their aides draft legislation, schedule meetings, and filter information. Characters who invest in building relationships with staffers will have more influence than characters who only try to reach the elected official. This is a key insight that separates experienced organizers from newcomers.

6.2 Writing to Representatives

The lowest-effort, lowest-impact form of advocacy. Still worth modeling because characters do this:

  • Handwritten letter: 2d10 + INT vs TN 10. Success: well-reasoned, cited arguments. The staffer actually reads it (rare!). Gain 1 Influence Point toward the issue.
  • Email: 2d10 + INT vs TN 12. Success: clear, concise argument. Gain 0.5 Influence Points.
  • Form letter / online petition: No check. Gain 0.1 Influence Points (the system is flooded with these; they rarely move the needle).
  • SP cost: +0.5 SP if the topic is personally important to the character (the frustration of knowing your voice is one of thousands)

6.3 Meeting with Officials

Once a character has built enough Influence Points (5+ on a specific issue), they can request a meeting:

  • Meeting request: 2d10 + PRC + Influence Points vs TN 14. Success: meeting scheduled within 2–4 weeks.
  • The meeting itself: 2d10 + PRC + INT + EXP vs TN 14. Success: official is genuinely persuaded (−2 TN on future checks for this issue). Failure: official gives platitudes and does nothing.
  • SP cost: +2 SP (navigating power dynamics is exhausting)
  • Preparation matters: Characters who spend 2+ hours preparing (researching the official's voting record, preparing data, anticipating counterarguments) gain +2 to the meeting check.
📝 Example: Aisha Meets with the City Councilor

Setup: After months of organizing (see Section 4), Aisha has accumulated 8 Influence Points on the affordable housing issue. She requests a meeting with City Councilor Ramirez.

Meeting request: 2d10 + PRC + 8 = 9 + 2 + 8 = 19 vs TN 14 → success. Ramirez's scheduler sets up a 20-minute meeting.

Preparation: Aisha spends 3 hours researching Ramirez's voting record on housing, preparing tenant displacement data, and drafting specific policy language. She gains +2 to the meeting check.

The meeting: 2d10 + PRC + INT + EXP + 2 prep = 13 + 2 + 1 + 3 + 2 = 21 vs TN 14 → critical success. Ramirez is genuinely moved. She agrees to co-sponsor a tenant protection ordinance. Aisha gains +5 Influence Points toward housing policy. SP: +2 (now at 13/25).

Aftermath: This is a real win. Not because the ordinance will pass tomorrow — it won't. But because a city councilor is now formally committed to the issue. The legislative process has begun. Aisha knows the next phase will be committee hearings, amendments, and negotiation. But this meeting was the turning point.

6.4 Symbolic Gestures vs. Real Influence

A key teaching in Reality RPG: not all political action is equally effective. Characters should understand the difference:

🎭 Symbolic Actions

Signing petitions, posting on social media, wearing a cause T-shirt, attending a one-off rally. These feel good and provide +1 Morale, but they generate negligible real-world impact. They are the political equivalent of hitting a punching bag — satisfying in the moment, but the bag is still standing afterward.

🔧 Real Influence

Showing up to committee meetings, building long-term relationships with staffers, voting in every local election, organizing sustained campaigns, running for office yourself. These are slow, unglamorous, and mechanically impactful. They generate Influence Points, Momentum Points, and actual policy change.

🔍 7. Misinformation & Media Literacy

In the modern information ecosystem, knowing what's true is an active skill — not a given. Characters must navigate fake news, deepfakes, algorithmic echo chambers, and the constant churn of outrage content.

7.1 Spotting Misinformation

When a character encounters a news story, claim, or viral post, they can attempt to verify it:

Source TypeVerification CheckTNTime RequiredFailure Effect
Mainstream news article2d10 + INT85–10 minutesMissed bias or framing
Social media post2d10 + INT1215–30 minutesShared misinformation; social embarrassment
Opinion piece / editorial2d10 + INT + EXP1010–20 minutesMistook opinion for fact
Deepfake / manipulated media2d10 + INT1630–60 minutesBelieved fabricated content; potential legal/social consequences
Foreign propaganda2d10 + INT + EXP181–2 hoursUnknowingly amplified foreign narrative
📏 Media Literacy Trait

Characters who invest in media literacy (through education, habit, or experience) can gain the Media Literate trait:

  • Requirement: INT ≥ 4 AND 3+ months of deliberate media literacy practice (fact-checking habits, understanding algorithmic bias, recognizing logical fallacies)
  • Benefit: −2 to all TNs for spotting misinformation; immune to echo chamber reinforcement (see below)
  • Maintenance: Requires ongoing practice. If the character stops actively fact-checking for 3+ months, the trait degrades to a −1 bonus.

7.2 Social Media Algorithm Effects

Every character who uses social media is subject to algorithmic influence:

  • Echo chamber reinforcement: Each month, roll 1d10. On 6–10, the algorithm pushes the character further toward ideological extremes. Characters with Intensity 3+ shift 1 step more extreme on the political spectrum. Characters with the Media Literate trait are immune.
  • Outrage engagement: The algorithm prioritizes content that triggers emotional responses. Each week, characters gain +0.5 SP from the cumulative effect of outrage content (round up at the end of each month).
  • Time sink: Characters spend 1–3 hours/day on social media. This is time not spent on other activities — including organizing, family time, or rest.

7.3 Doomscrolling

A specific and dangerous behavior: compulsive news consumption that directly harms mental health:

📏 Doomscrolling Mechanics

When a character doomscrolls (consuming negative news for 2+ hours without a break):

  • Gain +2 SP per session
  • Roll 2d10 + RES vs TN 12 at the end of each session. Failure: the character cannot stop and continues for another hour (another +2 SP).
  • Characters who doomscroll 3+ sessions per week develop News Anxiety: a persistent condition that adds +1 SP/day until they take a media detox (3+ days without consuming news).
  • Media detox: 2d10 + RES vs TN 14 to successfully go 3 days without news. Success: News Anxiety clears, and the character gains −2 SP from the break. Failure: the anxiety is too strong; they check their phone.

7.4 Deepfakes & Fabricated Content

An escalating threat. Characters may encounter deepfakes — AI-generated images, videos, or audio that appear real:

  • Encounter frequency: Roll 1d10 each month. On 1, the character encounters a deepfake related to a topic they care about.
  • Detection: 2d10 + INT vs TN 16. Success: character recognizes it as fake. Failure: they believe it and may share it.
  • Consequences of sharing a deepfake: If the character shares it publicly, they face social consequences when it's debunked. −2 to PRC-based checks for 1 month (reputation damage). If the deepfake is politically charged, add +3 SP from the social fallout.

🌊 8. Systemic Change Arcs

The crown jewel of Reality RPG political mechanics: long-term campaign arcs where characters work to change actual systems. These are multi-session storylines that span months or years of game time, with concrete milestones, setbacks, and victories.

8.1 Arc Structure

Every systemic change arc follows 5 phases:

PhaseNameDurationKey ChecksMilestone
1🌱 Problem Identification1–2 months2d10 + INT (research the issue)Clear problem statement; initial allies identified
2🤝 Coalition Building2–4 months2d10 + PRC + EXP (recruit partners)3+ active coalition partners; shared strategy
3📢 Public Campaign3–6 months2d10 + PRC (visibility); 2d10 + EXP (organizing)Media coverage; public awareness; official response required
4⚔️ Resistance & Setback1–3 months2d10 + RES (weather opposition); 2d10 + INT (adapt strategy)Survive the pushback; maintain coalition unity

8.2 Example Arcs

Arc 1: Ending Homelessness in Your City

📝 Arc Overview

Scope: City-wide. Duration: 2–5 years. Difficulty: Extreme.

Phase 1: Research homelessness data, identify root causes (housing costs, mental health access, employment barriers). 2d10 + INT vs TN 14.

Phase 2: Build coalition with homeless shelters, mental health advocates, housing developers, faith groups, and unhoused individuals themselves. 2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 16 (diverse coalition = harder to build).

Phase 3: Public campaign — "Housing First" model. Media events, personal stories, data presentations. 2d10 + PRC vs TN 14 per campaign action.

Phase 4: Opposition — NIMBY residents, property owners, fiscal conservatives. They organize counter-campaigns. 2d10 + RES vs TN 16 to maintain coalition unity. 2d10 + INT vs TN 14 to adapt strategy when setbacks hit (e.g., a shelter opening goes wrong).

Phase 5: Final push — city council vote on housing ordinance. 2d10 + PRC + EXP + INT vs TN 18. Success: ordinance passes. Partial success (meet TN −2): compromise ordinance passes (helps some, not all). Failure: ordinance defeated — but the campaign has built infrastructure for the next attempt.

SP cost across arc: 25–40 SP cumulative. Characters will need multiple recovery periods. This is intentional — the work is hard.

Arc 2: Reforming School Funding

Scope: District or state-wide. Duration: 1–3 years. Difficulty: Very Hard.

This arc focuses on the equity gap in school funding — where property-tax-based funding means wealthy districts have better schools. Key actions:

  • Research funding disparities (2d10 + INT vs TN 12)
  • Organize parents across district lines (2d10 + PRC vs TN 14)
  • Present at school board meetings (2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 12 per appearance)
  • Build relationship with state legislators (2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 16)
  • Final push: state budget vote (2d10 + all stats vs TN 18)

Arc 3: Fighting Gentrification

Scope: Neighborhood-level. Duration: 2–4 years. Difficulty: Hard.

This arc is about protecting existing communities from displacement. Key actions:

  • Tenant organizing (2d10 + PRC vs TN 12 per tenant recruited)
  • Community land trust formation (2d10 + INT + EXP vs TN 16)
  • Anti-displacement ordinance campaign (2d10 + PRC + EXP vs TN 14)
  • Legal challenges to development projects (2d10 + INT vs TN 16)
  • Final push: city council zoning vote (2d10 + PRC + EXP + INT vs TN 16)

Arc 4: Building Community Gardens

Scope: Neighborhood-level. Duration: 3–6 months. Difficulty: Medium.

A more accessible arc — great for new characters entering the politics system:

  • Find available land (2d10 + INT vs TN 10)
  • Get city permission (2d10 + PRC vs TN 12)
  • Recruit volunteers (2d10 + PRC vs TN 10 per recruitment drive)
  • Secure funding (2d10 + INT vs TN 12 for grants; 2d10 + PRC vs TN 10 for community donations)
  • Build and maintain (2d10 + DXT vs TN 10 for physical work; 2d10 + EXP vs TN 10 for garden management))
  • Reward: A functioning community garden provides +1 Morale per month to all neighborhood characters, and −1 SP/month for the organizer (the joy of growing things and building community).

Arc 5: Starting a Mutual Aid Network

Scope: Neighborhood to city-wide. Duration: 2–6 months to launch; ongoing. Difficulty: Medium.

Mutual aid networks are community-based systems of direct support — food distribution, rent assistance, childcare co-ops:

  • Assess community needs (2d10 + INT + PRC vs TN 12)
  • Recruit coordinators (2d10 + PRC vs TN 10 per coordinator)
  • Set up distribution systems (2d10 + INT + DXT vs TN 12)
  • Secure resources (2d10 + PRC vs TN 14 for donations; 2d10 + INT vs TN 12 for grants))
  • Maintain and scale (2d10 + EXP vs TN 12 per month of operation))
  • Reward: A successful mutual aid network provides +2 Morale to the organizer per month and +1 Morale to all community members served. It also provides a safety net: characters in the network gain −1 SP during crisis events (they know someone has their back).

📝 Example: Aisha's Full Arc — Tenant Protection Ordinance

Aisha (see character portrait above) has been organizing for 6 months. She enters Phase 3 of her housing justice arc.

Public Campaign (Month 4–6):

  • Media event: Aisha organizes a "displacement testimony" at City Hall. 2d10 + PRC = 16 + 2 = 18 vs TN 14 → success. Local news covers it. +3 Influence Points.
  • Data presentation: Aisha presents displacement data to the council. 2d10 + INT + EXP = 11 + 1 + 3 = 15 vs TN 14 → success. Councilor Ramirez commits to drafting the ordinance. +2 Influence Points.
  • Community rally: 200 attendees. 2d10 + PRC + EXP = 14 + 2 + 3 = 19 vs TN 14 → success. +4 Support Points.

Setback (Month 5): Opposition pushback — a property owners' group launches an ad campaign framing the ordinance as "anti-business." All TNs +2 for 1 month. Aisha's coalition wavers. 2d10 + RES = 12 + 1 = 13 vs TN 12 → success. Coalition holds, barely.

Final Push (Month 7): The ordinance goes to a city council vote. 2d10 + PRC + EXP + INT = 15 + 2 + 3 + 1 = 21 vs TN 16 → success. The Tenant Protection Ordinance passes 5–4. It includes rent stabilization, just-cause eviction protections, and a right-to-counsel provision.

Aftermath: Aisha gains the Change Agent trait. She's at 18/25 SP — close to burnout — but the Morale event from the victory is enormous. She takes 2 weeks off (−3 SP from rest). When she returns, she's already thinking about the next fight: enforcing the ordinance.

Real-world parallel: This mirrors real tenant protection campaigns in cities like Seattle, Minneapolis, and Chicago — where organizing took 1–3 years, faced fierce opposition, and ultimately passed through persistent grassroots pressure.

8.5 The GM's Role in Systemic Arcs

Systemic change arcs require active GM participation:

  • Play the opposition honestly. Systems resist change. Don't make it too easy — but don't make it impossible either. The best arcs have real tension where the outcome is uncertain until the final roll.
  • Track momentum across sessions. Use a visible tracker (whiteboard, shared document) so players can see their progress. Momentum is a motivator.
  • Model real-world complexity. Not every check has a clear pass/fail. Sometimes a "failure" produces a partial win with complications — which is more realistic and more interesting.
  • Connect arcs to other systems. A housing arc affects characters' living situations (Geography/Mobility). A school funding arc affects characters with children (Family/Children). A mutual aid arc affects characters' stress levels (Mental Health). Politics doesn't exist in a vacuum.
  • Allow failure to be meaningful. If a campaign fails, the character doesn't just "try again." They've spent real time, real money, and real emotional energy. The failure should have consequences — but also lessons. A failed campaign gives the character the Campaign Veteran trait and specific knowledge about what went wrong.
💡 Final GM Tip: Hope is a Mechanic

Reality RPG is about real life, and real life can be brutal. But hope is not naive — it is a discipline. Systemic change arcs should be difficult but achievable. Characters should feel the weight of the work and the genuine possibility of making a difference. The SP system models the cost; the arc system models the path. Together, they tell the story of what it actually takes to change the world — one meeting, one conversation, one vote at a time.
