Weapons & Violence

Self-defense, criminal weapons, legal consequences, and the trauma of violence. In Reality RPG, every act of force carries weight.

🛡️ 1. Weapons Framework

Weapons are not abstract game elements in Reality RPG. They are tools that exist in the real world, governed by real laws, producing real harm, and leaving real psychological scars. This chapter treats weapons with the seriousness they deserve.

⚠️ Safety Notice: This section covers real weapons, real violence, and real consequences. Players and GMs should discuss content boundaries before running scenes involving weapons. Violence in this game is not heroic — it is terrifying, consequential, and often tragic.

📋 Why Weapons Matter

In the real world, weapons intersect with nearly every aspect of life:

  • 🔒 Self-defense: Personal protection in dangerous situations
  • 🏛️ Military / Police careers: Professional weapon use with strict chains of command
  • 🎯 Sports: Shooting ranges, archery, competitive marksmanship
  • 🔪 Criminal activity: Armed robbery, gang violence, trafficking
  • 🏠 Home defense: Protecting household and property
  • 🎭 Roleplay depth: A character's relationship with weapons tells a story about their life, fears, and choices

⚖️ The Violence Consequence System

Every act of violence has three layers of consequences:

Layer Effect Mechanics
🩸 Physical Injury, disability, death Health Condition Track advancement; permanent injury roll for severe wounds
🧠 Psychological Trauma, PTSD, guilt, stress accumulation SP gain (see below); RES checks to process; trauma conditions
⚖️ Legal Arrest, charges, civil liability, license revocation GM applies jurisdiction-specific law table; possible campaign-defining consequences

🗣️ De-escalation as the Default

In Reality RPG, de-escalation is the first and best option. Before any violence, a character should attempt to talk their way out of danger:

📏 De-escalation Check: Roll 2d10 + EXP (Empathy / Negotiation skill) vs. TN set by the GM (45–65 depending on aggressor state). Success = the situation defuses. Failure = escalation. Critical failure (≤ 5) = the aggressor feels insulted and attacks immediately.

A character's RES also matters — a calm, centered person is better at de-escalation. Characters under high stress (SP ≥ 15) take a +5 penalty to de-escalation checks.

🪜 The Use-of-Force Ladder

Real-world law enforcement and self-defense doctrine follows a graduated response. Characters who escalate beyond what the situation warrants face legal consequences:

Level Action When Justified Legal Risk
1 🗣️ Verbal Warning Always None
2 ✋ Physical Restraint Against active physical threat Low (if proportional)
3 🏏 Impact Weapon Against armed or multiple attackers Medium
4 🔪 Blade Against lethal threat; last resort High
5 🔫 Firearm Imminent threat of death or great bodily harm Very High
💡 GM Note: If a character uses force at a level disproportionate to the threat, the GM should apply legal consequences immediately — even if the character "wins" the physical encounter. A good lawyer costs money; a bad outcome costs freedom.

💀 Lethality Ratings

Rating Health Track Advance Examples
🟢 Non-lethal 1 step (Minor → Hurt → Disabled) Fists, pepper spray, tasers, personal alarms
🟡 Potentially lethal 1–2 steps depending on target Baseball bat, knife, pipe, improvised weapons
🔴 Lethal 2 steps; can kill directly Firearms, shotguns, rifles, large blades
📖 Example — The Use-of-Force Ladder in Action:
Marcus (EXP +2, RES +1) is confronted by a drunk man shoving him at a bar. Marcus attempts de-escalation: he rolls 2d10 + EXP = 7 + 6 + 2 = 15 vs. TN 50. Failure. The man swings. Marcus steps back (flight), but the man follows. Marcus grabs a bottle (impact weapon, Level 3) — this is justified because the attacker is actively fighting. He strikes once, dropping the man. Aftermath: Marcus gains +5 SP from the violence. The bar calls police. Marcus gives a statement. Because he used proportional force against an active attacker, no charges are filed — but he still has to spend an evening at the station.

⚙️ Firearm Mechanics

📏 Firing: To fire a weapon, roll 2d10 + DXT + Firearm skill vs. TN set by GM. The TN depends on distance, lighting, target movement, and the shooter's proficiency level.

🔄 Firing Modes

ModeTN ModifierAmmo UseNotes
🎯 Single ShotNone1 roundMost accurate; default mode
💥 Burst (3-round)+5 to TN3 roundsDouble damage if hit; wasteful
🌊 Full Auto+10 to TN5–10 roundsTriple damage if hit; very inaccurate; wastes ammo

⚠️ Jamming

Each time a firearm is fired, roll 1d20. On a 1, the weapon jams. Clearing a jam requires a full action (one round). Novice shooters have a higher jam rate: roll 1d20 and jam on 1–2.

📏 Range Penalties

Beyond a weapon's effective range, the TN increases by +5 per 10 meters. Beyond double the effective range, hits are essentially impossible without Expert or Master proficiency.

🛡️ Cover and Concealment

Targets behind substantial cover (concrete, brick, thick tree trunk) gain +10 to the attacker's TN. Light cover (car doors, thin walls) grants +5. Concealment (hiding behind foliage, smoke) gives +5 to the defender's dodge roll.

💥 Missed Shots & Collateral Damage

When a shooter misses, the bullet doesn't vanish. The GM rolls 1d6 to determine where it goes:

  • 1–2: Bullet strikes a bystander or innocent object — roll for collateral damage
  • 3–4: Bullet ricochets — the GM may choose it hits someone else in the area
  • 5–6: Bullet goes wide harmlessly
⚠️ Realism Check: In an enclosed space (home, car, bar), missed shots are dangerous. Bullets penetrate walls, shatter windows, and can kill someone in the next room. The GM should remind players that firing a gun in a populated area is extremely risky for everyone.

📋 Concealed Carry Permit Process

To obtain a concealed carry permit, a character must:

  1. Complete a gun safety course (4–8 hours, $50–100) — 2d10 + INT vs. TN 40
  2. Pass a background check — automatic if no criminal record; 2d10 + INT vs. TN 50 if there are complications
  3. Pay permit fees ($50–150) and wait 2–8 weeks for processing

🚩 Red Flag Laws

In states with red flag laws, family members or law enforcement can petition a court to temporarily remove firearms from someone deemed a danger to themselves or others. A character facing a red flag order must make a RES check (TN 55) or suffer social and legal consequences.

📖 Example 2 — Range Training:
Sarah wants to get a concealed carry permit. She enrolls in a 6-hour safety course ($75). She rolls 2d10 + INT (INT +1) = 11 + 10 + 1 = 22 vs. TN 40. She passes the course. She then spends 15 hours at the range over 3 months, spending ~$150 on range fees and ~$60 on ammo. She reaches Competent proficiency. When she applies for her permit, she rolls 2d10 + INT = 14 + 10 + 1 = 25 vs. TN 50 — she passes the background check with no issues. After 6 weeks, she receives her permit.
📖 Example 3 — Defensive Firearm Use:
James (DXT +2, Competent with 9mm) is at home when someone breaks a window and enters. He grabs his 9mm from the safe. The intruder is 8 meters away, poorly lit hallway. TN = 45 (distance penalty + darkness). James rolls 2d10 + DXT + Competent = 8 + 12 + 10 = 30 vs. TN 45. Miss! He rolls 1d20 for jamming — rolls a 14, no jam. He rolls 1d6 for the missed shot — rolls a 1, the bullet hits a wall near the intruder, startling them. The intruder flees. Aftermath: James calls 911. Police arrive, investigate. James gains +10 SP from the encounter. He must file a report. His hand shakes for the rest of the day (−5 to all checks for 1 hour — adrenaline dump).

🔪 3. Bladed Weapons

Blades are intimate weapons. Unlike firearms, they require close proximity, making encounters deeply personal and terrifying. In Reality RPG, knife fights are brutal, short, and often end badly for both parties.

⚠️ Realism Check: Real knife fights are not like the movies. Both parties get cut. Even the winner is often hospitalized. A knife fight is a medical emergency regardless of who "wins."

⚙️ Blade Combat Mechanics

📏 Melee Attack: Roll 2d10 + DXT + Melee skill vs. TN set by GM (typically 40–50 based on target movement, environment, and number of opponents).

💥 Critical Hits

If the attacker rolls a 5 or lower on the 2d10 (a natural critical), the blade finds a vital area. The wound advances the Health Condition Track by 2 steps instead of 1. This represents a deep cut to an artery, organ, or major muscle group.

🤝 Disarming

An opponent can attempt to disarm the blade-wielder by making a DXT contest: both roll 2d10 + DXT. The higher total wins. If the defender wins, the attacker drops the weapon. If the attacker wins, the defender takes a wound and the attacker continues.

🔧 Blade Durability

Knives can dull (−5 to damage after 3+ strikes against hard surfaces). Axes can chip or break on impact — roll 1d20 after each strike against bone or metal; on a 1–2, the blade chips (−5 to TN for subsequent attacks).

📖 Example 4 — Knife Encounter:
David (DXT +1, Novice with blades) is mugged at night. The attacker pulls a knife. David grabs a broken bottle from the ground (improvised blade, TN 48). The attacker lunges — David must defend. He rolls 2d10 + DXT to dodge = 9 + 11 + 1 = 20 vs. TN 45. He fails — the attacker cuts his arm. David advances to "Hurt" on the Health Track. David retaliates with the bottle, rolling 2d10 + DXT = 6 + 11 + 1 = 18 vs. TN 48. Another miss. The attacker grabs David. David's friend arrives, shouting — the attacker drops the knife and runs. David has a deep laceration requiring 4–6 stitches. He gains +8 SP. The adrenaline dump gives him −5 to all checks for the next hour — he can barely drive home.

🏏 4. Impact Weapons

Impact weapons — clubs, bats, pipes, and improvised objects — deliver blunt force trauma. They are less immediately lethal than blades or firearms but can still cause severe injury, including broken bones, concussions, and internal bleeding.

🏏 Clubs & Bats

WeaponDamageTN (Melee)CostLegalityNotes

🔧 Pipe / Crowbar1–2 steps48Free (improvised)Legal to ownHeavy; awkward; can break
🔦 Tactical Flashlight1 step45$30–80Legal everywhereDual purpose; strobe mode blinds (TN 35 to resist)

🤜 Brass Knuckles

Brass knuckles are illegal in most US states and many countries. TN 42 melee, higher damage than bare fists (advances Health Track by 1–2 steps depending on strike location). Cost $10–30.

⚠️ Legal Danger: Simply possessing brass knuckles is a felony in many jurisdictions. Being found with them during an arrest — even for an unrelated offense — adds a separate charge with 1–5 years prison time.

⚙️ Impact Mechanics

🧠 Concussion Risk

Any strike to the head with an impact weapon risks concussion. The target must pass a VIG check (TN 60) or fall unconscious for 1–6 rounds. Even if they pass, they take −10 to all checks for the next 24 hours (brain fog, dizziness, nausea).

💔 Broken Weapons

Improvised weapons can break on impact. After striking with an improvised weapon (pipe, bottle, rock), roll 1d20. On a 1–3, the weapon shatters or breaks, leaving the attacker unarmed.

📖 Example 5 — Impact Weapon Self-Defense:
Tom (DXT +0, Novice) is walking home and two men approach him aggressively, blocking his path. He tries to de-escalate: 2d10 + EXP = 13 + 8 + 0 = 21 vs. TN 55. Failure — the men don't back down. He tries to run: 2d10 + VIG (Athletics) = 8 + 9 + 0 = 17 vs. TN 40. Failure — they grab his jacket. One pulls a knife. Now Tom is justified in using force. He grabs a heavy rock from the ground (improvised impact weapon, TN 48). He swings at the knife-wielder's arm. 2d10 + DXT = 7 + 10 + 0 = 17 vs. TN 48. Miss! He rolls 1d20 for breakage — rolls an 8, the rock holds. The second attacker punches Tom in the face. Tom fails his VIG check (TN 60) and falls unconscious. Aftermath: Tom wakes up in a hospital with a concussion and a broken nose. The attackers stole his wallet and phone. He gains +12 SP. The concussion gives him −10 to all checks for 24 hours. He files a police report but has no idea what the attackers look like beyond "two men."

🧴 5. Defensive & Spray Devices

Non-lethal defensive tools are the most socially and legally acceptable form of self-defense. They are designed to create distance and time to escape, not to "win a fight."

DeviceTN to HitEffectDurationCostLegality
🧴 Pepper Spray / OC Spray 45 (3m) / 60 (6m) VIG check TN 50 or disabled 1–3 minutes $10–25 Legal in most places; size restrictions in some states
⚡ Taser / Stun Gun 40 (5m) Electric shock incapacitates 15 seconds (one use) $150–400; reload $15–20 Banned in some states/countries
🚨 Personal Alarm No TN needed Loud noise (130 dB); draws attention Until turned off or battery dies $5–15 Legal everywhere
💡 Best Practice: The purpose of pepper spray and tasers is not to fight — it's to create a window to run away. After deploying a defensive device, the character should attempt flight (Athletics check, TN 40) rather than continuing the confrontation.
📖 Example 6 — Pepper Spray Defense:
Lisa (DXT +1) is walking to her car at night when a man approaches her aggressively, asking for money and getting closer. She tries to de-escalate (2d10 + EXP = 10 + 9 + 1 = 20 vs. TN 50 — failure). She pulls out her pepper spray and sprays him at 3 meters. 2d10 + DXT = 6 + 11 + 1 = 18 vs. TN 45. Miss — the wind blows it back partially. She gets some in her own eyes (−5 to checks for 2 minutes). She tries again at 2 meters: 2d10 + DXT = 9 + 10 + 1 = 20 vs. TN 40. Hit! The man must make a VIG check TN 50 — he rolls 2d10 + VIG = 12 + 8 = 20. Failure — he's blinded and writhing. Lisa runs to her car and locks the doors. She calls 911. The man recovers after about 2 minutes but has given up. Lisa gains +5 SP. No legal consequences because pepper spray is legal and proportionate.

🛡️ 6. Self-Defense Encounters

Self-defense encounters are the most emotionally intense situations in Reality RPG. They follow a structured resolution to ensure players consider all options before resorting to violence.

📋 Encounter Resolution Steps

StepActionMechanicOutcome
1️⃣ Awareness EXP + Empathy check (TN 40) Success = notice threat early, gain 1 extra round to act
2️⃣ De-escalation Communication check (TN 45–65) Success = threat defused; Failure = escalation
3️⃣ Flight Athletics check (TN 40) Success = escape; Failure = pursued or cornered
4️⃣ Physical Confrontation Combat resolution (see below) Health Track advancement for both parties
5️⃣ Weapon Use Weapon-specific checks Last resort; maximum consequences

⚔️ Combat Resolution

📏 Combat Rounds: Each round = 6 seconds. In each round, a character can perform one major action (attack, defend, move, use an item).
  • Attack: Roll 2d10 + DXT + Combat skill vs. TN (GM-set, 35–60 based on conditions)
  • Defense: Opponent rolls 2d10 + DXT to dodge or block
  • Hit: Advances target on Health Condition Track by the weapon's damage rating
  • Every violent encounter: Both parties gain SP based on severity

⚖️ Legal Aftermath of Self-Defense

CircumstanceLegal OutcomeSP Gain
✅ Proportional force against active threat Justified — no charges (usually) +5–8 SP
⚠️ Proportional force but questionable timing Investigation; possible charges; lawyer needed ($2000–10000+) +8–12 SP
❌ Excessive force (attacker already down) Criminal charges likely; 2–15 years depending on outcome +10–15 SP
❌ Force against non-threatening person Assault charges; potential civil suit; prison time +12–20 SP
💀 Killing in self-defense (justified) Mandatory investigation; possible charges even if justified +15 SP minimum; RES check TN 60 or gain trauma
📏 Stand Your Ground vs. Duty to Retreat: In "Stand Your Ground" states, a character has no legal duty to retreat before using force. In "Duty to Retreat" jurisdictions, the character must attempt to flee before using deadly force, or they face criminal charges regardless of the outcome. The GM should determine the jurisdiction's law before the scene.

🧠 Psychological Impact

EventSP GainAdditional Effect
Being threatened (verbal) +2–3 SP None
Being physically attacked +5–8 SP −5 to checks for 1 hour (adrenaline dump)
Striking someone +3–5 SP −5 to checks for 1 hour (adrenaline dump)
Shooting someone (non-lethal) +10 SP minimum PTSD trigger possible; RES check TN 55
Killing someone (any means) +15 SP Mandatory RES check TN 60 or gain Trauma condition
Witnessing someone else killed +10–15 SP RES check TN 50 or gain Trauma condition
⚠️ The Adrenaline Dump: After any violent encounter, characters experience an adrenaline crash. For 1 hour after the encounter ends, they suffer −5 to all skill checks. They may experience shaking hands, nausea, confusion, emotional volatility, and difficulty concentrating. This is real — even professional soldiers experience this.

🔒 7. Criminal Use of Weapons

Weapons in criminal contexts dramatically escalate the stakes. What might be a misdemeanor becomes a felony. What might be a slap on the wrist becomes decades in prison. Reality RPG does not glorify armed crime — it portrays the brutal reality of what happens when weapons enter criminal encounters.

⚠️ Content Warning: This section covers criminal violence, gang activity, and legal consequences. GMs should warn players before running criminal weapon scenarios. This is not a guidebook for actual crime.

💰 Armed Crime Mechanics

  • Robbery with weapon: The Communication check to intimidate gets +5 when a weapon is visible, but the crime is automatically classified as armed robbery — a felony.
  • Intimidation without display: A character can threaten without showing a weapon (TN 40 Communication check). If discovered, the charge escalates to armed robbery.
  • Gang violence: Group combat follows the same rules as individual combat, but each participant acts on their own turn. Being part of a gang provides social benefits but creates legal entanglements that follow the character throughout the campaign.
  • Weapons trafficking: Dealing in illegal weapons is a high-risk, high-reward criminal career path. Each transaction carries the risk of police sting operations (GM rolls 1d20 per deal; on a 1, law enforcement intervenes).

⚖️ Criminal Charges Table

ChargeSeverityPrison SentenceAdditional Consequences

💰 Armed Robbery Felony 10–20+ years Fine up to $25,000; victim restitution
👊 Assault with Deadly Weapon Felony 2–10 years Restraining orders; civil liability

🚔 Police Encounters

📏 Stopped While Armed: If a character is stopped by police while carrying a weapon (especially concealed), the encounter escalates immediately. The officer may draw their weapon. Both parties must make a RES check (TN 50). Failure by the citizen = they move suddenly, causing the officer to fire. Failure by the officer = they misjudge the situation, causing unnecessary escalation.

The "split-second decision problem" is a real phenomenon: police officers have approximately 1.5 seconds to decide whether a reaching motion is for a wallet or a weapon. In Reality RPG, this is modeled by requiring immediate RES checks when a weapon is visible during a police stop.

📖 Example 7 — Police Encounter:
Carlos (RES +1) is pulled over for a broken taillight. He has a legally concealed 9mm in his trunk. He tells the officer he has a firearm. The officer asks him to step out of the car. Carlos reaches into his pocket for his wallet. The officer's heart rate spikes — he draws his weapon. Carlos must make a RES check TN 50: 2d10 + RES = 11 + 10 + 5 = 26 vs. TN 50. Failure — Carlos freezes for a half-second, which looks suspicious. The officer yells "Hands! Now!" Carlos slowly raises his hands. The situation de-escalates after 30 tense seconds. Carlos gains +3 SP from the encounter. He receives a $150 ticket for the taillight. His heart rate won't come down for an hour.

✈️ Travel with Firearms

📏 Airline Rules: Firearms must be declared at check-in. The firearm must be unloaded, locked in a hard-sided case, and placed in checked baggage. Ammunition must be in its original packaging or a separate ammo container. Failure to declare a firearm in checked baggage = a $500–1000 fine from TSA, potential criminal charges, and possible no-fly list placement.

Crossing state lines: Laws vary dramatically. A firearm legal in the home state may be illegal in the destination state. Characters traveling with firearms should research destination laws or face criminal charges. The GM may require an INT check (TN 50) to correctly research and comply with all applicable laws.

🚗 Vehicle Storage

Many states allow firearms in vehicles without a concealed carry permit, but the rules vary:

  • Gun locks / trunk mounts: $10–50; required in some states for vehicles
  • Glove box safes: $20–60; convenient but visible to thieves
  • Under-seat holsters: $15–40; quick access but less secure

🏡 Home Defense Setup

A responsible gun owner must balance two competing priorities: accessibility (for defense) and security (preventing unauthorized access, especially by children). The GM should consider:

  • Quick-access safes near the bedroom (biometric, 2–3 second access)
  • Child-proof storage for long guns (mounted racks with locks)
  • Ammunition stored separately from firearms (additional security layer)
  • A home defense plan: where to retreat, where to call 911 from, what to say to police
⚠️ Real-World Stat: According to the CDC, homes with firearms have a 3× higher risk of suicide and a 5× higher risk of accidental death involving a child. In Reality RPG, a GM may roll 1d20 at the start of each year for a character with firearms in the home and children present. On a 1, an accident occurs — the GM and player work together to determine the nature and severity of the incident. This is not punitive — it reflects real-world risk.

1 💢 Minor Scratches, bruises, minor cuts −5 to DXT checks
2 🤕 Hurt Deep cuts, broken nose, sprains −10 to DXT checks; −5 to all checks
3 🏥 Wounded Broken bones, serious lacerations, internal bleeding −15 to all checks; requires medical attention
4 🚑 Critical Life-threatening injuries; organ damage; severe blood loss Cannot act; VIG check TN 65 per hour or die
5 💀 Dead Deceased Game over. See Death & Grief chapter.
📏 Recovery: Minor injuries heal in 1–3 days (−1 step). Hurt injuries require first aid and heal in 3–7 days. Wounded injuries require hospital care and heal in 1–4 weeks. Critical injuries require surgery and heal in 1–6 months. Characters at Wounded or worse may suffer permanent injuries (roll 1d20; on 1–5, lose 1 point from a physical attribute permanently).
⚠️ A Final Note on Violence in Reality RPG:

Violence is not a solution — it is a failure. In the real world, people who use violence are changed by it. They carry the weight of what they've done. Their relationships suffer. Their mental health deteriorates. Their legal entanglements follow them for years, sometimes decades.

This chapter exists not to make violence "fun" but to portray it honestly. When a player character picks up a weapon, the GM should look them in the eye and say: "Are you sure? Because this changes everything."

The best self-defense is awareness. The best awareness is de-escalation. The best de-escalation is not being in a dangerous situation at all.
